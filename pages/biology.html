Cell Cycle and Division

Interphase Phases

G₁ phase (Gap 1): Cell grows; organelle synthesis; metabolically active; prepares for DNA synthesis. Longest phase in rapidly dividing cells. Restriction point (R point) — commitment to divide.

S phase (Synthesis): DNA replication. DNA amount doubles (2C → 4C). Histones synthesized. Chromosome number unchanged (still 2n, but each chromosome now has 2 sister chromatids).

G₂ phase (Gap 2): Cell grows further; checks for DNA replication errors; synthesizes proteins needed for mitosis (tubulin for spindle). G₂/M checkpoint.

G₀ phase: Cells exit the cycle and enter quiescence. Neurons, cardiac muscle cells remain in G₀ permanently (non-dividing).

Proteins and Enzymes

Enzyme Kinetics (Michaelis-Menten):
Km (Michaelis constant) = substrate concentration at half-maximum velocity (Vmax/2).
Low Km = high affinity for substrate; High Km = low affinity.
Vmax = maximum reaction velocity (when all enzyme active sites are saturated).

Lock and Key model: Rigid active site perfectly fits specific substrate.
Induced Fit model: Active site is flexible and changes shape to fit substrate upon binding (more accurate).

Factors affecting enzyme activity: Temperature (optimum ~37°C for human enzymes; denaturation above 40–50°C), pH (optimum varies: pepsin pH 2, salivary amylase pH 7, trypsin pH 8), substrate concentration (increases rate until Vmax).

Inhibition types: Competitive inhibition (inhibitor resembles substrate, blocks active site; increase substrate to overcome); Non-competitive inhibition (inhibitor binds allosteric site; reduces Vmax; cannot be overcome by increasing substrate).

2. Genetics & Molecular Biology

Mendel's Laws

  • Law of Segregation (1st Law): During gamete formation, the two alleles of a gene separate (segregate) so each gamete carries only one allele. Demonstrated in monohybrid crosses.
  • Law of Independent Assortment (2nd Law): Alleles of different genes assort independently of one another during gamete formation — applies only to genes on different (non-homologous) chromosomes or far apart on same chromosome. Demonstrated in dihybrid crosses.

Molecular Genetics

DNA Structure

Watson-Crick Double Helix (1953):
• Two antiparallel polynucleotide strands wound around each other (right-handed helix)
• Backbone: alternating sugar (deoxyribose) — phosphate on the outside
• Bases on the inside: A pairs with T (2 hydrogen bonds); G pairs with C (3 hydrogen bonds)
• Chargaff's Rules: %A = %T; %G = %C; therefore %A+%G = %T+%C = 50%
• Helix diameter: 2 nm; pitch (full turn): 3.4 nm; base pairs per turn: 10
• 5' → 3' direction: phosphodiester bonds connect 3'-OH of one nucleotide to 5'-phosphate of next

DNA Replication (Semi-Conservative — Meselson-Stahl Experiment, 1958)

Lagging strand: Synthesized discontinuously in the direction away from the fork; Okazaki fragments (100–200 nucleotides in eukaryotes) are later joined by ligase.

Transcription

Steps: Initiation (RNA polymerase binds promoter) → Elongation (synthesis of mRNA in 5'→3' direction using template strand read 3'→5') → Termination (terminator sequence reached).

Prokaryotes: Sigma (σ) factor helps RNA polymerase recognize the −10 (Pribnow box) and −35 sequences of the promoter. No nuclear envelope; translation begins while transcription is still ongoing (coupled).

Eukaryotes — mRNA processing (pre-mRNA → mature mRNA):
1. 5' capping: Addition of 7-methylguanosine (m⁷G) cap → protects mRNA, aids ribosome recognition
2. 3' polyadenylation: Addition of poly-A tail (50–250 adenine nucleotides) → stability, nuclear export
3. Splicing: Introns (non-coding) removed; exons (coding) joined by spliceosomes (snRNPs)

Translation (Protein Synthesis)

Ribosome structure:
Prokaryotes: 70S = 50S large subunit (23S + 5S rRNA) + 30S small subunit (16S rRNA)
Eukaryotes: 80S = 60S large subunit (28S + 5.8S + 5S rRNA) + 40S small subunit (18S rRNA)
Mitochondrial ribosomes: 70S (similar to prokaryotes — endosymbiotic theory)

Genetic code features:
• 61 sense codons (code for 20 amino acids) + 3 stop codons (UAA, UAG, UGA = amber, ochre, opal)
• Start codon: AUG (codes for methionine in eukaryotes; formylmethionine in prokaryotes)
• Degenerate: most amino acids encoded by multiple codons (synonymous codons)
• Unambiguous: each codon specifies only one amino acid
• Nearly Universal: same code in almost all organisms

Steps: Initiation (ribosome assembles at start codon AUG) → Elongation (tRNA anticodon matches codon; peptide bond forms; ribosome translocates) → Termination (stop codon recognized by release factor; polypeptide released).

Absorption in Small Intestine

Structural adaptations for maximum absorption:
Villi (finger-like projections) → Microvilli (on each villus cell = brush border) → massively increase surface area (estimated 200–300 m² in adult human).

Monosaccharides: Absorbed by active transport (co-transport with Na⁺) or facilitated diffusion → portal vein → liver.
Amino acids: Active transport co-transport with Na⁺ → portal vein → liver.
Fatty acids + Monoglycerides: Diffuse into epithelial cells → reassembled into triglycerides → packaged into chylomicrons (with apolipoprotein B-48) → enter lacteals (lymphatic capillaries) → thoracic duct → blood (bypass liver initially).
Fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K): Absorbed with fats via chylomicrons.
Water-soluble vitamins (B, C): Absorbed into portal blood directly.

Gas Transport in Blood

O₂ transport: ~97% as oxyhaemoglobin (HbO₂) in RBCs; ~3% dissolved in plasma.
Hb + 4O₂ ⇌ HbO₈ (oxyhaemoglobin) — cooperativity; sigmoidal O₂ dissociation curve.
Bohr effect: ↑CO₂, ↑H⁺, ↑temperature → right shift (lower O₂ affinity → O₂ released to tissues).

CO₂ transport:
~70% as bicarbonate ion (HCO₃⁻) in plasma: CO₂ + H₂O ⇌ H₂CO₃ ⇌ H⁺ + HCO₃⁻ (carbonic anhydrase in RBCs)
~23% as carbaminohaemoglobin (CO₂ binds to Hb protein, not heme): Hb + CO₂ ⇌ HbCO₂
~7% dissolved in plasma.

Nervous System

Action Potential Steps

Resting membrane potential: −70 mV (inside negative; maintained by Na⁺/K⁺ ATPase pump — pumps 3 Na⁺ out, 2 K⁺ in).

1. Depolarization: Stimulus → voltage-gated Na⁺ channels open → Na⁺ rushes in → membrane potential rises to +30 mV (threshold = −55 mV).
2. Repolarization: Na⁺ channels inactivate; voltage-gated K⁺ channels open → K⁺ rushes out → membrane potential falls.
3. Hyperpolarization (undershoot): K⁺ channels slow to close → potential falls below −70 mV briefly (to ~−80 mV).
4. Refractory period: Absolute refractory (no new AP possible) → Relative refractory (AP possible with stronger stimulus).
5. Resting potential restored by Na⁺/K⁺ pump.

4. Plant Physiology

Photosynthesis

Light Reactions (Z-scheme, at Thylakoid Membrane)

Photosystem II (PSII): Absorbs light at 680 nm (P680). Water splitting (photolysis): 2H₂O → 4H⁺ + 4e⁻ + O₂. Electrons passed to electron transport chain.

Electron Transport Chain: Electrons flow through plastoquinone (PQ) → Cytochrome b₆f complex → Plastocyanin (PC). Proton gradient drives ATP synthesis via ATP synthase (photophosphorylation — noncyclic).

Photosystem I (PSI): Absorbs light at 700 nm (P700). Accepts electrons from plastocyanin. Reduces NADP⁺ to NADPH via ferredoxin (Fd) and ferredoxin-NADP⁺ reductase (FNR).

Net equation for light reactions:
12H₂O + 12NADP⁺ + 18ADP + 18Pᵢ + light → 6O₂ + 12NADPH + 18ATP

Calvin Cycle (Dark Reactions / Light-Independent Reactions, in Stroma)

Phase 1 — CO₂ Fixation: CO₂ + RuBP (5C, ribulose-1,5-bisphosphate) → 2 × 3-PGA (3C, 3-phosphoglycerate). Enzyme: RuBisCO (most abundant enzyme on Earth).

Phase 2 — Reduction: 3-PGA + ATP + NADPH → G3P (glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate, 3C). Uses ATP and NADPH from light reactions.

Phase 3 — Regeneration of RuBP: 5 G3P + 3 ATP → 3 RuBP (to accept more CO₂).

Net per turn: 3 CO₂ → 1 G3P (extra G3P = net gain). Requires 2 turns for 1 full G3P net. 6 turns for 1 glucose molecule (6 CO₂ fixed).

Overall summary: 6CO₂ + 18ATP + 12NADPH + 12H₂O → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 18ADP + 18Pᵢ + 12NADP⁺ + 6H₂O

Respiration in Plants and Animals

Glycolysis (in Cytoplasm — Anaerobic/Aerobic)

Glucose (6C) → 2 Pyruvate (3C)
Net gain: 2 ATP (substrate-level) + 2 NADH + 2 pyruvate
Steps: Glucose → G6P → F6P → F1,6-BP → 2 G3P → 2 1,3-BPG → 2 3-PG → 2 2-PG → 2 PEP → 2 Pyruvate

Pyruvate Oxidation (Mitochondrial Matrix, Aerobic Only)

Pyruvate → Acetyl-CoA + CO₂ + NADH (per pyruvate; ×2 from glucose)
Enzyme: Pyruvate dehydrogenase complex
Net: 2 pyruvate → 2 Acetyl-CoA + 2 CO₂ + 2 NADH

Krebs Cycle / Citric Acid Cycle (Matrix, per Acetyl-CoA turn)

Acetyl-CoA (2C) + Oxaloacetate (4C) → Citrate (6C) → Isocitrate → α-Ketoglutarate → Succinyl-CoA → Succinate → Fumarate → Malate → Oxaloacetate

Per turn: 3 NADH + 1 FADH₂ + 1 GTP (≈ 1 ATP) + 2 CO₂
For 1 glucose (2 turns): 6 NADH + 2 FADH₂ + 2 GTP + 4 CO₂

5. Ecology

Food Chains, Food Webs, Ecological Pyramids

Food chain: Producer → Primary consumer → Secondary consumer → Tertiary consumer
Food web: Interconnected network of food chains; more realistic representation of energy flow.

Ecological Pyramids:
• Pyramid of Numbers: number of organisms at each trophic level (can be inverted, e.g., tree-insect-bird)
• Pyramid of Biomass: total dry mass at each level (inverted in aquatic ecosystems — phytoplankton biomass < zooplankton)
• Pyramid of Energy: energy flow (always upright — energy lost at each level; never inverted)

10% Law (Lindeman's Law, 1942): Only ~10% of energy at one trophic level is transferred to the next. ~90% is lost as heat, movement, excretion, respiration. Maximum trophic levels possible: ~4–5.

Population Ecology

Exponential (J-shaped) growth: dN/dt = rN
N(t) = N₀ × eʳᵗ   where r = intrinsic rate of natural increase
Occurs when resources are unlimited; population grows without bound.

Logistic (S-shaped / Sigmoidal) growth: dN/dt = rN × (K−N)/K
K = carrying capacity (maximum sustainable population size for that environment)
Population grows slowly, then faster, then slows as it approaches K.

Species interactions:
Mutualism (+/+): both benefit (e.g., mycorrhizae — fungi + plant roots)
Commensalism (+/0): one benefits, other unaffected (e.g., barnacles on whale)
Parasitism (+/−): parasite benefits, host harmed (e.g., Plasmodium in humans)
Predation (+/−): predator benefits, prey harmed
Competition (−/−): both harmed (interspecific or intraspecific)

Biodiversity and Conservation

Types of biodiversity:
• Alpha diversity: diversity within a single habitat/community (species richness of a forest)
• Beta diversity: diversity between different habitats (turnover of species between two forests)
• Gamma diversity: total diversity across an entire region or landscape

Biodiversity hotspots (34 worldwide): Areas with exceptionally high species richness AND high degree of endemism (species found nowhere else), but under significant threat.
India's 4 biodiversity hotspots: Eastern Himalayas (part of Himalaya hotspot), Western Ghats and Sri Lanka, Indo-Burma (northeastern India), Sundaland (includes Nicobar Islands)

In-situ conservation (in the wild): National Parks (no human activity allowed), Wildlife Sanctuaries (some human activities permitted), Biosphere Reserves (core + buffer + transition zones), Sacred groves, Marine protected areas.

Ex-situ conservation (outside natural habitat): Zoological parks (zoos), Botanical gardens, Seed banks (e.g., Svalbard Global Seed Vault), Gene banks, Cryopreservation (−196°C in liquid N₂), in vitro fertilization, tissue culture propagation.

6. Biotechnology

Recombinant DNA Technology

Steps in rDNA technology:
1. Identification and isolation of the gene of interest (e.g., human insulin gene)
2. Cut DNA at specific sites using restriction endonucleases (molecular scissors)
3. Insert gene into a suitable vector (plasmid, phage, cosmid, BAC)
4. Introduce recombinant vector into host cell (transformation: CaCl₂/heat shock; electroporation; biolistics)
5. Screen and select recombinant cells (antibiotic resistance markers, blue-white screening using lacZ gene and X-gal)
6. Clone and amplify; express the desired protein

PCR (Polymerase Chain Reaction)

Developed by: Kary Mullis (1983); Nobel Prize Chemistry 1993.

Steps per cycle:
1. Denaturation: 94–95°C for 30 s → double-stranded DNA separates into single strands
2. Annealing: 50–65°C for 30 s → primers (short oligonucleotides) anneal to complementary sequences on template strands
3. Extension (Elongation): 72°C for 1 min/kb → Taq polymerase extends primers in 5'→3' direction

After n cycles: 2ⁿ copies. 30 cycles → ~10⁹ copies from a single DNA molecule.

Components required: Template DNA, Primers (2, forward and reverse), Taq DNA polymerase (thermostable, from Thermus aquaticus), dNTPs (dATP, dCTP, dGTP, dTTP), MgCl₂ (cofactor), Buffer.

Applications: Medical diagnosis (COVID-19 RT-PCR, HIV), Forensic analysis (DNA fingerprinting), Cloning/genetic engineering, Genetic disease screening, Evolutionary studies, Paternity testing.

10. Practice MCQs with Explanations

Q1. Which of the following organelles is responsible for the synthesis of proteins destined for export outside the cell?
(A) Smooth ER    (B) Rough ER    (C) Lysosome    (D) Peroxisome
Answer: (B) Rough ER
Explanation: Rough ER (studded with ribosomes) synthesizes proteins that will be secreted from the cell (secretory proteins) or become membrane proteins. After synthesis, they are transported to the Golgi apparatus for further processing, packaging, and export. Smooth ER synthesizes lipids; lysosomes digest materials; peroxisomes oxidize fatty acids and metabolize H₂O₂.
Q2. In a monohybrid cross, tall (T) is dominant over dwarf (t). If a heterozygous tall plant (Tt) is crossed with a dwarf plant (tt), what will be the phenotype ratio of the offspring?
(A) 3 Tall : 1 Dwarf    (B) 1 Tall : 1 Dwarf    (C) All Tall    (D) 1 TT : 2 Tt : 1 tt
Answer: (B) 1 Tall : 1 Dwarf
Explanation: This is a test cross (Tt × tt). Gametes from Tt: T and t. Gametes from tt: only t. Offspring: Tt (Tall) and tt (Dwarf) in equal proportions. Phenotype ratio = 1:1 Tall:Dwarf. Genotype ratio also 1:1 (Tt:tt). Test crosses are used to determine if an individual is homozygous or heterozygous dominant.
Q3. Which enzyme is responsible for removing RNA primers during DNA replication in E. coli?
(A) DNA Polymerase III    (B) Helicase    (C) DNA Polymerase I    (D) DNA Ligase
Answer: (C) DNA Polymerase I
Explanation: DNA Polymerase I has both 5'→3' polymerase activity AND 5'→3' exonuclease activity. It removes RNA primers left by primase and simultaneously replaces them with DNA nucleotides by nick translation. After the primer is removed and replaced with DNA, DNA Ligase seals the nick (gap) between adjacent Okazaki fragments. DNA Pol III is the main synthesis enzyme; helicase unwinds the double helix.
Q4. The 10% law in ecology was proposed by which scientist, and what does it imply?
(A) Lindeman (1942) — only 10% of energy at one trophic level passes to the next
(B) Darwin (1859) — only 10% of species survive competition
(C) Odum (1983) — 10% of biomass is decomposed each year
(D) Lotka (1925) — 10% of predators control 90% of prey
Answer: (A) Lindeman (1942)
Explanation: Raymond Lindeman's 10% law states that only approximately 10% of the energy stored at one trophic level is transferred to the next higher trophic level. The remaining ~90% is lost as heat during metabolic processes, used for growth and reproduction, or excreted. This limits the number of trophic levels to about 4–5 in any ecosystem, and explains why large carnivores are always relatively rare.
Q5. Which of the following is the correct sequence of events in PCR (Polymerase Chain Reaction)?
(A) Annealing → Denaturation → Extension
(B) Denaturation → Annealing → Extension
(C) Extension → Denaturation → Annealing
(D) Denaturation → Extension → Annealing
Answer: (B) Denaturation → Annealing → Extension
Explanation: PCR cycle has 3 steps: (1) Denaturation at 94–95°C — double-stranded DNA template is separated into two single strands by heat. (2) Annealing at 50–65°C — primers anneal (hybridize) to their complementary sequences on the single-stranded templates. (3) Extension at 72°C — Taq DNA polymerase extends from the 3' end of each primer, synthesizing new DNA strands in the 5'→3' direction. This cycle is repeated 25–35 times, exponentially amplifying the target DNA.
Q6. Which plant hormone is known as the "stress hormone" and promotes stomatal closure during water deficit?
(A) Auxin (IAA)    (B) Gibberellin    (C) Abscisic Acid (ABA)    (D) Cytokinin
Answer: (C) Abscisic Acid (ABA)
Explanation: ABA is called the stress hormone. Under drought/water stress conditions, ABA accumulates in leaves and triggers stomatal closure by causing guard cells to lose K⁺ ions (and water follows osmotically) → guard cells shrink → stomata close → reduces transpiration water loss. ABA also promotes seed dormancy, inhibits germination, and accelerates leaf abscission. It antagonizes the effects of gibberellins.
Q7. Which of the following is NOT a characteristic of the genetic code?
(A) The code is degenerate (redundant)
(B) The code is non-overlapping
(C) The code is ambiguous (same codon can code for two amino acids)
(D) The code is nearly universal
Answer: (C) The code is ambiguous
Explanation: The genetic code is UNAMBIGUOUS — each codon specifies only one amino acid (never two). This is different from degeneracy: many amino acids are encoded by multiple codons (degenerate/synonymous), but each specific codon always codes for the same one amino acid. The code is non-overlapping (codons are read sequentially without overlap), comma-less (no punctuation between codons within a reading frame), and nearly universal (same in all organisms with few exceptions like mitochondria).
Q8. In C4 plants, CO₂ is initially fixed by the enzyme PEP carboxylase in mesophyll cells into a 4-carbon compound. What is this compound?
(A) 3-Phosphoglycerate (3-PGA)    (B) Ribulose bisphosphate (RuBP)    (C) Oxaloacetate (OAA)    (D) Malate
Answer: (C) Oxaloacetate (OAA)
Explanation: In C4 plants, CO₂ + Phosphoenolpyruvate (PEP, 3C) → Oxaloacetate (OAA, 4C), catalyzed by PEP carboxylase in mesophyll cells. OAA is then converted to malate or aspartate (both 4C) and transported to bundle sheath cells. There, CO₂ is released and enters the Calvin cycle (C3 cycle) as usual. PEP carboxylase has much higher affinity for CO₂ than RuBisCO, and unlike RuBisCO, it cannot use O₂ as a substrate (no photorespiration in C4 plants).
Q9. Erythroblastosis fetalis occurs due to which type of incompatibility?
(A) ABO incompatibility    (B) Rh incompatibility    (C) MHC mismatch    (D) Platelet antigen incompatibility
Answer: (B) Rh incompatibility
Explanation: Erythroblastosis fetalis (hemolytic disease of the newborn) occurs when an Rh⁻ mother carries an Rh⁺ fetus. During first delivery, fetal Rh⁺ RBCs enter maternal blood → mother produces anti-Rh IgG antibodies. In second Rh⁺ pregnancy, maternal anti-Rh IgG crosses placenta → destroys fetal RBCs → severe anemia, jaundice, hydrops fetalis. Prevention: Rh-negative mother receives Rh immunoglobulin (anti-D) within 72 hours of delivery of first Rh⁺ baby.
Q10. Which of the following is an example of in-situ conservation?
(A) Seed bank    (B) Zoological park    (C) Biosphere Reserve    (D) Cryopreservation
Answer: (C) Biosphere Reserve
Explanation: In-situ conservation = conservation of species in their natural habitat. Examples: National Parks (Jim Corbett, Kaziranga), Wildlife Sanctuaries, Biosphere Reserves (Nilgiri, Sundarban), Sacred groves, Marine protected areas. Ex-situ conservation = outside natural habitat. Examples: Zoological parks (zoos), Botanical gardens, Seed banks, Gene banks, Cryopreservation, in vitro culture. Remember: the question mentions Biosphere Reserve (natural habitat) = in-situ; Seed bank, Zoo, Cryopreservation = ex-situ.

FAQ

What is the difference between mitosis and meiosis?

Mitosis produces 2 genetically identical diploid (2n) daughter cells and is used for growth, repair, and asexual reproduction. Meiosis produces 4 genetically unique haploid (n) cells used for sexual reproduction (gametes in animals; spores in plants). Meiosis involves two rounds of division: Meiosis I (reductive — separates homologous chromosomes, involves crossing over in Prophase I) and Meiosis II (equational — separates sister chromatids, like mitosis). Crossing over in Meiosis I creates genetic recombination, generating diversity.

What are the most important chapters in NEET biology?

The highest-weightage chapters are: Human Physiology (complete — digestion, circulation, respiration, excretion, neural, endocrine, locomotion — approximately 12–15 questions), Genetics and Evolution (Mendel, molecular genetics, evolution — 10–12 questions), Reproduction (human + plant — 8–10 questions), Cell Biology (structure, division, biomolecules — 6–8 questions), Plant Physiology (photosynthesis, respiration, hormones — 6–8 questions), Ecology and Environment (6–8 questions), and Biotechnology (4–6 questions). Together these account for approximately 70–75% of NEET biology questions.

What is the central dogma of molecular biology?

The central dogma describes the directional flow of genetic information: DNA → RNA → Protein. The three fundamental processes are: Replication (DNA → DNA; semiconservative; by DNA polymerase), Transcription (DNA → mRNA; by RNA polymerase), and Translation (mRNA → Protein; at ribosomes, using tRNA). Reverse transcription (RNA → DNA by reverse transcriptase) occurs in retroviruses like HIV. The concept was proposed by Francis Crick in 1958.

How does photosynthesis differ from cellular respiration?

Photosynthesis (in chloroplasts) converts light energy into chemical energy: 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + light → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂. It occurs in plants, algae, and cyanobacteria. Cellular respiration (mainly in mitochondria) releases energy stored in glucose: C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + ~30–32 ATP. Both processes are opposite in terms of reactants and products. Light reactions of photosynthesis occur in thylakoids; Calvin cycle in stroma. Glycolysis occurs in cytoplasm; Krebs cycle and ETC in mitochondria.

What is the difference between prokaryotic and eukaryotic cells?

Prokaryotes (Bacteria, Archaea) lack a membrane-bound nucleus and membrane-bound organelles. They have circular DNA (no histones in bacteria; archaea have histone-like proteins), 70S ribosomes, cell wall (peptidoglycan in bacteria), and are generally smaller (1–10 μm). Eukaryotes (animals, plants, fungi, protists) have a true nucleus with a nuclear envelope, linear DNA complexed with histones, 80S ribosomes (70S in organelles), and complex membrane-bound organelles (mitochondria, ER, Golgi). Eukaryotes are generally larger (10–100 μm). The endosymbiotic theory proposes mitochondria and chloroplasts evolved from engulfed prokaryotes.
