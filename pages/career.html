2. Scholarships for Indian Students

Indian students have access to a wide range of government-funded, institution-specific, and private scholarships for studying abroad. Here is a comprehensive overview of the most important ones.

Government of India Scholarships

  • National Overseas Scholarship (NOS): Funded by the Ministry of Social Justice and Empowerment. For SC/ST and other OBC students to pursue Masters and PhD abroad. Covers tuition, living allowance, and airfare. Apply through nospmsma.nic.in.
  • ICCR Scholarship (Indian Council for Cultural Relations): India offers outbound scholarships for study abroad through bilateral agreements. Also provides incoming scholarships for foreign students to study in India.
  • Commonwealth Scholarship: For postgraduate study in UK Commonwealth countries. Fully funded. Administered by the Commonwealth Scholarship Commission in the UK.
  • Inlaks Scholarship: For exceptional Indian students for postgraduate study at top universities abroad. Covers tuition and living expenses up to USD 100,000.

4. GRE Preparation Guide

The GRE General Test (Graduate Record Examination) is administered by ETS and required for admission to most graduate programs in the USA and many other countries. It tests Verbal Reasoning, Quantitative Reasoning, and Analytical Writing.

GRE Format (Revised GRE, as of 2023)

SectionStructureScore ScaleTime
Verbal Reasoning2 sections × 27 questions each130–170 (1-point increments)27 min per section (41 min total)
Quantitative Reasoning2 sections × 27 questions each130–170 (1-point increments)35 min per section (47 min total)
Analytical Writing (AW)1 essay task (Issue Essay)0–6 (0.5-point increments)30 minutes
Total Duration~1 hour 58 minutes

Note: The GRE was shortened in September 2023. The Argument Essay was removed; total time is now approximately 2 hours.

GRE Verbal Reasoning — Tips and Strategies

  • Text Completion (TC): Look for logical flow clues in surrounding sentences before choosing blanks. Transition words (however, although, therefore) are critical signals. For 3-blank TCs, treat each blank independently using surrounding context.
  • Sentence Equivalence (SE): The correct answer must be two words that (1) complete the sentence meaningfully AND (2) produce sentences with equivalent meaning. Both words must work — not just individually.
  • Reading Comprehension (RC): Read for structure, not detail. Identify the author's purpose, main argument, and tone first. Details are in the passage — you can return to them. For long passages, process the first sentence of each paragraph.
  • Vocabulary building: Use Magoosh GRE Vocabulary Flashcards (1000 words). Focus on high-frequency GRE vocabulary. Learn words in context sentences, not isolated definitions.
  • High-frequency GRE words to master: Abstruse, Ameliorate, Anachronism, Apposite, Calumny, Chicanery, Diffident, Duplicity, Enervate, Equivocate, Exacerbate, Fastidious, Garrulous, Impecunious, Imperturbable, Loquacious, Mendacious, Obsequious, Paradigm, Perfidy, Recondite, Solipsism, Sycophant, Tenuous, Veracity, Vituperate

GRE Quantitative Reasoning — Tips and Strategies

GRE Quant tests high school math (up to pre-calculus level) but with tricky problem setups and under tight time pressure.

  • Key topics: Arithmetic, Number Properties, Algebra, Word Problems, Geometry, Data Analysis and Statistics
  • Quantitative Comparison (QC) strategy: Before calculating, consider whether you can reason logically. Try plugging in numbers for variables. Check whether the relationship changes for different values.
  • Problem Solving shortcuts: Use approximation when answer choices are far apart. Work backward from answer choices for complex algebra. Draw diagrams for geometry problems.
  • Common trap questions: Integer vs decimal assumptions, positive vs negative number traps, "how many" vs "what is the value" distinction
  • Calculator use: GRE provides an on-screen calculator — use it for arithmetic but not as a crutch. Mental estimation is often faster.

GMAT Preparation Strategy

  • Official GMAT Prep: Start with the Official Guide (OG) and 2 free official practice tests from mba.com. These are the gold standard for realistic practice.
  • Quant: For most Indian and Chinese students with strong math backgrounds, focus time on Data Sufficiency (unique question type) and number properties. GMAT Quant tests reasoning, not calculation complexity.
  • Verbal (CR + RC): Critical Reasoning requires logical analysis of argument structure. Practice identifying premises, conclusions, and assumptions in every argument.
  • Data Insights: The new section combining Data Sufficiency with integrated reasoning. Practice reading complex data tables and multi-source scenarios.
  • Preparation timeline: 3–6 months for a 700+ score goal. Begin with a diagnostic test to identify weakest areas and allocate prep time accordingly.
  • Top prep resources: Manhattan Prep GMAT Focus materials, Magoosh GMAT, TargetTestPrep (Quant focused), TTP (highly recommended for Quant perfectionists).

6. Country-by-Country Study Abroad Guide

USA — Studying in the United States

  • Student Visa: F-1 Visa. Apply after receiving Form I-20 from your institution. Attend USCIS interview at US Embassy/Consulate. Processing time: 1–3 months.
  • Application system: Common App (CommonApp.org) for most undergraduate programs; GradCafe and individual university portals for graduate admissions.
  • Application timeline: Begin 15 months before intended start. Regular Decision deadline: January 1. Early Decision/Early Action: November 1.
  • Required tests: SAT or ACT (undergraduate); GRE (most graduate programs); GMAT (MBA); TOEFL or IELTS (non-native English speakers, typically TOEFL 100+ for top schools)
  • Financial aid for internationals: Need-based aid available at select universities (Harvard, MIT, Princeton, Yale, Amherst). Most schools offer merit scholarships to top international students.
  • OPT (Optional Practical Training): F-1 students can work in the US in their field for up to 3 years after graduation (12 months standard, 24-month STEM extension for CS/Engineering/Math/Science graduates).

UK — Studying in the United Kingdom

  • Application system: UCAS (ucas.com) for undergraduate admissions. Direct applications to universities for postgraduate programs.
  • Student Visa (formerly Tier 4): Requires a Confirmation of Acceptance for Studies (CAS) from your university. Apply up to 6 months before course start. Show funds: approximately GBP 1,334/month (London) or GBP 1,023/month (elsewhere).
  • Post-Study Work Visa: Graduate Visa allows 2 years (3 years for PhD graduates) of work in the UK after completing your degree. Excellent pathway for international students.
  • Required tests: A-Levels or equivalent for undergraduate (Indian 12th board results accepted at many universities); IELTS 6.5–7.5 for most programs; GRE/GMAT for postgraduate.
  • Top UK universities: Oxford (17% acceptance), Cambridge (21%), Imperial College London (14%), UCL (20%), LSE (20%), King's College London, University of Edinburgh, Manchester, Warwick.
  • Tuition: International undergraduate typically GBP 15,000–38,000/year. Postgraduate GBP 12,000–30,000/year depending on program and university.

Canada — Studying in Canada

  • Study Permit: Required for courses over 6 months. Apply online via IRCC portal. Processing time: 4–8 weeks for most applicants. Apply with acceptance letter and financial proof.
  • Top universities: University of Toronto (UofT, QS #26), UBC Vancouver (QS #34), McGill University (QS #46), University of Waterloo (tech/co-op focus), McMaster (medicine), Western University.
  • Co-op programs: Canada excels in co-op (cooperative education) where students alternate study and paid work terms. University of Waterloo is world-famous for this model. Excellent for international students seeking Canadian work experience.
  • Post-Graduation Work Permit (PGWP): International graduates can work in Canada for a period equal to the length of their study program (up to 3 years). Clear pathway to Permanent Residency through Express Entry.
  • PR pathway: Canadian Experience Class (CEC) through Express Entry is one of the most accessible PR routes for international graduates with Canadian work experience. Strong demand for engineers, nurses, IT professionals.
  • Tuition: CAD 20,000–40,000/year for international undergraduate students. Postgraduate: CAD 15,000–35,000/year.

Australia — Studying in Australia

  • Student Visa (Subclass 500): Apply online. Requires Confirmation of Enrolment (CoE) from institution. Must show financial capacity and English proficiency (IELTS 6.0+).
  • Top universities: University of Melbourne (QS #28), UNSW Sydney (QS #19), University of Sydney (QS #42), ANU Canberra (QS #30), Monash University, University of Queensland.
  • Post-Study Work Stream: Temporary Graduate Visa (Subclass 485) allows 2–6 years of work in Australia after graduation depending on location and degree level. Regional study grants extra years.
  • Scholarships: Australia Awards (fully funded for developing country students), Destination Australia (for regional study), Research Training Program (for PhD students).
  • Tuition: AUD 20,000–45,000/year for international students. Medicine and veterinary programs higher. Living costs: AUD 21,000+/year minimum.

Germany — The Free Tuition Destination

  • Free tuition at public universities: Most public German universities charge only a semester fee (EUR 150–350) for all students including internationals. One of the best-value study destinations globally.
  • Blocked account: Must show EUR 11,208 (as of 2024) in a blocked German bank account as financial proof for student visa. Funds are released gradually each month after arrival.
  • Language requirements: German-taught programs require TestDaF (minimum 4 in all areas) or DSH (DSH-2 or DSH-3). English-taught Masters programs growing rapidly at TU Munich, RWTH Aachen, LMU Munich, University of Heidelberg, etc.
  • DAAD Scholarship: Germany's primary scholarship program for international students. Over 100,000 scholarships annually. Apply at daad.de. Both full and partial funding available.
  • Top German universities: TU Munich (QS #37), LMU Munich, Heidelberg University, Humboldt Berlin, RWTH Aachen (Engineering), KIT Karlsruhe.
  • Post-study work: 18-month job-seeker visa after graduation. Can convert to work visa once employed in a relevant field. Germany has high demand for engineers and IT professionals.

Management and MBA

  • When to do MBA: Most top MBA programs (Harvard, Stanford, Wharton) prefer 3–7 years of post-undergraduate work experience. Plan your career accordingly.
  • Work experience matters: Average Harvard MBA student has 5 years of work experience. Consulting, banking, and technology backgrounds are most common.
  • MBA vs PGDM in India: IIM PGP (Post Graduate Programme) is equivalent to MBA in industry value. IIM A, B, C graduates command the highest packages in India.
  • Executive MBA (EMBA): For mid-career professionals (8+ years experience) who want a leadership-focused MBA without leaving work. Good for Chinese and Indian professionals already in senior roles.
  • Specializations in demand: Finance (Investment Banking, PE/VC), Consulting (McKinsey, BCG, Bain target MBA programs), Technology Management, Healthcare Management.

Research and Academia

  • PhD application process: Contact professors before applying. Write a strong Statement of Purpose (SOP) explaining your specific research interest and fit with the professor's work. GRE scores, publications, and research experience are critical differentiators.
  • Research Statement: Must clearly articulate: (1) the problem you want to solve, (2) why it matters, (3) your approach, (4) your qualifications. 2 pages maximum for most programs.
  • Fellowship opportunities for PhD applicants: NSF GRFP (USA, open to international students at US universities), DOE Computational Science Graduate Fellowship, Hertz Fellowship, NDSEG Fellowship.
  • Funding in STEM PhDs: In the USA, most STEM PhD programs offer full funding: tuition waiver plus stipend (USD 20,000–40,000/year). Do not pay for a STEM PhD in the USA.
  • Research internships: DAAD RISE (Germany, for undergrads), MITACS Globalink (Canada), CERN Summer Student Program (Switzerland), IIT Research Internship for Chinese students.

8. Frequently Asked Questions

What GRE score is needed for top US universities?

For top US CS programs (MIT, Stanford, CMU, Berkeley), competitive applicants typically have Quant scores of 167 or above (96th+ percentile) and Verbal scores of 158 or above. For STEM programs at other strong universities (Purdue, UIUC, Georgia Tech), Quant 163+ and Verbal 153+ is competitive. For Humanities and Social Science programs, Verbal scores of 160+ are often more important. Note: Many top programs have made GRE optional since 2020, but submitting a strong GRE score still strengthens your application.

Which country is best for Indian students to study abroad?

The best country depends on your goals. USA: best for academic prestige, research opportunities, and post-study OPT work options (3 years for STEM). Canada: excellent for post-study work permit (up to 3 years) and the clearest pathway to Permanent Residency, especially through Express Entry. UK: strong universities, 2-year post-study work visa, shorter degree duration (3-year undergrad, 1-year Masters). Germany: free tuition at public universities, strong engineering programs, 18-month job-seeker visa after graduation. Australia: good universities, 2-6 year post-study work visa, high quality of life. For budget-conscious students wanting PR pathways, Canada is often the top recommendation. For academic prestige and research, USA is unmatched.

How can Chinese students apply for Fulbright or Chevening?

Fulbright is available to Chinese students through the US-China Fulbright Program. Eligible Chinese students can apply for Fulbright scholarships to study in the USA. Apply through the US Embassy in Beijing or local Fulbright commission. Chevening is a UK Government scholarship for international students including Chinese applicants. Requirements: Chinese nationality, at least 2 years of work experience, a plan to return to China after studies, and a letter of unconditional acceptance from a UK university. Apply at chevening.org during the August to November application window each year.

Is a Masters degree in Germany really free for international students?

Yes, most public German universities (Technische Universitat Munchen, Heidelberg, Humboldt, RWTH Aachen) charge no tuition fees for international students at the Masters and PhD level. You pay only a semester fee of approximately EUR 150 to 350 per semester for student union services and public transport. However, you must show sufficient funds (the blocked account with EUR 11,208 as of 2024 for visa purposes) and cover living costs of approximately EUR 800 to 1,200 per month. Private universities in Germany do charge tuition. Germany offers excellent value for world-class engineering and STEM education with essentially zero tuition cost.

When should I start preparing for US university applications?

For US undergraduate admissions, start 15 to 18 months before your intended entry. Key timeline: Grade 11 (take SAT/ACT, shortlist 10 to 15 colleges, begin extracurricular documentation), Summer before Grade 12 (college essays, campus visits if possible), Grade 12 September to November (Early Decision/Early Action applications due), Grade 12 December to January (Regular Decision applications due). For US graduate admissions, start 12 months before. Research professors in September, take GRE by October, request LORs in September, submit applications by December 1 to 15 for most programs.

What IELTS score is needed to study in the UK or Australia?

For UK universities, most undergraduate programs require IELTS 6.5 overall (with minimum 6.0 in each band). Oxford and Cambridge typically require 7.0 to 7.5 overall. For UK Student Visa, the minimum is IELTS 4.0 to 5.5 depending on CAS level. For Australian Student Visa (Subclass 500), the minimum IELTS is typically 5.5 overall. However, university programs require 6.0 to 7.0 depending on the course. Medicine and teaching programs in both countries generally require IELTS 7.0 or above. TOEFL iBT is accepted as an alternative to IELTS at most universities in both countries.

Should Indian engineers do an MBA or MS in the USA?

This depends entirely on your career goal. Choose an MS if you want deep technical expertise and aim for research, technical specialist, or senior engineering roles at tech companies. MS graduates enter at individual contributor technical roles. Starting salaries at top tech companies: USD 130,000 to 180,000 for MS CS graduates. Choose an MBA if you want to transition to product management, consulting, investment banking, or general management. MBA requires 3 to 7 years of work experience first and opens doors to roles with higher long-term earning potential and broader organizational impact. The MS to MBA pathway (3 to 5 years engineering work, then MBA from a top program) is increasingly popular among Indian engineers aiming for leadership roles at technology companies.

9. Statement of Purpose (SOP) Writing Guide

A compelling Statement of Purpose (SOP) is often the single most important component of a graduate school application. Admissions committees read thousands of applications; a poorly written SOP can eliminate a strong academic profile while an outstanding SOP can compensate for a slightly below-average GPA or test score.

Structure of a Strong SOP

Paragraph 1: Opening Hook

  • Start with a specific experience, problem, or moment that ignited your interest in the field
  • Avoid generic openings like "Since childhood I have been fascinated by..."
  • The opening should demonstrate intellectual curiosity and specificity
  • Example opening angle: a research problem you encountered, a project that failed and what you learned, or a specific question you cannot stop thinking about

Paragraphs 2-3: Academic and Research Background

  • Highlight 2 to 3 most relevant academic experiences or research projects
  • Be specific: mention the research question, methodology you used, and what you found or learned
  • Do not just list accomplishments; analyze what the experience taught you
  • If you have publications, conference papers, or thesis work, mention them here

Paragraph 4: Professional Experience (if applicable)

  • Connect work experience to your academic goals
  • Explain problems you encountered at work that motivated graduate study
  • For MBA applicants: this is often the core of your SOP
  • Show what skills you have and what skills you still need (and why this program provides them)

Paragraphs 5-6: Why This Program / Professor

  • Name 2 to 3 specific professors whose research aligns with your interests
  • Reference their specific papers, projects, or labs (shows you have done real research)
  • Mention unique program features: specific courses, research centers, industry partnerships
  • Explain why this specific program is the right fit, not just any good program

Closing Paragraph: Future Goals

  • Articulate specific short-term goals (first 2 years post-graduation) and long-term goals (5 to 10 year vision)
  • Explain how this degree is a necessary step in your specific career path
  • For international students: briefly mention your home country impact or global contribution

Common SOP Mistakes to Avoid

  • Writing about childhood dreams rather than current academic interests
  • Using the same generic SOP for every university (always customize)
  • Exceeding the word limit (typically 500 to 1,000 words for most programs)
  • Focusing entirely on accomplishments rather than intellectual growth
  • Poor grammar, passive voice overuse, and verbose academic jargon

10. Letter of Recommendation (LOR) Strategy

Letters of Recommendation (LORs) provide third-party validation of your academic ability, research potential, and professional character. Most programs require 2 to 3 LORs. The quality and specificity of LORs can significantly impact admission decisions, particularly for competitive programs.

UndergraduateAcademic teachers, extracurricular mentors, part-time work supervisorsSubject teacher firstGeneric form-letter style recommendations

How to Request an Effective LOR

  • Ask early: Request LORs at least 6 to 8 weeks before the earliest deadline. Professors are busy; last-minute requests result in generic letters.
  • Provide a "brag package": Give your recommender a document containing your CV, research summary, the programs you are applying to, why those programs, and 2 to 3 specific projects or interactions you had with them that you would like them to highlight.
  • Remind them of specific moments: Remind your professor about the project you worked on, the paper you wrote, the grade you received, or the problem you solved in their class. This enables them to write specific, compelling details.
  • Send deadline reminders: Send a polite reminder 2 weeks before the deadline and 3 to 5 days before the deadline.
  • Thank them regardless of outcome: Always send a thank-you note after submission and update your recommender on admissions outcomes.

Scholarship Application Success Strategies

  • Apply broadly: Apply to 8 to 12 scholarships, not just 1 or 2. Most scholarships have low acceptance rates (Fulbright: 20% of finalists; Gates Cambridge: 1 in 200 applicants). Volume of high-quality applications increases success probability.
  • Start early: Many prestigious scholarships (Rhodes, Marshall, Fulbright) open applications in August for the following year's entry. Most Indian and Chinese students miss deadlines by starting too late.
  • Leadership narrative: All major international scholarships (Fulbright, Gates Cambridge, Chevening, Rhodes) explicitly look for demonstrated leadership potential and commitment to community impact. Document your leadership experiences throughout your undergraduate years.
  • Research fit: For research scholarships, tailor your research proposal to align with the funding priorities of the scholarship body. Fulbright prioritizes US-India/China cultural exchange; Gates Cambridge looks for social impact in research.
  • Interview preparation: Chevening, Rhodes, Marshall, and Gates Cambridge all include selection interviews. Prepare for questions about your research proposal, global issues in your field, and your post-degree plans. Mock interviews with professors or mentors are essential.
  • University-specific funding: Beyond named scholarships, most universities offer merit scholarships, teaching assistantships (TA), and research assistantships (RA). TA and RA positions at US PhD programs typically include full tuition waiver plus monthly stipend of USD 1,800 to 3,500.
