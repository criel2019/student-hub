2. Atomic Radius

Across a period (left → right): Atomic radius DECREASES ↓

Reason: Nuclear charge (Z) increases by 1 each step, but electrons are added to the same shell. More nuclear charge pulls electrons closer → smaller atom.

Down a group (top → bottom): Atomic radius INCREASES ↑

Reason: Each period adds a new electron shell, which is farther from the nucleus. Increased shielding also reduces effective nuclear charge felt by outer electrons.

Types of Atomic Radius

TypeDefinitionExampleWhen Used
Covalent radiusHalf the bond length between two identical atoms in a covalent bondC–C in diamond: r = 77 pmNon-metals and metalloids
Van der Waals radiusHalf the distance between two non-bonded identical atoms in closest contactNoble gases, packed moleculesNoble gases; always larger than covalent
Metallic radiusHalf the distance between nearest nuclei in a metallic crystalNa–Na: r = 186 pmMetals
Ionic radiusRadius of an ion (cations smaller, anions larger than neutral atom)Na (186 pm) → Na⁺ (102 pm)Ionic compounds

Isoelectronic Species (same number of electrons)

For isoelectronic species, the one with more protons has a smaller radius (stronger pull on same number of electrons).

Cations series: Na⁺ (11p) < Mg²⁺ (12p) < Al³⁺ (13p) [all have 10 electrons; more protons → smaller]

Anions series: N³⁻ (7p) > O²⁻ (8p) > F⁻ (9p) [all have 10 electrons; fewer protons → larger]

Mixed: O²⁻ > F⁻ > Na⁺ > Mg²⁺ > Al³⁺ (all 10 electrons)

Anomalies in Atomic Radius

  • Ga < Al: Gallium is smaller than aluminum despite being below it. Reason: d-block contraction. The 10 d-electrons added in period 4 (from Sc to Zn) provide poor shielding → Ga's outer electrons feel a larger Z_eff → Ga is unexpectedly small.
  • 4th-period d-block elements do not increase much in radius from Sc to Zn: nuclear charge increases but d-electrons shield poorly.
  • Lanthanide contraction: Lanthanides (4f filling) have very poor f-electron shielding → Z_eff increases → 5th and 6th period d-block elements have very similar radii (Zr ≈ Hf; Nb ≈ Ta).

3. Ionization Energy (IE)

Definition: Minimum energy required to remove the most loosely bound electron from a gaseous atom in its ground state.

M(g) + Energy → M⁺(g) + e⁻    (1st ionization energy, IE₁)

Across a period: IE generally INCREASES (higher Z_eff, electrons harder to remove)

Down a group: IE generally DECREASES (outer electrons farther from nucleus, more shielded)

Important Exceptions in Ionization Energy

AnomalyComparisonReason
Be > BIE₁(Be) = 900 kJ/mol > IE₁(B) = 801 kJ/molB loses a 2p electron (less stable, higher energy) while Be loses a 2s electron (fully filled 2s, stable). Also 2s penetrates more than 2p.
N > OIE₁(N) = 1402 kJ/mol > IE₁(O) = 1314 kJ/molN has half-filled 2p³ (extra stability due to exchange energy of parallel spins). O has paired electrons in one p orbital → electron-electron repulsion → easier to remove one.
Mg > AlSimilar to Be > B patternMg loses 3s electron; Al loses 3p electron (easier to remove).
P > SSimilar to N > O patternP has half-filled 3p³ stable; S has one paired 3p electron.

Successive Ionization Energies

IE₁ < IE₂ < IE₃ < ... (each successive IE is larger; removing from more positive ion)

Sudden jump in IEn: Indicates that the (n)th ionization removes a core electron (inner shell). This tells you the number of valence electrons.

Example: For Mg: IE₁ = 738, IE₂ = 1451, IE₃ = 7733 kJ/mol → huge jump at IE₃ → Mg has 2 valence electrons (Group 2).

Example: For Al: Small jumps at IE₁, IE₂, IE₃; large jump at IE₄ → 3 valence electrons (Group 13).

4. Electron Affinity (EA)

Definition: Energy released (or absorbed) when an electron is added to a neutral gaseous atom.

X(g) + e⁻ → X⁻(g) + Energy    (negative EA = energy released = stable anion)

Higher (more negative) EA = atom more readily accepts an electron = more electronegative tendency

Across period: EA generally becomes more negative (more exothermic) as nuclear charge increases

Down group: EA generally becomes less negative (less exothermic) as radius increases

Important Exceptions in Electron Affinity

AnomalyExplanation
F has lower EA than ClF is very small; adding an electron causes more electron-electron repulsion in the compact 2p orbital. Cl is larger → added electron experiences less repulsion. EA(Cl) = −349 kJ/mol vs EA(F) = −328 kJ/mol.
N has very low (nearly zero) EAN has half-filled stable 2p³ configuration. Adding electron disrupts stability → EA(N) ≈ 0 or slightly positive (endothermic).
Be and Mg have nearly zero EAFully filled s-subshell (ns²) → extra electron goes into a higher-energy p orbital → not favorable.
Noble gases have positive EAAdding electron to noble gas requires it to go into a new outer shell → always endothermic → positive EA (not favorable).
O has lower EA than SO²⁻ requires electron addition against repulsion; O is smaller → more repulsion than S.

5. Electronegativity

Definition (Pauling scale): The tendency of an atom in a molecule to attract the shared pair of electrons towards itself.

Across period: Increases (↑ nuclear charge, same period → stronger pull)

Down group: Decreases (↑ atomic radius, electrons farther → weaker pull)

Highest EN: F = 4.0 (most electronegative element)

Lowest EN: Cs = 0.7 (or Fr ≈ 0.7); Fr is theoretical

Applications of Electronegativity

  • Predicting bond polarity and dipole moment
  • Acid strength: More electronegative atoms attached to OH → more acidic (stabilize conjugate base). HF < HCl < HBr < HI (acid strength in water – bond energy effect dominates)
  • Inductive effect (–I): Electronegative substituents withdraw electron density through sigma framework
  • Oxidation state assignment: More electronegative atom gets lower (more negative) oxidation state in bond

6. Metallic Character

Across period: Decreases (Li → Ne; metals on left, non-metals on right)

Down group: Increases (Na > Li; metals become more electropositive)

Metallic character is inversely related to ionization energy: lower IE = easier to form M⁺ = more metallic.

7. Diagonal Relationships

Diagonal pairs in the periodic table (element in period n, group g has similar properties to element in period n+1, group g+1) because they have similar charge/radius ratio (ionic potential).

Diagonal PairSimilar Properties
Li – Mg Both form nitrides (Li3N, Mg3N2); react slowly with water; form carbides; LiCl and MgCl2 both deliquescent; similar ionic radii (Li⁺ = 76 pm, Mg²⁺ = 72 pm)
Be – Al Both amphoteric oxides (BeO and Al2O3); form covalent halides; soluble in both acids and alkalis; form polymeric hydrides; BeH2 and AlH3 both polymeric; diagonal charge/radius ratio similar
B – Si Both form weak/acidic oxides (B2O3, SiO2); both form volatile chlorides that hydrolyze; form network covalent structures; similar electronegativity; both semiconductor-like behavior

Anomalous Behavior of Li and Be

  • Li (vs other alkali metals): Hard, high mp, does not give flame color easily, reacts slowly with water, forms stable nitride (Li3N), oxide (Li2O, not Li2O2 or LiO2 like others). Li resembles Mg (diagonal relationship).
  • Be (vs other alkaline earths): Forms covalent compounds, amphoteric oxide (BeO), does not react with water, BeF2 is polymeric, BeH2 is polymeric. Be resembles Al (diagonal relationship).

p-Block Elements (Groups 13–18)

  • Group 15 (N, P, As, Sb, Bi): N2 has very strong triple bond (bond energy 945 kJ/mol) → very inert at room temperature. Allotropes: P (white, red, black); As (gray, yellow, black)
  • Group 16 (O, S, Se, Te, Po): O2 is paramagnetic (2 unpaired electrons; confirmed by MO theory). S allotropes: rhombic (stable at room temp) and monoclinic. Ozone (O3) is a pale blue gas; strong oxidizing agent.
  • Group 17 (Halogens): F2 is most electronegative and most oxidizing. HF has anomalously high bp (19.5°C) due to H-bonding. HI is strongest acid among hydrogen halides.
  • Group 18 (Noble Gases): XeF2, XeF4, XeF6, XeO3, XeO4, XeOF4 are known xenon compounds. Kr and Rn form limited compounds. He and Ne form no compounds.
  • Allotropes of Carbon: Diamond (sp3, hardest natural substance), Graphite (sp2 layers, lubricant, conducts electricity), Fullerene (C60, buckminsterfullerene, soccer-ball shape), Graphene (single layer of graphite)

Lanthanide Contraction – Explanation and Consequences

Lanthanide contraction: As 4f electrons are added from Ce to Lu, the nuclear charge increases by 1 each time. However, 4f electrons shield each other very poorly (due to their diffuse shape). Therefore, the effective nuclear charge felt by outer electrons increases significantly → atomic/ionic radius decreases steadily across the lanthanide series despite going down the period.

Consequences:

  • 5th and 6th period d-block elements have nearly identical radii (Zr ≈ Hf, Nb ≈ Ta) → very difficult to separate these pairs
  • 6th period transition metals have higher densities and stronger bonds than expected
  • Chemical properties of Zr and Hf are nearly indistinguishable

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

Q: Why does atomic radius decrease across a period but increase down a group?

Across a period: nuclear charge (Z) increases by 1 at each step while electrons are added to the same principal shell. Greater nuclear charge pulls all electrons (including outer ones) closer → radius decreases. Down a group: each new period adds a new electron shell farther from the nucleus. The increasing number of electron shells (and the shielding by inner electrons) means the outer electrons are held much farther out → radius increases.

Q: Why is the ionization energy of N higher than O?

Nitrogen has a half-filled 2p³ configuration (one electron in each of the three 2p orbitals). This arrangement has extra stability due to exchange energy (parallel spins minimize electron-electron repulsion). Removing an electron from this stable configuration requires more energy than removing a paired electron from oxygen (which has one paired 2p orbital). The paired electrons in O repel each other → one is easier to remove → IE(O) < IE(N).

Q: Why does F have a lower electron affinity than Cl?

Fluorine is very small (2p orbital). When a new electron is added to already compact 2p orbital, significant electron-electron repulsion occurs within the small orbital. Chlorine is larger (3p), so the added electron has more space and experiences less repulsion → Cl releases more energy when gaining an electron → Cl has higher (more negative) EA than F. Note: F is more electronegative overall, but EA specifically measures energy change upon gaining one electron.

Q: What are the exceptions in electronic configuration and why?

Chromium (Cr, Z=24): Expected [Ar]3d⁴4s², actual [Ar]3d⁵4s¹. The half-filled 3d⁵ (five electrons, one per orbital, all same spin) is extra stable due to symmetric exchange energy. Copper (Cu, Z=29): Expected [Ar]3d⁹4s², actual [Ar]3d¹⁰4s¹. Fully filled 3d¹⁰ is more stable. In both cases, one electron "promotes" from 4s to 3d to achieve the extra-stable half-filled or fully filled d subshell. Similar exceptions: Mo [Kr]4d⁵5s¹, Ag [Kr]4d¹⁰5s¹, Au [Xe]5d¹⁰6s¹, Pd [Kr]4d¹⁰ (no 5s electrons!).

Q: What is the difference between amphoteric and neutral oxides?

Amphoteric oxides (like Al2O3, ZnO) react with BOTH acids and bases. They are metal oxides that are neither fully basic nor fully acidic. In acid: Al2O3 + 6HCl → 2AlCl3 + 3H2O (basic behavior). In base: Al2O3 + 2NaOH → 2NaAlO2 + H2O (acidic behavior). Neutral oxides (like CO, NO, N2O) do NOT react with either acids or bases at normal conditions. They are simple covalent oxides that are literally neutral (no acid-base reaction).
