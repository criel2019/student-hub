1. Reaction Types Overview

Organic reactions can be broadly classified into five categories based on what happens to the bonds and atoms during the transformation:

Reaction Type Description Key Examples Bond Change
Substitution One atom or group is replaced by another SN1, SN2, EAS (electrophilic aromatic substitution) One bond broken, one formed
Addition Atoms added across a multiple bond (C=C or C=O) Hydrohalogenation, Hydrogenation, Halogenation, Hydration Pi bond broken, two sigma bonds formed
Elimination Atoms removed to form a double or triple bond E1, E2, Dehydration of alcohols, Dehydrohalogenation Two sigma bonds broken, one pi bond formed
Oxidation–Reduction Change in oxidation state of carbon KMnO4 oxidation, NaBH4 reduction, Jones oxidation, Swern Electron transfer; C–H broken or C–O formed
Rearrangement Carbon skeleton reorganizes; isomers interconvert Beckmann, Baeyer–Villiger, Wagner–Meerwein, Pinacol Migration of group to adjacent carbon
Tip for JEE/NEET: Always identify the reaction type first. Ask: Is there a leaving group? (Substitution or Elimination) Is there a pi bond? (Addition) Is there a carbonyl? (Nucleophilic addition or named reaction)

2. Nucleophilic Substitution Reactions

2.1 SN2 Mechanism (Substitution Nucleophilic Bimolecular)

Mechanism (Concerted, One Step)

  Nu:⁻  +  C–LG  ──────→  [Nu---C---LG]‡  ──────→  Nu–C  +  LG⁻
          (back-side attack)   (transition state)      (inverted product)

  Example:  HO⁻  +  CH3–Br  →  HO–CH3  +  Br⁻
  • Rate: Rate = k[substrate][Nu]   (Second order; depends on both)
  • Mechanism: Single concerted step – nucleophile attacks the back side of the C–LG bond simultaneously as LG departs
  • Stereochemistry: 100% Walden Inversion (R → S or S → R at the reacting carbon)
  • Substrate Reactivity: CH3X > 1° > 2° >> 3° (steric hindrance increases with substitution)
  • Best Solvents: Polar aprotic – DMSO, DMF, acetone, acetonitrile (dissolves cation, does NOT solvate anion → nucleophile is "naked" and reactive)
  • Good Nucleophiles: I⁻, CN⁻, RS⁻, HO⁻, RO⁻, N3⁻, Br⁻, NH3
  • Leaving Group Order: I⁻ > Br⁻ > Cl⁻ >> F⁻ (better LG = weaker conjugate base)
  • Neopentyl and bridgehead halides: Extremely slow or unreactive in SN2 due to steric hindrance

Solved Example 1 – Simple SN2

Q: CH3CH2Br + NaOH (aqueous DMSO) → ?

Solution: Primary substrate + strong nucleophile (OH⁻) + polar aprotic solvent → SN2.

OH⁻ attacks back side of C–Br bond → CH3CH2OH + Br⁻ (ethanol). No rearrangement; inversion at sp3 carbon (for primary, stereochemistry not an issue as product is achiral).

Answer: CH3CH2OH (ethanol)

Solved Example 2 – Stereochemistry in SN2

Q: (R)-2-bromobutane + CN⁻ (DMSO) → ?

Solution: 2° substrate. CN⁻ is a good nucleophile. Polar aprotic solvent. → SN2 occurs.

Back-side attack at C2 → complete inversion → (S)-2-methylbutanenitrile.

Answer: (S)-2-methylbutanenitrile (100% inversion of configuration)

Solved Example 3 – SN2 or SN1?

Q: Predict whether (CH3)2CHBr reacts by SN1 or SN2 with: (a) NaCN/DMSO, (b) H2O (no added nucleophile)

Solution: (a) Strong nucleophile, polar aprotic → SN2 (2° substrate can do SN2 with good Nu).

(b) Weak nucleophile (H2O), polar protic → SN1 (2° substrate can ionize slowly).

2.2 SN1 Mechanism (Substitution Nucleophilic Unimolecular)

Mechanism (Two Steps)

  Step 1 (RDS, slow):  R–LG  →  R⁺  +  LG⁻  (ionization; forms carbocation)
  Step 2 (fast):        R⁺  +  Nu  →  R–Nu   (nucleophile attacks flat carbocation)
  • Rate: Rate = k[substrate]   (First order; only depends on substrate)
  • Substrate Reactivity: 3° > 2° > 1° >> CH3 (based on carbocation stability)
  • Stereochemistry: Racemization (flat carbocation attacked from both sides), sometimes slight inversion if ion pair partially shields one face
  • Best Solvents: Polar protic – H2O, ROH, RCOOH (solvate and stabilize the leaving group and carbocation)
  • Carbocation Stability: 3° > 2° > allylic/benzylic > 1° > CH3⁺
  • Rearrangements possible: Carbocation intermediate can rearrange via hydride shift or methyl shift to give more stable carbocation → unexpected products

Solved Example 4 – SN1 with Racemization

Q: (R)-3-chloro-3-methylpentane + H2O → ?

Solution: 3° substrate, weak nucleophile (H2O), polar protic solvent → SN1.

Forms a stable 3° carbocation (flat, sp2) → H2O attacks from both faces equally → racemic mixture of (R) and (S)-3-methyl-3-pentanol.

Answer: Racemic 3-methyl-3-pentanol (50:50 R and S)

Solved Example 5 – Rearrangement in SN1

Q: 3-chloro-2,2-dimethylbutane + AgNO3/EtOH → major product?

Solution: 3° substrate → SN1 → forms 3° carbocation at C3. Adjacent C2 has a methyl group. Methyl shift from C2 to C3 → more stable (still 3°) but now (CH3)3C–CH+(CH3) type. OEt⁻ attacks → ether product. In water → rearranged alcohol is major product.

Solved Example 6 – Predict SN1 Product Stereochemistry

Q: (S)-2-bromobutane undergoes SN1 with CH3OH. What stereochemistry of product?

Answer: Racemic mixture of (R)- and (S)-2-methoxybutane (since flat carbocation is attacked from both sides equally).

3. Elimination Reactions

3.1 E2 Mechanism (Elimination Bimolecular)

  • Mechanism: Single concerted step – base removes β-H simultaneously as LG leaves; π bond forms
  • Geometry required: Anti-periplanar arrangement (H and LG must be 180° apart; trans or anti-coplanar)
  • Rate: Rate = k[substrate][base] (2nd order)
  • Zaitsev's Rule: Major product = more substituted alkene (more stable, more hyperconjugation)
  • Hofmann's Rule: When a bulky base (t-BuO⁻) is used → less substituted alkene (less hindered β-H removed)
  • Favored by: 2°, 3° substrates + strong, non-bulky base + high temperature
  • Strong bases for E2: t-BuO⁻ (Hofmann), NaOH, KOH, RO⁻

Solved Example – E2 (Zaitsev vs Hofmann)

Q: 2-bromobutane + (a) KOH/EtOH, (b) KO-t-Bu/t-BuOH → products?

(a) KOH/EtOH (small base): E2 → Zaitsev product → but-2-ene (more substituted, major) + but-1-ene (minor). Predominantly trans-but-2-ene.

(b) KO-t-Bu/t-BuOH (bulky base): E2 → Hofmann product → but-1-ene (less substituted, less hindered β-H, major).

3.2 E1 Mechanism (Elimination Unimolecular)

  • Step 1 (RDS): Ionization → carbocation (same first step as SN1)
  • Step 2: Base (or solvent) removes β-H → alkene formed
  • Rate: Rate = k[substrate] (1st order)
  • Regioselectivity: Follows Zaitsev's rule (more stable alkene major)
  • Competes with SN1: Both form carbocation; higher temperature → more elimination
  • Substrate: 3° > 2°; requires stable carbocation

3.3 Decision Chart: SN1 vs SN2 vs E1 vs E2

  START: What is the substrate?
  │
  ├─ Methyl (CH3X) or Primary (1°)
  │   └─ Strong nucleophile/base? → SN2 (fast)
  │   └─ Very bulky strong base? → E2 (rare for methyl)
  │
  ├─ Secondary (2°)
  │   ├─ Strong nucleophile, polar aprotic → SN2
  │   ├─ Strong bulky base, high temp → E2 (major)
  │   └─ Weak nucleophile/base, polar protic → SN1 + E1 mixture
  │
  └─ Tertiary (3°)
      ├─ Strong base (KOH/EtOH), high temp → E2
      ├─ Weak nucleophile (H2O), polar protic → SN1 + E1
      └─ Strong Nu, low temp → SN1 still dominates (steric!)
         (Note: 3° never does SN2 – too hindered)

  Temperature: High temperature always favors elimination over substitution.
  Solvent: Polar aprotic → SN2; Polar protic → SN1/E1

4. Electrophilic Addition to Alkenes

4.1 Hydrohalogenation (HX Addition)

  • Reagent: HCl, HBr, HI
  • Markovnikov's Rule: H adds to the carbon bearing more hydrogens (less substituted); X adds to the more substituted carbon (more stable carbocation intermediate)
  • Mechanism: Proton (E⁺) attacks π bond → more stable carbocation → halide attacks carbocation
  • Reactivity of HX: HI > HBr > HCl (bond dissociation energy decreases)
  • Anti-Markovnikov (Peroxide effect): HBr + ROOR (peroxides) → radical mechanism → Br adds to less substituted carbon (H adds to more substituted)
  • Note: Anti-Markovnikov only works with HBr (HCl and HI do not give anti-Markovnikov even with peroxides)

Solved Example: CH2=CHCH3 + HBr → ? (a) Normal conditions (b) Peroxide conditions

(a) Markovnikov: Br adds to C2 (more substituted) → CH3CHBrCH3 (2-bromopropane)

(b) Anti-Markovnikov (peroxide): Br adds to C1 (less substituted) → BrCH2CH2CH3 (1-bromopropane)

4.2 Halogenation (Br2 or Cl2 Addition)

  • Reagent: Br2/CCl4 or Cl2/CCl4
  • Mechanism: Cyclic halonium ion (3-membered ring) intermediate → nucleophile attacks back side → anti addition (trans product)
  • Stereochemistry: Anti addition (vicinal dihalide, trans/anti configuration)
  • Test: Br2/CCl4 solution turns from brown to colorless → confirms presence of C=C double bond
  • Product: Vicinal dihalide (halogen on adjacent carbons)

Solved Example: cis-2-butene + Br2/CCl4 → ?

Anti addition via bromonium ion → (2R,3S)-2,3-dibromobutane (meso compound). Trans-2-butene + Br2 → (2R,3R) + (2S,3S) racemic mixture.

4.3 Acid-Catalyzed Hydration (H2O/H3O⁺)

  • Reagent: H2O + H3O⁺ (dilute H2SO4)
  • Rule: Markovnikov addition (OH adds to more substituted carbon)
  • Mechanism: H⁺ adds to less substituted C → carbocation at more substituted C → H2O attacks
  • Rearrangements: Possible if less stable to more stable carbocation interconversion occurs

4.4 Catalytic Hydrogenation (H2/Pd or Pt or Ni)

  • Mechanism: Syn addition (both H atoms add to same face of double bond)
  • Catalyst: Pd/C, PtO2 (Adams), Ni (Raney Ni); heterogeneous surface catalysis
  • Result: Alkene → alkane (reduction)
  • Stereochemistry: Syn (cis) addition

4.5 Ozonolysis

  • Step 1: O3 reacts with alkene → ozonide (molozonide → ozonide)
  • Oxidative workup (H2O2): Ozonide → carboxylic acids (terminal CH2= → CO2 + H2O; internal R-CH= → RCOOH; R2C= → R2CO ketone stays)
  • Reductive workup (Zn/H2O or Me2S or PPh3): Ozonide → aldehydes (R-CH=) or ketones (R2C=)

5. Electrophilic Aromatic Substitution (EAS)

In EAS, the aromatic ring acts as the nucleophile. The electrophile attacks the ring, forming an unstable arenium ion (σ-complex), which then loses H⁺ to restore aromaticity.

ReactionReagentsElectrophile GeneratedProduct
HalogenationCl2/FeCl3 or Br2/FeBr3Cl⁺ or Br⁺ (activated by Lewis acid)Chloro- or Bromobenzene
NitrationHNO3 + H2SO4 (conc.)NO2⁺ (nitronium ion)Nitrobenzene
SulfonationSO3 + H2SO4 (fuming)SO3 (or H2S2O7)Benzenesulfonic acid
Friedel-Crafts AlkylationRCl + AlCl3R⁺ (carbocation)Alkylbenzene
Friedel-Crafts AcylationRCOCl + AlCl3RCO⁺ (acylium ion)Aryl ketone (e.g., acetophenone)
Why Acylation is preferred over Alkylation in synthesis:
  • Alkylation: Polyalkylation occurs (product is more reactive than starting material); carbocation rearrangements give unexpected products
  • Acylation: Product (ketone) deactivates the ring → monosubstitution only; no rearrangements (acylium ion is stable); can reduce the carbonyl afterward (Clemmensen or Wolff-Kishner) to get alkylated product cleanly

6. Important Named Reactions – Complete Reference

6.1 Aldol Condensation

Reagents / Conditions: Dilute NaOH or KOH (base-catalyzed); or dilute acid

Requirements: Carbonyl compound must have α-hydrogen (at least one H adjacent to C=O)

Reaction:

  2 CH3CHO  ──NaOH/H2O──→  CH3CH(OH)CH2CHO  (aldol; β-hydroxy aldehyde)
                  −H2O (heat)
                 ──────────→  CH3CH=CHCHO  (crotonaldehyde; α,β-unsaturated aldehyde)

Key Points:

  • Aldol = aldehyde + alcol (has both –OH and –CHO)
  • Dehydration (on heating) gives α,β-unsaturated carbonyl compound (enal or enone)
  • Mixed (Claisen-Schmidt): Aromatic aldehyde (no α-H) + ketone + base → chalcone (only one partner has α-H, so mixed aldol is clean)
  • Intramolecular aldol: Forms 5- or 6-membered rings (thermodynamically preferred)

Solved Example: CH3COCH3 (acetone) + base → ?

Self-aldol: diacetone alcohol → mesityl oxide (dehydration). But with NaOH/cold: CH3COCH2CH(OH)CH3 (wait – both carbonyl compounds are acetone). Enolate of one acetone attacks carbonyl of another → 4-hydroxy-4-methylpentan-2-one (diacetone alcohol).

6.2 Cannizzaro Reaction

Reagents / Conditions: Concentrated NaOH or KOH (no heat needed)

Requirements: Aldehyde with NO α-hydrogen (HCHO, PhCHO, (CH3)3CCHO, R-CHO where R has no α-H)

  2 HCHO  ──conc. NaOH──→  CH3OH  +  HCOONa
  (formaldehyde)           (methanol)  (sodium formate)

  2 C6H5CHO  ──NaOH──→  C6H5CH2OH  +  C6H5COONa
  (benzaldehyde)           (benzyl alcohol) (sodium benzoate)

Key Points:

  • Disproportionation: one molecule oxidized (→ carboxylate), one reduced (→ alcohol)
  • Hydride transfer mechanism: OH⁻ attacks carbonyl → alkoxide with hydride; hydride transferred to second aldehyde molecule
  • Crossed Cannizzaro: HCHO + ArCHO + NaOH → ArCH2OH + HCOONa (HCHO is always the one oxidized to formate; ArCHO is reduced to ArCH2OH)

Solved Example: PhCHO + HCHO + conc. KOH → ? (crossed Cannizzaro)

HCHO (better hydride donor, more reactive) acts as reducing agent → oxidized to HCOOK. PhCHO reduced → PhCH2OH (benzyl alcohol).

Answer: Benzyl alcohol + potassium formate

6.3 Claisen Condensation

Reagents / Conditions: NaOEt (sodium ethoxide, strong base) in ethanol

  2 CH3COOC2H5  ──NaOEt──→  CH3COCH2COOC2H5  +  C2H5OH
  (ethyl acetate)              (ethyl acetoacetate, a β-ketoester)
  • Requires ester with α-H; two ester molecules condense
  • Product: β-ketoester (one α-H between two carbonyls → very acidic, pKa ~11)
  • Dieckmann cyclization: Intramolecular Claisen → cyclic β-ketoester; used to make 5- and 6-membered rings
  • Mixed Claisen: One ester has no α-H (e.g., ethyl benzoate) + one ester with α-H → mixed β-ketoester (clean reaction)

6.4 Grignard Reaction

Reagent: RMgX (alkyl or aryl magnesium halide; Grignard reagent)

Conditions: Dry ether (Et2O) or THF; strictly anhydrous, moisture-free!

Solved Example 1: Prepare (CH3)3COH from Grignard reagent.

Use CH3MgBr (3 equivalents not needed; use CH3MgBr + acetone → (CH3)2C(OH)CH3). Wait: (CH3)3COH is t-butanol. Use CH3MgBr + (CH3)2CO → (CH3)3COH. Yes! Methyl Grignard + acetone → t-butanol.

Solved Example 2: PhMgBr + CO2 → ? (after H3O⁺ workup)

PhMgBr acts as carbanion (Ph⁻) → attacks CO2 → Ph–COO⁻MgBr⁺ → H3O⁺ → PhCOOH (benzoic acid).

Solved Example 3: Grignard reagent from 1-bromopentane + HCHO → product?

1-bromopentane + Mg/ether → n-PentylMgBr. + HCHO → n-PentylCH2OH (1-hexanol, primary alcohol).

6.5 Diels-Alder Reaction

Type: [4+2] Cycloaddition (pericyclic, concerted, thermally allowed by Woodward–Hoffmann)

Components: Diene (4π system) + dienophile (2π system) → cyclohexene

  CH2=CH–CH=CH2  +  CH2=CH2  →  Cyclohexene
  (1,3-butadiene)   (ethylene)
  • Diene requirement: Must be in s-cis conformation (both double bonds on same side); s-trans diene is unreactive
  • Dienophile: Electron-poor dienophiles react fastest: maleic anhydride, acrylonitrile, TCNE, acrolein, maleic acid; simple alkenes react slowly
  • Stereospecificity: Syn addition; substituents on dienophile retain their relative orientation in product (cis dienophile → cis product)
  • Endo Rule: In bicyclic products, the endo product is kinetically preferred (secondary orbital interactions stabilize TS); exo is thermodynamic product
  • Retro Diels-Alder: Reverse reaction at high temperatures; used in synthesis

Solved Example: Cyclopentadiene + maleic anhydride → ?

Cyclopentadiene is locked in s-cis. Maleic anhydride is excellent dienophile (two electron-withdrawing groups). Endo product = bicyclo[2.2.1]hept-5-ene-2,3-dicarboxylic anhydride (norbornene anhydride, endo isomer major).

6.6 Wolff-Kishner Reduction

  R2C=O  ──NH2NH2, KOH, Ethylene glycol, Δ──→  R2CH2
  (carbonyl)                                     (methylene)
  • Conditions: Basic (KOH or NaOH), ethylene glycol solvent, high temperature
  • Mechanism: Carbonyl → hydrazone (C=NNH2) → base removes H from N → diazene intermediate → N2 lost → carbanion → protonated → CH2
  • Use: Removes ketone/aldehyde to give methylene group; good when substrate is acid-sensitive
  • Huang Minlon modification: Uses excess KOH in ethylene glycol without isolation of hydrazone

6.7 Clemmensen Reduction

  R2C=O  ──Zn(Hg), conc. HCl, Δ──→  R2CH2
  • Conditions: Acidic (conc. HCl), zinc amalgam catalyst
  • Complementary to Wolff-Kishner: Use Clemmensen for base-sensitive substrates; use Wolff-Kishner for acid-sensitive substrates
  • Application: Synthesis of alkylbenzenes via Friedel-Crafts acylation + Clemmensen reduction (avoids rearrangement of Friedel-Crafts alkylation)

Application: Synthesize n-propylbenzene from benzene.

Benzene + propanoyl chloride + AlCl3 → propiophenone (C6H5COCH2CH3). Then Clemmensen reduction → n-propylbenzene (C6H5CH2CH2CH3).

6.8 Baeyer-Villiger Oxidation

  Ketone + mCPBA (or peracid)  →  Ester  (or Lactone from cyclic ketone)
  R-CO-R'  →  R-CO-O-R'  or  R-O-CO-R'  (depends on migratory aptitude)
  • Reagents: mCPBA, peracetic acid (CH3CO3H), perbenzoic acid, H2O2/H2SO4
  • Migratory Aptitude: 3° alkyl > cyclohexyl > 2° alkyl > Ph > 1° alkyl > CH3 > H
  • Rule: The group with higher migratory aptitude migrates preferentially → ends up on oxygen side of ester (becomes the alkoxy group)
  • Cyclic ketones → Lactones: Cyclohexanone → ε-caprolactone (7-membered ring); cyclopentanone → δ-valerolactone
  • Retention of configuration: Migrating group retains its stereochemistry

6.9 Beckmann Rearrangement

  Ketoxime (R2C=NOH)  ──H⁺ or PCl5──→  Amide (R-CO-NHR')
  Example: Cyclohexanone oxime  →  ε-Caprolactam  (Nylon-6 precursor!)
  • Conditions: Acid catalyst (H2SO4, PCl5, BF3)
  • Mechanism: OH protonated → OH leaves; group anti to OH migrates → nitrilium ion → H2O attacks → amide
  • Stereochemistry: The group anti (trans) to the –OH migrates (anti periplanar migration)
  • Industrial importance: Cyclohexanone → cyclohexanone oxime → Beckmann → ε-caprolactam → Nylon-6 by ring-opening polymerization

6.10 Hell-Volhard-Zelinsky (HVZ) Reaction

  RCOOH  ──Br2, PBr3 (cat.)──→  RCH(Br)COOH
  (carboxylic acid)              (α-bromo acid)
  • α-bromination of carboxylic acids using Br2 + red phosphorus (or PBr3 as catalyst)
  • Only works for carboxylic acids (not esters or amides)
  • Useful for preparing α-amino acids via Gabriel synthesis after bromination

6.11 Wittig Reaction

  Ph3P=CHR  +  R'2C=O  →  R'2C=CHR  +  Ph3P=O
  (phosphonium ylide)  (carbonyl)   (alkene)  (triphenylphosphine oxide)
  • Conditions: Room temperature, mild
  • Advantage over Grignard: Does not react with OH, COOH, ester, halide groups; very selective for C=O
  • Stereochemistry: Non-stabilized ylides → cis (Z) alkenes; stabilized ylides (with Ph or carbonyl) → trans (E) alkenes
  • Ylide preparation: Ph3P + RX → Ph3P⁺–CH2R X⁻ (phosphonium salt) → strong base (n-BuLi) → Ph3P=CHR

Solved Example: Synthesize (Z)-2-butene using Wittig reaction.

Use CH3CHO + Ph3P=CHCH3 (non-stabilized ylide) → Z-selective → (Z)-CH3CH=CHCH3 + Ph3P=O.

6.12 Reformatsky Reaction

  α-Bromoester + Zn  →  Reformatsky reagent (zinc enolate)
  Zinc enolate + RCHO  →  (after hydrolysis)  β-Hydroxy ester
  • Similar to Grignard but milder; zinc carbanion is less reactive than Grignard
  • Does not react with ester groups in molecule → useful for substrates with esters

Practice Problems (15 Questions)

Q1. Which of the following substrates reacts fastest by SN2 with NaCN in DMSO?
(A) (CH3)3CCl   (B) (CH3)2CHCl   (C) CH3CH2Cl   (D) CH3Cl

Show Answer

Answer: (D) CH3Cl – SN2 favors least hindered substrate. Methyl chloride is least hindered; tertiary is most hindered and does not react via SN2 at all.

Q2. (R)-2-bromobutane reacts with NaOH in DMSO. What is the stereochemistry of the product?

Show Answer

Answer: (S)-butan-2-ol – SN2 (2° substrate, good nucleophile, polar aprotic solvent) → 100% inversion → configuration changes from R to S.

Q3. An aldehyde X reacts with conc. NaOH to give benzyl alcohol and sodium benzoate. Identify X.

Show Answer

Answer: Benzaldehyde (C6H5CHO) – Cannizzaro reaction; benzaldehyde has no α-H. Under conc. NaOH it disproportionates: one PhCHO → PhCH2OH (reduced), one PhCHO → PhCOO⁻Na⁺ (oxidized).

Q4. What is the major product of 2-methylbut-2-ene + HBr (no peroxide)?

Show Answer

Answer: 2-bromo-2-methylbutane – Markovnikov addition. H adds to C3 (has 1H), Br adds to C2 (no H, more substituted, forms 3° carbocation).

Q5. Give the product of: Cyclohexanone + NH2OH → (product A) + H2SO4 → ?

Show Answer

Answer: ε-Caprolactam – Cyclohexanone + NH2OH → cyclohexanone oxime (A). Then Beckmann rearrangement with H2SO4 → ε-caprolactam (Nylon-6 precursor). The C–C bond anti to OH migrates to N.

Q6. Which reagent converts PhCOCH3 (acetophenone) to PhCH2CH3 (ethylbenzene)?

Show Answer

Answer: Zn(Hg)/conc. HCl (Clemmensen reduction) or alternatively N2H4/KOH (Wolff-Kishner). Both remove the carbonyl oxygen to give a methylene group.

Q7. Identify the product: 1,3-butadiene + acrolein (CH2=CHCHO) [Diels-Alder].

Show Answer

Answer: Cyclohex-3-enecarbaldehyde (3-cyclohexene-1-carbaldehyde) – Diels-Alder [4+2] cycloaddition; acrolein is an electron-poor dienophile; CHO group ends up in the ring as substituent on the cyclohexene ring.

Q8. What is the product of ozonolysis (reductive workup, Zn/H2O) of (Z)-4-octene?

Show Answer

Answer: 2 × butanal (CH3CH2CH2CHO) – Internal alkene, symmetric; both sides give CH3CH2CH2CHO. Reductive workup gives aldehyde, not acid.

Q9. CH3CH2MgBr + CO2 → [H3O⁺] → ?

Show Answer

Answer: Propanoic acid (CH3CH2COOH) – Grignard adds to CO2 → RCOOMgBr → acidic workup → RCOOH. Ethyl Grignard + CO2 → propanoic acid.

Q10. Which direction does –NO2 group direct in EAS, and why?

Show Answer

Answer: Meta direction – –NO2 is a strong electron-withdrawing group via –M effect (resonance withdrawal). Ortho and para positions are made electron-poor; meta position retains relatively more electron density → electrophile attacks meta.

Q11. In E2 elimination of 2-bromobutane with KOH/EtOH, what is the major alkene product?

Show Answer

Answer: but-2-ene (major, Zaitsev) – E2 with non-bulky base follows Zaitsev's rule. More substituted alkene (but-2-ene) is thermodynamically more stable than but-1-ene. trans-But-2-ene is preferred over cis-but-2-ene.

Q12. What product is formed when ethyl acetate undergoes Claisen condensation with NaOEt?

Show Answer

Answer: Ethyl acetoacetate (CH3COCH2COOEt) – Two molecules of ethyl acetate condense; one loses α-H as enolate attacks the ester carbonyl of the other → β-ketoester (ethyl 3-oxobutanoate) + EtOH.

Q13. Cyclopentanone + mCPBA → product? (Baeyer-Villiger)

Show Answer

Answer: δ-Valerolactone (oxacyclohexan-2-one, 6-membered lactone) – Baeyer-Villiger inserts an oxygen adjacent to carbonyl. Cyclic ketone → ring expansion → one carbon larger lactone. Cyclopentanone → 6-membered lactone.

Q14. What does Ph3P=CHCOOC2H5 (stabilized ylide) + benzaldehyde (PhCHO) give?

Show Answer

Answer: trans (E)-ethyl cinnamate (PhCH=CHCOOC2H5) – Wittig with stabilized ylide (ester group) → trans alkene (E-selective). Product is ethyl (E)-3-phenylacrylate (trans-ethyl cinnamate).

Q15. A compound X reacts with Tollens' but not Fehling's reagent. What type of compound is X?

Show Answer

Answer: Aromatic aldehyde (e.g., benzaldehyde) – Tollens' (Ag(NH3)2⁺) oxidizes both aliphatic and aromatic aldehydes. Fehling's (Cu2+ in alkaline tartrate) only oxidizes aliphatic (alpha-hydroxy) aldehydes; aromatic aldehydes do not reduce Fehling's reagent.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

Q: How do I decide between SN1, SN2, E1, E2 quickly in an exam?

Use the "TESS" checklist: Tertiary → SN1/E1 (weak Nu) or E2 (strong base, high temp); Excess strong base + high temp → elimination; Secondary → depends on conditions; Strong Nu + polar aprotic → SN2. Methyl/primary always go SN2 unless very bulky base forces E2. The key shortcuts: no SN2 at 3°; no E1 or SN1 at methyl/1°; high temp always favors elimination.

Q: Why does NaBH4 not reduce esters and carboxylic acids but LiAlH4 does?

LiAlH4 (hydride source with Al–H) is much more powerful: the hydride is more nucleophilic and the reaction is irreversible. NaBH4 is a milder hydride donor: the hydride attacks only highly electrophilic carbonyls (aldehydes and ketones). Esters and carboxylic acids are less electrophilic (resonance donation from O reduces C=O electrophilicity) and require the stronger LAH to reduce.

Q: What is the difference between Aldol condensation and Claisen condensation?

Both involve enolate formation and nucleophilic addition to a carbonyl. Aldol condensation involves two carbonyl compounds (both carbonyl; at least one must have α-H); the product is a β-hydroxy carbonyl compound (aldol), which can dehydrate to an α,β-unsaturated carbonyl. Claisen condensation involves two esters (or ester + ketone); the product is a β-ketoester (or 1,3-diketone). The driving force in Claisen is the deprotonation of the acidic α-H of the β-ketoester product.

Q: Why is the Diels-Alder reaction stereospecific?

The Diels-Alder is a concerted pericyclic reaction (all bonds form and break simultaneously in one step via a cyclic transition state). Because it is concerted, there is no carbocation or radical intermediate that could rotate. The relative stereochemistry of dienophile substituents is therefore preserved in the product (cis dienophile → cis ring substituents; trans dienophile → trans ring substituents). The endo product is kinetically favored due to secondary orbital interactions in the TS.

Q: When should I use Wolff-Kishner vs Clemmensen reduction?

Both convert C=O to CH2. Clemmensen uses acidic conditions (Zn(Hg)/conc. HCl) – use this when the substrate is base-sensitive (e.g., contains acetal, β-hydroxy carbonyl, or other groups unstable to base). Wolff-Kishner uses basic conditions (N2H4, KOH, Δ) – use this when the substrate is acid-sensitive (e.g., acid-labile double bonds, acetals). For JEE/NEET, remember: Clemmensen = acid; Wolff-Kishner = base. Both remove oxygen from carbonyl. After Friedel-Crafts acylation, use Clemmensen to get alkylbenzene without rearrangement.
