IELTS Writing Task 2 – Complete Guide

Sample Essays (Band 7–8), Templates, Useful Phrases & Tips to Boost Your Score

Time: 40 minutes Minimum words: 250 Weight: 2/3 of Writing score Recommended: 40 minutes on Task 2

1. Overview of IELTS Writing Task 2

IELTS Writing Task 2 requires you to write an essay in response to a point of view, argument or problem. It is the same for both Academic and General Training candidates. You must write at least 250 words and spend approximately 40 minutes on this task.

Scoring Criteria (4 × 25%)

Your essay is marked on four equally weighted criteria:

Criterion What It Measures Key Requirements
Task Achievement (TA) How well you address all parts of the task Answer all questions, present a clear position, support ideas with evidence
Coherence & Cohesion (CC) Logical flow and use of linking devices Clear paragraph structure, appropriate connectives, logical sequencing
Lexical Resource (LR) Range and accuracy of vocabulary Varied word choice, precise collocations, correct spelling
Grammatical Range & Accuracy (GRA) Variety and correctness of grammar Mix of simple and complex sentences, few errors, punctuation

Band Descriptors Summary

Band Task Achievement Coherence & Cohesion Lexical Resource Grammar
9 Fully addresses all parts; position fully developed Seamless, logical; cohesion unnoticed Full flexibility; sophisticated control Wide range; virtually no errors
8 Sufficiently addresses all parts; well-developed Sequences ideas logically; effective cohesion Wide range; rare minor errors Wide range; few minor errors
7 Addresses all parts; clear position; main ideas supported Clear progression; some cohesion issues Sufficient range; some errors in less common vocabulary Variety of complex forms; some errors
6 Addresses task; position relevant but conclusions may be unclear Coherent but faulty cohesion Adequate range; errors in complex vocabulary Mix of simple/complex; errors, but meaning clear
5 Partly addresses task; position inconsistent Some organisation but no progression Limited range; noticeable errors Limited range; frequent errors

2. Essay Types and How to Identify Them

Identifying the essay type is the first step. Read the question carefully and look for signal words.

Essay Type Question Signal Words Recommended Structure
Opinion / Agree–Disagree "Do you agree or disagree?", "To what extent do you agree?", "What is your opinion?" Intro (state opinion) + Body 1 (reason 1) + Body 2 (reason 2) + Conclusion
Discussion "Discuss both views", "Some people believe... others think...", "Consider both sides" Intro + Body 1 (View A) + Body 2 (View B) + Conclusion (your opinion)
Problem–Solution "What problems does X cause?", "What solutions can be proposed?", "How can this be solved?" Intro + Body 1 (Problems) + Body 2 (Solutions) + Conclusion
Advantages–Disadvantages "What are the advantages and disadvantages?", "Do the benefits outweigh the drawbacks?" Intro + Body 1 (Advantages) + Body 2 (Disadvantages) + Conclusion (balanced)
Two-Part Question Two separate question sentences ending in "?" Intro + Body 1 (Answer Q1) + Body 2 (Answer Q2) + Conclusion
Pro Tip: Spend 5 minutes planning before you write. Identify essay type, brainstorm 2–3 ideas per body paragraph, and note vocabulary. This prevents going off-topic.

3. Essay Structure Templates

Opinion Essay Template

Introduction:
In today's [topic context], [paraphrase the question topic]. While some people argue that [opposing view], I firmly believe that [your position] because [brief reasons — 1–2 words each].

Body Paragraph 1 (Main Reason):
The primary reason I support this view is [main point]. [Explanation + development of idea]. For example, [specific example with data or real-world reference]. This clearly demonstrates that [link back to thesis].

Body Paragraph 2 (Supporting Reason):
Furthermore, [second reason]. [Explanation]. A notable example of this is [example]. Consequently, [conclusion sentence for this paragraph linking to thesis].

Conclusion:
In conclusion, I maintain that [restate position] due to [summary of main reasons]. [Optional: future prediction or recommendation — 1 sentence].

Discussion Essay Template

Introduction:
The question of [topic] has sparked considerable debate in recent years. [Brief context sentence]. This essay will examine both perspectives before presenting my own view.

Body Paragraph 1 (First View):
Proponents of [first view] argue that [main point]. They contend that [explanation/development]. [Specific example or evidence]. Consequently, [impact/result].

Body Paragraph 2 (Second View):
On the other hand, opponents maintain that [second view]. [Explanation]. [Example]. This suggests that [connection to argument].

Conclusion:
In conclusion, while [first view] has merit, I believe [your position] is more convincing because [strongest reason]. [Optional recommendation or future outlook].

Problem–Solution Template

Introduction:
[Topic] has become an increasingly serious issue in many parts of the world. This essay will examine the main causes of this problem and propose viable solutions.

Body Paragraph 1 (Problems/Causes):
One of the primary causes of this issue is [cause 1]. [Explanation]. Another significant factor is [cause 2]. [Explanation + example].

Body Paragraph 2 (Solutions):
To address these problems, [solution 1] could be implemented. [Explanation of how]. In addition, [solution 2] would help alleviate [specific aspect of the problem]. [Development].

Conclusion:
In conclusion, [restate problem] is a multifaceted issue. However, by [summary of solutions], it is possible to [desired outcome].

Advantages–Disadvantages Template

Introduction:
[Topic] is a subject of ongoing debate. This essay will consider the main benefits and drawbacks of [topic], before reaching a balanced conclusion.

Body Paragraph 1 (Advantages):
There are several notable advantages to [topic]. Firstly, [advantage 1]. [Explanation]. Moreover, [advantage 2]. [Evidence/example].

Body Paragraph 2 (Disadvantages):
Nevertheless, [topic] also carries significant disadvantages. The most serious is [disadvantage 1]. [Explanation]. Furthermore, [disadvantage 2]. [Example].

Conclusion:
In conclusion, although [topic] offers [key benefit], the [key drawback] cannot be overlooked. On balance, [your overall position].

4. Sample Essay 1 – Discussion Essay Band 8

Topic: Some people believe that universities should focus on providing academic knowledge and skills. Others think universities should prepare students for their future careers. Discuss both views and give your opinion.

The role of higher education has long been a subject of vigorous debate. While traditionalists argue that universities exist primarily to cultivate intellectual curiosity and theoretical knowledge, others contend that these institutions bear a responsibility to equip students with practical skills for the modern workforce. In my view, the most effective universities should strive to integrate both purposes.

Those who advocate for a purely academic focus maintain that universities serve a unique function distinct from vocational training schools. Pursuing subjects such as philosophy, pure mathematics, or classical literature broadens students' critical thinking abilities, nurtures creativity, and enriches their understanding of the human experience. Graduates who develop these transferable intellectual skills often prove highly adaptable in a rapidly changing job market, as they can apply analytical reasoning across multiple fields. Harvard University's liberal arts model, for instance, has produced numerous leaders in business, law, and public service precisely because it encourages deep intellectual exploration.

On the other hand, proponents of career-focused education argue that the considerable financial investment of a university education must yield tangible employment outcomes. With the cost of tuition rising globally and graduate unemployment remaining stubbornly high in many nations, universities that ignore vocational preparation do their students a disservice. Engineering programmes that include industry placements, or business degrees incorporating real-world consulting projects, have demonstrably higher graduate employment rates. Students who leave university equipped with professional networks and sector-specific competencies are better positioned to contribute meaningfully from their first day of employment.

In conclusion, while both perspectives carry weight, I believe the false dichotomy between academic learning and career preparation should be abandoned. The most forward-thinking universities already blend rigorous theoretical study with applied professional experience, and this integrated approach ultimately best serves both the individual graduate and the broader economy.

Examiner Analysis – Why this scores Band 8:
  • TA Addresses all parts of the task; clear position stated in intro and reaffirmed in conclusion
  • CC Logical progression; effective use of "While...", "On the other hand", "In conclusion"; each paragraph has a clear topic sentence
  • LR Sophisticated vocabulary: "vocational training", "liberal arts model", "transferable intellectual skills", "tangible employment outcomes", "false dichotomy"
  • GRA Wide range of structures: complex sentences, passive voice, relative clauses; virtually no errors

5. Sample Essay 2 – Problem–Solution Band 7

Topic: Many cities are experiencing serious problems with traffic congestion. What are the causes of this problem? What measures could be taken to solve it?

Traffic congestion has emerged as one of the most pressing urban challenges of the 21st century, affecting millions of commuters worldwide and causing significant economic and environmental damage. This essay will examine the principal causes of this problem and propose several practical remedies.

The main causes of urban traffic congestion are closely linked to rapid urbanisation and inadequate infrastructure planning. As cities expand, the volume of private vehicles on the road has grown exponentially, outpacing the capacity of existing road networks. In cities such as Bangkok and Mumbai, the number of registered vehicles has increased by over 300% in the past two decades, while road capacity has grown by less than 50%. Additionally, insufficient investment in public transportation forces residents to rely on private cars for their daily commutes, further intensifying road congestion during peak hours.

Several effective measures could alleviate these problems. Firstly, governments should invest heavily in expanding and modernising public transport systems, including metro networks, bus rapid transit, and cycle lanes. When residents have access to reliable, affordable alternatives, many will choose to leave their cars at home. Secondly, congestion pricing schemes, such as those implemented in London and Singapore, have proven highly effective at reducing peak-hour traffic by charging drivers a fee to enter city centres. Finally, encouraging remote working through tax incentives for employers could permanently reduce commuter volumes during rush hours.

In conclusion, traffic congestion stems primarily from unplanned urban growth and over-reliance on private vehicles. However, through investment in public transport, the introduction of congestion charges, and flexible working arrangements, cities can significantly reduce gridlock and improve the quality of life for their residents.

Band 7 Features:
  • TA Both questions answered clearly; each body paragraph addresses one part
  • CC "Additionally", "Firstly", "Secondly", "Finally", "However" used effectively; clear paragraph structure
  • LR Good range: "exponentially", "peak-hour traffic", "gridlock", "congestion pricing schemes"; minor collocational imprecision
  • GRA Complex sentences used; occasional awkward phrasing but meaning always clear

6. Sample Essay 3 – Agree/Disagree Band 7

Topic: Some people think that the increasing use of technology in education has more disadvantages than advantages. Do you agree or disagree?

The integration of digital technology into educational settings has transformed classrooms around the world. Although some critics argue that this shift is ultimately detrimental, I disagree with this view and believe that technology brings substantially more benefits than drawbacks to modern education.

The most compelling advantage of educational technology is that it dramatically expands access to learning resources. Online platforms such as Khan Academy, Coursera, and YouTube allow students in remote or underprivileged communities to access world-class instruction that would otherwise be unavailable to them. Furthermore, interactive software can personalise the learning experience, adjusting the pace and difficulty of tasks to match each student's individual needs — something a single teacher managing thirty pupils simply cannot achieve. Studies in Finland and South Korea have shown that students who use adaptive learning platforms consistently outperform peers in standardised assessments.

Critics, however, point to genuine concerns. Excessive screen time has been linked to reduced concentration spans, increased rates of anxiety, and diminished face-to-face social interaction among young people. There is also the issue of digital inequality: in many developing nations, unreliable electricity supply and unaffordable devices mean that technology-based education widens rather than bridges the attainment gap. These are legitimate concerns that policymakers must address.

In conclusion, while the disadvantages of educational technology are real and should not be ignored, I maintain that its benefits — particularly in terms of accessibility and personalisation — significantly outweigh its drawbacks, provided that governments invest in digital infrastructure and establish sensible guidelines for screen use.

Band 7 Features: Clear position throughout; good use of examples (Khan Academy, Finland/South Korea); acknowledges opposing view without being swayed; vocabulary range adequate for Band 7 with phrases like "adaptive learning platforms", "digital inequality", "attainment gap".

7. Sample Essay 4 – Advantages/Disadvantages Band 7.5

Topic: In some countries, young people are encouraged to work or travel for a year between finishing high school and starting university. Do the advantages of this outweigh the disadvantages?

The concept of taking a "gap year" between secondary school and higher education has grown substantially in popularity, particularly in Western nations. While there are valid concerns about the potential disruption this may cause to academic momentum, I believe the overall benefits of this practice are considerable and outweigh the drawbacks.

A gap year offers young people the invaluable opportunity to develop maturity, self-reliance, and a clearer sense of purpose before committing to a degree programme. Students who spend time working in different industries or volunteering abroad often enter university with a far stronger understanding of how their chosen field connects to real-world challenges. Research from the University of Melbourne found that gap year students showed higher levels of academic motivation and reported greater satisfaction with their degree choices compared to peers who transitioned directly from school. This suggests that taking time to gain perspective can actually improve long-term academic outcomes.

The main disadvantage, however, is the financial cost. International travel and extended periods without income can place considerable strain on young people and their families, potentially increasing student debt. There is also a risk that individuals who defer their education may lose academic habits or find it difficult to re-engage with formal study after a year away. In some cases, unforeseen circumstances during the gap year may even prevent students from returning to education at all.

Overall, I believe the personal growth, clarity of direction, and enhanced motivation gained through a well-planned gap year represent benefits that outweigh the financial and academic risks, particularly when students approach the experience with clear goals and proper financial planning.

8. Sample Essay 5 – Modern Topic (AI) Band 8

Topic: Artificial intelligence is increasingly being used across many sectors of society. Do the benefits of this development outweigh the risks?

Few technological developments in recent memory have generated as much excitement — or anxiety — as the rapid proliferation of artificial intelligence. From healthcare diagnostics to financial trading, AI systems are now embedded in critical areas of modern life. Having considered both the transformative potential and the genuine risks, I believe the benefits of AI development currently outweigh its dangers, provided robust governance frameworks are established.

The most significant advantages of AI lie in its capacity to process vast quantities of data at speeds no human team could match, leading to breakthroughs in fields that directly save lives. In medicine, AI algorithms have demonstrated diagnostic accuracy for cancers such as melanoma that rivals or exceeds that of specialist dermatologists. In climate science, machine learning models are accelerating our understanding of atmospheric patterns, helping policymakers design more effective mitigation strategies. Furthermore, AI-driven automation is boosting productivity across manufacturing and logistics, freeing human workers to focus on creative, interpersonal, and strategic tasks that machines cannot replicate.

Nevertheless, the risks must not be minimised. The displacement of workers by automation poses a serious threat to economic stability, particularly for lower-skilled employees with limited means to retrain. Additionally, algorithmic bias — where AI systems perpetuate or amplify existing societal prejudices — has already produced troubling outcomes in areas such as criminal sentencing and loan approval. The potential for AI to be weaponised or used for mass surveillance further raises urgent ethical and geopolitical concerns that international institutions are ill-prepared to address.

In conclusion, while the risks of AI are substantial and demand urgent regulatory attention, the technology's proven capacity to enhance healthcare, scientific research, and economic productivity means its benefits represent a compelling case for continued, carefully governed development. The challenge for society lies not in resisting AI, but in shaping it responsibly.

Band 8 Features: Sophisticated vocabulary throughout ("proliferation", "mitigation strategies", "algorithmic bias", "geopolitical", "regulatory attention"); nuanced position (benefits outweigh risks IF governed properly); excellent paragraph cohesion; minimal errors; strong conclusion with original framing.

9. Useful Phrases for IELTS Writing Task 2

Introduction Phrases

  • It is widely acknowledged that...
  • In recent years, there has been growing concern about...
  • The issue of [X] has become increasingly prominent in contemporary society.
  • It is a matter of considerable controversy whether...
  • [Topic] has long been a subject of intense debate among academics and policymakers alike.
  • With [technology / globalisation / urbanisation] advancing at an unprecedented pace, [topic] has become...

Adding Points

  • Furthermore, / Moreover, / In addition, / Additionally,
  • Not only... but also...
  • What is more, ...
  • A further point worth noting is that...
  • Beyond this, ...

Contrasting & Conceding

  • However, / Nevertheless, / On the other hand, / That said,
  • Despite [noun] / In spite of [noun]
  • Although / While / Whereas + clause
  • Conversely, / By contrast,
  • Admittedly, [concession], but [counter-argument].
  • While it is true that [opposing point], the evidence suggests that [your point].

Giving Examples

  • For instance, / For example,
  • A case in point is [country / company / study]...
  • This is clearly illustrated by [example].
  • Research conducted by [institution] found that...
  • According to [source], ...
  • A striking example of this can be found in [place/context]...

Cause and Effect

  • As a consequence of / As a result of / Due to / Owing to...
  • This has led to / This has resulted in...
  • Consequently, / Therefore, / Thus, / Hence,
  • One of the main reasons for this is that...
  • This can be attributed to...

Concluding

  • In conclusion, / To conclude, / To sum up,
  • In summary, / All things considered, / On balance,
  • I maintain that... / I firmly believe that... / I am convinced that...
  • The evidence clearly suggests that...
  • Looking ahead, [future prediction or recommendation].

12. IELTS Writing Task 2 Final Checklist

Use this before you finish writing and when you review your practice essays:

  • Have I answered ALL parts of the question?
  • Is my position/opinion clearly stated?
  • Does my introduction paraphrase the question (not copy it)?
  • Do I have a clear introduction, at least 2 body paragraphs, and a conclusion?
  • Does each body paragraph have a clear topic sentence?
  • Is each main point supported with an explanation and/or example?
  • Have I used a variety of linking words (not just "firstly, secondly, finally")?
  • Is my vocabulary precise and varied? Have I avoided repetition?
  • Have I used a mix of sentence structures (simple and complex)?
  • Is my conclusion consistent with my introduction?
  • Have I avoided bullet points, numbered lists, or headings?
  • Have I written at least 250 words?
  • Have I checked subject-verb agreement?
  • Have I checked article usage (a/an/the)?
  • Are my verb tenses consistent?
  • Is my punctuation (commas, full stops, apostrophes) correct?
  • Have I avoided informal language and contractions?
  • Is my handwriting/typing legible?
  • Have I avoided starting sentences with "And", "But", or "Because"?
  • Have I read through the essay one final time for sense?
Time Management Tip: Spend 5 min planning → 30 min writing → 5 min checking. A well-planned essay is easier to write and check. Never skip the planning stage, even under time pressure.

14. Top 15 Mistakes That Lower Your Band Score

MistakeWhy It Lowers Your ScoreHow to Fix It
Copying words from the question Penalised under Task Achievement; copied words are not counted Always paraphrase the question topic using synonyms and different structure
No clear position in an opinion essay Fails Task Achievement — examiner cannot identify your view State your opinion clearly in the introduction and maintain it throughout
Using bullet points or numbered lists IELTS requires continuous prose; lists are penalised Write in connected paragraphs; use linking words to add multiple points
Writing fewer than 250 words Automatic penalty for being under the minimum word count Always develop each point fully; practise timing so you know your pace
Overusing "Firstly, Secondly, Thirdly, Finally" Repetitive — lowers Lexical Resource and Coherence scores Vary your connectors: "Furthermore", "In addition", "Beyond this", "A further point is..."
Informal language and contractions Lowers Lexical Resource — IELTS requires formal register Avoid "don't", "can't", "a lot of", "kids", "stuff", "things". Use "do not", "cannot", "numerous", "children"
Very long paragraphs with no focus Lowers Coherence & Cohesion — ideas must be logically grouped Each paragraph should have one clear topic sentence. Aim for 80–100 words per body paragraph
Using "I think" repeatedly Weakens the argument; informal; repetitive Use "I believe", "I maintain", "In my view", "It is my contention that..."
Vague examples ("for example, in many countries") Does not demonstrate real-world knowledge; too general Use specific examples: country names, studies, statistics, real organisations
Conclusion that introduces new ideas Penalised — conclusion must summarise, not add new arguments Restate position + summarise key reasons + optional future outlook only
Memorised phrases that don't fit the topic Examiners recognise memorised openings; penalised for irrelevance Learn flexible templates and adapt them to each specific question
Subject-verb agreement errors Grammatical errors reduce GRA score Check every sentence: "The number of students IS" (not "are"); "Each person HAS" (not "have")
Inconsistent tense use Grammatical accuracy issue — reduces GRA score Decide your tense and stick to it. Check for tense shifts in your review time
Discussing irrelevant issues Lowers Task Achievement — must address the specific question asked Re-read the question after planning. Every paragraph must connect to the task prompt
Starting every sentence the same way Lowers both GRA and LR — lack of sentence variety Mix sentence starters: participial phrases, "There is/are", inverted structures, subordinate clauses

16. 10 Model Introduction Paragraphs

Study these model introductions across different essay types. Note how the question is paraphrased and how the thesis is stated.

Topic: The rise of online shopping has had a negative impact on traditional retail. Do you agree?

Model Introduction: The rapid expansion of e-commerce has profoundly transformed consumer behaviour across the globe, raising concerns about its impact on bricks-and-mortar retail. While the disruption caused by online shopping is undeniable, I disagree that its overall impact has been primarily negative, as it has created new opportunities alongside the challenges it has posed.

Topic: Some people say that music should be taught in schools. Others think children's time would be better spent on other subjects. Discuss both views and give your opinion.

Model Introduction: Whether music education deserves a place in the school curriculum alongside core academic subjects is a matter of ongoing debate among educators and policymakers. This essay will examine both the case for music education and the arguments of those who prefer to prioritise other subjects, before arguing that a balanced approach best serves the needs of students.

Topic: In many cities, public transport is inadequate. What are the causes of this problem and what measures could improve it?

Model Introduction: Inadequate public transport infrastructure remains one of the most persistent urban challenges worldwide, affecting quality of life and economic productivity for millions of city-dwellers. This essay will examine the principal factors contributing to this shortfall and propose concrete solutions that governments and urban planners could implement.

Topic: Some people believe that studying abroad is the best way to learn a new language. To what extent do you agree?

Model Introduction: Immersive language learning through international study has long been regarded as one of the most effective routes to fluency. While I broadly agree that studying abroad offers unique advantages, I would argue that it is not the only path to language mastery, and that its benefits depend heavily on how the experience is approached.
