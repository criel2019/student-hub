All 12 English Tenses – Rules, Formulas & Examples

The complete reference guide to every English tense: formation formulas, when to use them, time expressions, five examples each, and common mistakes to avoid.

Why Tenses Matter

English tenses communicate not just when something happens, but also its completeness, duration, and relationship to other events. Mastery of the 12 tenses is essential for IELTS (Writing and Speaking), TOEFL, CET-4/6, and professional communication. The 12 tenses divide neatly into three time frames (Present, Past, Future) and four aspects (Simple, Continuous, Perfect, Perfect Continuous).

Present Tenses

1Present Simple
(+) Subject + V₁ (add -s/-es for 3rd person singular)
(–) Subject + do/does + not + V₁
(?) Do/Does + Subject + V₁ ?

When to use:

  • Habits & routines – actions that happen regularly
  • Universal facts – things that are always true
  • Scheduled future events – timetables and programmes
  • Newspaper headlines – present simple for drama
  • Instructions & directions – step-by-step guides

Common time expressions: always usually often sometimes rarely/seldom never every day/week/year on Mondays

Examples:

  1. She teaches mathematics at Delhi University.
  2. The sun rises in the east and sets in the west.
  3. I usually wake up at 6 AM and go for a run.
  4. The train leaves at 9:15 tomorrow morning. (scheduled future)
  5. Water boils at 100°C at sea level. (scientific fact)
Common Mistake: Using continuous for stative verbs.
I am knowing the answer. → I know the answer.
She is liking pizza. → She likes pizza.
2Present Continuous (Present Progressive)
(+) Subject + am/is/are + V-ing
(–) Subject + am/is/are + not + V-ing
(?) Am/Is/Are + Subject + V-ing ?

When to use:

  • Action happening now – at this very moment
  • Temporary situations – not permanent states
  • Future arrangements – planned and confirmed
  • Trends & changes – with verbs like "growing", "increasing"
  • Annoying habits – with "always" for criticism

Common time expressions: now right now at the moment currently this week/month today

Examples:

  1. She is studying for her IELTS exam right now.
  2. I am staying with my cousin this week while my flat is being renovated.
  3. We are meeting the professor tomorrow at 10 AM. (arrangement)
  4. Global temperatures are rising at an alarming rate. (trend)
  5. He is always leaving his dishes in the sink! (annoying habit)
Common Mistake: Using continuous with stative verbs.
I am understanding the concept. → I understand the concept.
They are owning a house. → They own a house.
3Present Perfect
(+) Subject + have/has + V₃ (past participle)
(–) Subject + have/has + not + V₃
(?) Have/Has + Subject + V₃ ?

When to use:

  • Life experience – at any point up to now (no specific time)
  • Unfinished past – with "for" and "since"
  • Recent past – relevant to the present moment
  • Change over time – "The situation has improved..."

Common time expressions: ever never already yet just recently for + duration since + point in time

Examples:

  1. I have visited Japan three times. (life experience — no specific time)
  2. She has worked at this company since 2019. (unfinished — still works there)
  3. The government has just announced a new climate policy. (recent, relevant now)
  4. Have you ever tried skydiving? (experience question)
  5. Living standards in the region have improved dramatically over the past decade. (change)
Common Mistake: Using Present Perfect with a specific past time.
I have seen him yesterday. → I saw him yesterday.
She has graduated in 2022. → She graduated in 2022.
4Present Perfect Continuous
(+) Subject + have/has + been + V-ing
(–) Subject + have/has + not + been + V-ing
(?) Have/Has + Subject + been + V-ing ?

When to use:

  • Duration of ongoing activity – started in past, still happening
  • Repeated activity – over a period up to now
  • Visible result of recent activity – "She looks tired. She has been working all day."

Common time expressions: for + duration since + point all day/morning/week lately recently

Examples:

  1. I have been studying English for five years and I am still learning.
  2. She has been feeling unwell since Monday — she should see a doctor.
  3. The researchers have been collecting data for this study since 2021.
  4. Why are your hands dirty? — I have been repairing my bicycle.
  5. How long have you been waiting? — I have been waiting for over an hour.

Past Tenses

5Past Simple
(+) Subject + V₂ (regular: +ed; irregular: see table below)
(–) Subject + did + not + V₁
(?) Did + Subject + V₁ ?

When to use:

  • Completed past action – at a specific known time
  • Sequence of events – narrative, storytelling
  • Past habits – with "used to" or "would"
  • Historical facts

Common time expressions: yesterday last week/month/year in 1990/2010 ago when I was young in the morning

Examples:

  1. She graduated from university in 2021 and moved to London immediately afterwards.
  2. The Second World War ended in 1945.
  3. I visited my grandparents last weekend and we cooked together.
  4. Did you finish the report before the deadline?
  5. He didn't understand the lecture, so he reviewed the notes that evening.
Common Mistake: Using Past Simple instead of Present Perfect (or vice versa).
I have called him yesterday. → I called him yesterday.
Did you ever visit Paris? (asking about life experience) → Have you ever visited Paris?
6Past Continuous (Past Progressive)
(+) Subject + was/were + V-ing
(–) Subject + was/were + not + V-ing
(?) Was/Were + Subject + V-ing ?

When to use:

  • Interrupted past action – "was doing WHEN something happened"
  • Simultaneous past actions – "while...was...ing"
  • Setting the scene – background in narrative
  • Polite/tentative requests – "I was wondering if..."

Examples:

  1. I was reading a book when the phone rang. (interrupted action)
  2. While she was preparing dinner, her son was doing his homework. (simultaneous)
  3. The sun was setting, birds were singing, and the village was slowly coming to life. (scene setting)
  4. At 8 PM last night, they were watching the championship final. (specific time in past)
  5. I was wondering if you could help me with this task. (polite/tentative)
7Past Perfect
(+) Subject + had + V₃
(–) Subject + had + not + V₃
(?) Had + Subject + V₃ ?

When to use:

  • Earlier past action – completed before another past action
  • Reported speech – "He said he had finished."
  • Third conditional – "If she had studied harder, she would have passed."

Common time expressions: before after by the time already never...before when

Examples:

  1. By the time we arrived, the concert had already started. (earlier past)
  2. She had never seen snow before she moved to Canada. (experience before past reference)
  3. He told me he had submitted the application two weeks earlier. (reported speech)
  4. The doctor examined the patient after the nurse had taken his blood pressure.
  5. If they had invested in renewable energy earlier, they would have saved millions.
8Past Perfect Continuous
(+) Subject + had + been + V-ing
(–) Subject + had + not + been + V-ing
(?) Had + Subject + been + V-ing ?

When to use:

  • Duration before a past point – how long something had been happening before another event
  • Cause of a past state – explains why something was the case

Examples:

  1. She was exhausted because she had been working for 14 hours straight.
  2. By the time he received the scholarship, he had been applying for three years.
  3. The road was wet. It had been raining heavily all morning.
  4. I had been waiting for over an hour when the bus finally arrived.
  5. They had been negotiating the contract for months before both sides finally agreed.

Future Tenses

9Future Simple (will)
(+) Subject + will + V₁
(–) Subject + will + not (won't) + V₁
(?) Will + Subject + V₁ ?

When to use:

  • Predictions – about the future (no evidence, just belief)
  • Spontaneous decisions – decided at the moment of speaking
  • Promises & offers
  • Conditional clauses – "If it rains, I will stay home."

Common time expressions: tomorrow next week/year in the future soon by 2030

Examples:

  1. Experts predict that global temperatures will rise by 1.5°C by 2040. (prediction)
  2. The phone is ringing. — Don't worry, I'll answer it. (spontaneous decision)
  3. I promise I will call you as soon as I land. (promise)
  4. Will you help me move this weekend? (offer/request)
  5. If governments fail to act, biodiversity loss will accelerate dramatically.
"Will" vs "Be going to": Use "be going to" for plans/intentions already decided ("I am going to study medicine") and for predictions based on present evidence ("Look at those clouds — it is going to rain"). Use "will" for spontaneous decisions and general predictions.
10Future Continuous
(+) Subject + will + be + V-ing
(–) Subject + will + not + be + V-ing
(?) Will + Subject + be + V-ing ?

When to use:

  • Ongoing action at a future time – "This time next week, I will be flying to Tokyo."
  • Polite enquiries – about future plans
  • Anticipated events – expected to happen in the normal course of events

Examples:

  1. At 9 AM tomorrow, I will be presenting my thesis to the committee.
  2. This time next month, she will be living in New York and starting her new job.
  3. Will you be using the car tonight? (polite enquiry)
  4. In 2050, many countries will be running entirely on renewable energy.
  5. Don't call between 2 and 4 PM — I will be attending an important meeting.
11Future Perfect
(+) Subject + will + have + V₃
(–) Subject + will + not + have + V₃
(?) Will + Subject + have + V₃ ?

When to use:

  • Completed before a future time – action that will be finished before a deadline or future reference point
  • Expectation – "She will have arrived by now."

Common time expressions: by + future time by the time before

Examples:

  1. By 2030, scientists will have developed a cure for several forms of cancer.
  2. By the time you read this letter, I will have already left the country.
  3. She will have completed her PhD by the end of this academic year.
  4. Don't worry — the technician will have fixed the server before the morning shift starts.
  5. Will they have finished the construction by the time the Olympics begin?
12Future Perfect Continuous
(+) Subject + will + have + been + V-ing
(–) Subject + will + not + have + been + V-ing
(?) Will + Subject + have + been + V-ing ?

When to use:

  • Duration up to a future point – how long something will have been happening by a future time

Examples:

  1. By next June, I will have been studying English for ten years.
  2. When she retires, she will have been teaching at this school for 35 years.
  3. By the time the project launches, the team will have been working on it for two years.
  4. He will have been waiting for three hours by the time the flight lands.
  5. In 2027, they will have been married for a quarter of a century.

Dual-meaning verbs: Some verbs can be stative OR dynamic depending on meaning. "I have a car" (possess = stative) vs "I am having lunch" (eating = dynamic). "She looks tired" (seems = stative) vs "She is looking at the map" (deliberate action = dynamic).

Active vs Passive Voice

Passive voice is formed with be + past participle (V₃). Use passive when the agent (doer) is unknown, unimportant, or obvious from context. It is common in academic and formal writing.

Tense Active Form Passive Form
Present Simple Scientists discover new species. New species are discovered (by scientists).
Present Continuous They are building a new hospital. A new hospital is being built.
Present Perfect The committee has approved the proposal. The proposal has been approved.
Past Simple The company launched the product in 2022. The product was launched in 2022.
Past Continuous Workers were repairing the bridge. The bridge was being repaired.
Past Perfect The researchers had published the findings. The findings had been published.
Future Simple The government will announce the policy. The policy will be announced.
Future Perfect Engineers will have completed the project. The project will have been completed.
