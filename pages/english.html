1.4 Common Grammar Mistakes

MistakeWrongCorrectExplanation
Subject-verb agreementThe students was studying.The students were studying.Plural subject needs plural verb.
it's vs itsThe dog wagged it's tail.The dog wagged its tail."its" = possessive; "it's" = it is
their/there/they'reThere going to the exam.They're going to the exam."they're" = they are
your/you'reYour going to succeed.You're going to succeed."you're" = you are
affect vs effectThe pollution effected the crops.The pollution affected the crops."affect" = verb; "effect" = noun
then vs thanShe is smarter then him.She is smarter than him."than" for comparisons; "then" for time
who vs whomWho did you speak with?Whom did you speak with?"whom" after preposition / object position
lie vs layPlease lay down and rest.Please lie down and rest."lie" = recline (intransitive); "lay" = to place (transitive)
fewer vs lessThere are less students today.There are fewer students today."fewer" for countable; "less" for uncountable
double negativesI don't know nothing.I don't know anything.Two negatives cancel each other in standard English

Band 7+ Task 1 Tips:
  • Write at least 150 words; aim for 170–190 words.
  • Always identify the overall trend in a separate overview sentence.

Task 1 Sample Response (Line Graph)

Question: The graph below shows the number of international students enrolled in UK universities from 2000 to 2020. Summarise the information by selecting and reporting the main features, making comparisons where relevant.

The line graph illustrates changes in international student enrolment in UK universities over a twenty-year period from 2000 to 2020.

Overall, the number of international students increased substantially over the period, with a brief dip around 2012 before rising again to reach its highest point in 2020.

In 2000, approximately 200,000 international students were enrolled in UK universities. This figure climbed steadily over the following decade, reaching a peak of around 310,000 by 2010. However, between 2010 and 2014, enrolment declined noticeably, falling to roughly 270,000, possibly due to changes in visa regulations.

From 2015 onwards, international student numbers recovered strongly and rose sharply to an all-time high of approximately 450,000 in 2020. This represents a more than doubling of the 2000 figure, underscoring the growing appeal of UK higher education for international students.

Task 2 Sample Essay (Opinion — Band 7)

Topic: Some people think that university education should be free for all students. To what extent do you agree or disagree?

In today's society, the cost of higher education is a widely debated topic. While some argue that university should be fully funded by the government, I believe that a mixed funding model, combining government support with reasonable tuition fees, is a more effective and sustainable approach.

On the one hand, free university education would significantly increase access for students from low-income families. In countries such as Germany and Norway, tuition-free higher education has resulted in higher enrolment rates and reduced social inequality. Furthermore, an educated workforce benefits society as a whole, suggesting that the government has a responsibility to invest in higher education.

On the other hand, making universities entirely free places an enormous financial burden on governments, which may result in cuts to other vital public services such as healthcare. Moreover, when students pay at least a portion of their fees, they tend to take their education more seriously and make more informed choices about their courses.

In conclusion, while free university education has clear social benefits, a mixed funding system that provides government subsidies alongside manageable student contributions offers a more balanced and financially viable solution. Governments should focus on providing scholarships and low-interest loans rather than making all higher education entirely free.

(~225 words | Estimated Band: 7)

3.4 IELTS Reading Tips

  • Skimming: Read quickly to get the general idea. Do this first for each passage (2–3 minutes).
  • Scanning: Look for specific information, keywords, numbers, or names.
  • True/False/Not Given: "Not Given" means the information is simply absent — do not infer. Do not confuse with "False".
  • Matching Headings: Read the first and last sentence of each paragraph first.
  • Time management: Spend no more than 20 minutes per passage. Move on and return if stuck.
  • Paraphrasing: Questions rarely use the same words as the text — look for synonyms.

3.5 IELTS Listening Tips

  • Predict before listening: Read the questions carefully before the audio starts to anticipate key information.
  • Note-taking: Write abbreviations quickly — do not try to write full sentences.
  • Watch for spelling traps: The speaker may spell words for you — write exactly what is dictated.
  • Plural traps: "one cat" vs "three cats" — missing the plural costs a mark.
  • Distractors: Speakers often change their minds ("Actually, let's meet at 3, not 2") — wait for the final answer.
  • Section 4 is a monologue — the hardest. Focus intensely and keep up with the speaker.

6.3 PEEL Body Paragraph Structure

P – Point: State your main idea for this paragraph. ("One major benefit of learning English is greater career opportunity.")

E – Evidence: Provide facts, examples, or data. ("Studies show that bilingual employees earn 5–20% more than monolingual counterparts.")

E – Explanation: Explain how the evidence supports your point. ("This demonstrates that English proficiency directly enhances employability in the global market.")

L – Link: Connect back to the thesis or transition to the next point. ("Therefore, investing in English education is a practical strategy for career development.")

Section 11: Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

Q1: What IELTS band do I need for studying in the UK/Australia?

Most UK universities require IELTS 6.0–6.5 for undergraduate and 6.5–7.0 for postgraduate admission. Top universities like Oxford and Cambridge may require 7.0–7.5. Australian universities typically require 6.0–6.5 overall with no band below 6.0.

Q2: How long does it take to improve IELTS from 5.5 to 7?

On average, students need 200–400 hours of dedicated study to move up one full band score. Improving from 5.5 to 7.0 typically takes 6–12 months of consistent daily practice, depending on your starting level and study intensity.

Q3: Is TOEFL or IELTS better for Indian students?

Both are widely accepted. IELTS Academic is generally considered better for UK/Australia applications, while TOEFL iBT is preferred for North American universities. TOEFL is fully computer-based; IELTS speaking is face-to-face. Indian students often find the IELTS reading and writing formats more familiar due to British-style English education.

Q4: What is the passing score for CET-4?

CET-4 is scored on a scale of 710. A score of 425 is generally considered a pass, though many employers and universities prefer 500+. There is no official "pass/fail" — the score is used as a reference benchmark.

Q5: How can I improve my English speaking quickly?

The most effective methods are: (1) speak with native or fluent speakers daily, (2) shadow English podcast episodes, (3) record yourself and listen back, (4) join English debate or discussion groups online, (5) think in English throughout the day rather than translating from your mother tongue.

Q6: What is the best vocabulary learning method?

Research strongly supports spaced repetition (using apps like Anki). Learn words in context — not isolated lists. Aim for 10–20 new words per day. Review old words every 3–7 days. Study word families (e.g., analyse / analysis / analytical / analytically) to quadruple your vocabulary efficiently.

Q7: Can I use contractions in IELTS writing?

No. Contractions (it's, don't, can't) are informal and should not be used in IELTS Academic Writing Tasks 1 or 2. Always write out full forms: "it is", "do not", "cannot".

Q8: How many words should I write in IELTS Task 2?

The minimum is 250 words. Aim for 260–300 words. There is no maximum penalty, but excessively long essays (350+ words) under time pressure often contain more errors. Quality over quantity.

Q9: What is the difference between IELTS Academic and IELTS General Training?

IELTS Academic is for students applying for undergraduate or postgraduate university programmes and professional registration. The reading and writing sections use academic texts and require formal academic writing styles. IELTS General Training is for those migrating to English-speaking countries (e.g., Australia, Canada, UK) or applying for secondary education and work experience programmes. The reading passages are more practical (notices, advertisements, workplace documents) and the writing requires a letter in Task 1. The Listening and Speaking modules are identical for both versions.

Q10: How can I improve my IELTS writing score quickly?

Focus on these four areas assessed by IELTS examiners: (1) Task Achievement/Response — directly answer every part of the question; (2) Coherence and Cohesion — organise ideas logically, use a wide range of cohesive devices naturally; (3) Lexical Resource — use a variety of vocabulary accurately and avoid repeating the same words; (4) Grammatical Range and Accuracy — mix simple and complex sentences, minimise errors. The fastest improvements usually come from studying model answers, identifying their structure, and practising daily timed writing with feedback.
