1. Equations of Motion (Constant Acceleration)

When a body moves along a straight line with constant (uniform) acceleration, four kinematic equations relate displacement s, initial velocity u, final velocity v, acceleration a, and time t.

1.1 The Four Standard Equations

#EquationQuantity NOT involved
1v = u + atdisplacement s
2s = ut + ½at²final velocity v
3v² = u² + 2astime t
4sn = u + a(2n−1)/2–

sn = displacement in the n-th second (not total displacement). This is especially useful in JEE/NEET single-second questions.

1.2 Derivations

Equation 1 (v = u + at): By definition, acceleration a = (v−u)/t. Rearranging: v = u + at.

Equation 2 (s = ut + ½at²): Average velocity = (u+v)/2. s = average velocity × t = [(u+v)/2]×t. Substitute v = u+at: s = [(u+u+at)/2]×t = ut + ½at².

Equation 3 (v² = u² + 2as): From eq.1: t = (v−u)/a. Substitute in eq.2: s = u(v−u)/a + ½a×(v−u)²/a². Multiply by 2a: 2as = 2u(v−u) + (v−u)² = v²−u². Hence v² = u² + 2as.

Equation 4 (sn derivation): sn = s(up to n sec) − s(up to n−1 sec) = [un + ½an²] − [u(n−1) + ½a(n−1)²] = u + a(2n−1)/2.

1.3 Sign Convention (Very Important for JEE)

Choose a positive direction at the start of the problem. Usually: upward = positive, downward = negative for vertical problems; rightward = positive for horizontal.

  • If initial velocity and acceleration have the same sign → body speeds up.
  • If they have opposite signs → body slows down, stops, then reverses.
  • For a ball thrown upward: u = +20 m/s, a = −10 m/s² (g downward).
  • Displacement s can be negative (means the body ended up on the negative side).
  • Distance ≠ |displacement|; if the body reverses, calculate each segment separately.

1.4 Solved Examples

Example 1 – Car Accelerating from Rest

Problem: A car starts from rest and accelerates uniformly at 2 m/s². Find (a) velocity after 5 s, (b) distance covered in 5 s, (c) distance in the 5th second.

Given: u = 0, a = 2 m/s², t = 5 s

(a) v = u + at = 0 + 2×5 = 10 m/s

(b) s = ut + ½at² = 0 + ½×2×25 = 25 m

(c) s5 = u + a(2×5−1)/2 = 0 + 2×9/2 = 9 m

Check: s1=1, s2=3, s3=5, s4=7, s5=9. Sum = 25 m ✓

Example 2 – Ball Thrown Upward

Problem: A ball is thrown vertically upward with u = 20 m/s. Find (a) max height, (b) time to reach top, (c) total flight time. (g = 10 m/s²)

Given: u = +20 m/s, a = −10 m/s² (upward positive)

(a) At max height, v = 0. Using v² = u² + 2as:
0 = 400 − 20H → H = 20 m

(b) v = u + at → 0 = 20 − 10t → t = 2 s

(c) Total time = 2tup = 4 s (symmetric for same level; or: s=0 in s=20t−5t² gives t=4 s)

Example 3 – Two Trains (Relative Motion Preview)

Problem: Train A moves at 72 km/h and train B moves at 36 km/h in the same direction. Train B is 200 m ahead of A. When will A catch B, and how far does A travel?

Convert: vA = 20 m/s, vB = 10 m/s. Relative velocity of A w.r.t. B = 20−10 = 10 m/s.

Time to close 200 m gap: t = 200/10 = 20 s

Distance by A: 20×20 = 400 m

2. Kinematics Graphs

Graphs are a favourite topic in JEE and board exams. The ability to extract physical information from a graph is essential.

2.1 Position–Time (x–t) Graph

Shape of x–t graphPhysical meaning
Horizontal straight lineBody at rest (x = constant)
Straight line with +ve slopeConstant positive velocity (uniform motion)
Straight line with −ve slopeConstant negative velocity (moving backward)
Upward-opening parabolaPositive constant acceleration (starting from rest)
Curve with increasing slopeIncreasing velocity (acceleration)
Curve with decreasing slopeDecreasing velocity (deceleration)

Key rule: The slope (gradient) of the x–t graph at any point = instantaneous velocity at that point.

For uniform acceleration: x = x0 + ut + ½at² is a parabola on the x–t graph.

2.2 Velocity–Time (v–t) Graph

Shape of v–t graphPhysical meaning
Horizontal straight lineConstant velocity (zero acceleration)
Straight line with +ve slopeUniform positive acceleration
Straight line with −ve slopeUniform deceleration
Curve (concave up)Increasing acceleration
Curve (concave down)Decreasing acceleration
v crosses zero axisBody reverses direction

Slope of v–t graph = instantaneous acceleration.

Area under v–t graph = displacement (signed area; area below x-axis = negative displacement).

Total distance = sum of absolute areas of all regions (above + below axis).

2.3 Acceleration–Time (a–t) Graph

FeatureMeaning
Horizontal line at a = constantUniform acceleration
Horizontal line at a = 0Uniform velocity
Area under a–t graphChange in velocity: Δv = ∫a dt

2.4 How to Read Complex Graphs – Step-by-Step Method

Step 1: Identify axis labels and units.

Step 2: Divide the graph into regions with distinct shapes.

Step 3: For each region, find slope = velocity (if x–t) or acceleration (if v–t).

Step 4: Calculate areas (triangles, rectangles, trapezoids) for displacement.

Step 5: Note where the curve crosses zero – that is where velocity or position changes sign.

3. Projectile Motion

A projectile moves under gravity alone (no air resistance). The horizontal and vertical motions are independent. Horizontal: uniform velocity. Vertical: uniform acceleration g downward.

3.1 General Equations (Projection at angle θ with horizontal)

Initial components: ux = u cosθ, uy = u sinθ

At time t:

  • Horizontal: x = u cosθ · t    (vx = u cosθ = constant)
  • Vertical: y = u sinθ · t − ½gt²    (vy = u sinθ − gt)
  • Resultant speed: v = √(vx² + vy²)
  • Angle with horizontal: tanφ = vy/vx

3.2 Key Derived Formulas

QuantityFormulaSpecial cases
Time of flight TT = 2u sinθ/gθ=90°: T=2u/g (straight up)
Maximum height HH = u²sin²θ/(2g)θ=90°: H=u²/(2g)
Horizontal range RR = u²sin2θ/gMax R at θ=45°: Rmax=u²/g
Range identityR(θ) = R(90°−θ)e.g. 30° and 60° give same R

3.3 Equation of Trajectory

Eliminate t from x and y equations:

y = x tanθ − gx² / (2u²cos²θ)

This is a parabola opening downward. It can be rewritten as:

y = x tanθ (1 − x/R)   where R = u²sin2θ/g

3.4 Solved Examples

Example 1 – Standard Projection at 30°

Problem: A ball is projected at 20 m/s at 30° to horizontal. Find T, H, R (g=10 m/s²).

ux = 20cos30° = 20×√3/2 = 10√3 m/s  |  uy = 20sin30° = 10 m/s

T = 2×10/10 = 2 s

H = 10²/(2×10) = 5 m

R = 20²×sin60°/10 = 400×√3/2÷10 = 20√3 ≈ 34.6 m

Example 2 – Horizontal Projection from a Cliff

Problem: A stone is thrown horizontally at 15 m/s from a cliff 45 m high. Find (a) time to hit ground, (b) horizontal distance, (c) velocity on impact. (g=10)

(a) Vertical: 45 = ½×10×t² → t² = 9 → t = 3 s

(b) x = 15×3 = 45 m

(c) vx = 15 m/s; vy = 10×3 = 30 m/s; v = √(225+900) = √1125 = 15√5 ≈ 33.5 m/s

Angle: tanφ = 30/15 = 2 → φ = tan−¹(2) ≈ 63.4° below horizontal

Example 3 – Ball Thrown from Top of Building

Problem: A ball is thrown at 10 m/s horizontally from the top of a 20 m building. Find where it lands (horizontal distance from the base).

Time to fall 20 m: 20 = ½×10×t² → t = 2 s

Horizontal distance = 10×2 = 20 m from the base

Example 4 – Maximum Range Condition

Problem: A cannon can fire shells at 100 m/s. What is the maximum range on a horizontal plane? At what angle must it be fired to hit a target 500 m away?

Max range: Rmax = u²/g = 10000/10 = 1000 m at 45°

For 500 m: R = u²sin2θ/g → 500 = 10000 sin2θ/10 → sin2θ = 0.5 → 2θ = 30° or 150° → θ = 15° or 75°

Example 5 – Tricky JEE Type: River Crossing (Minimum Distance)

Problem: A river of width d flows with velocity vr. A swimmer can swim at vs in still water. Find the angle to cross in minimum distance.

For minimum distance (shortest path = perpendicular to opposite bank), the swimmer must direct his velocity upstream so that resultant is perpendicular to bank.

Let swimmer aim at angle α upstream. Resultant vertical velocity = vscosα. Drift = 0 requires: vr = vssinα

α = sin−¹(vr/vs) upstream from perpendicular. Minimum distance = d (width only, no drift).

Condition: Only possible if vs > vr.

4. Circular Motion

4.1 Angular Quantities

QuantitySymbolRelation
Angular displacementθradians; 1 rev = 2π rad
Angular velocityω = dθ/dtv = rω
Angular accelerationα = dω/dtatangential = rα
PeriodT = 2π/ωf = 1/T = ω/2π

Rotational equations (analogous to linear, for constant α):

  • ω = ω0 + αt
  • θ = ω0t + ½αt²
  • ω² = ω0² + 2αθ

4.2 Centripetal Acceleration and Force

Centripetal acceleration: ac = v²/r = rω² (directed toward centre)

Centripetal force: Fc = mv²/r = mrω²

Centripetal force is NOT a new force – it is the net inward force provided by tension, gravity, normal force, friction etc. depending on the problem.

4.3 Non-Uniform Circular Motion

Two acceleration components exist simultaneously:

  • Centripetal (radial): ac = v²/r (toward centre)
  • Tangential: at = rα = dv/dt (along tangent)
  • Net acceleration: a = √(ac² + at²)

4.4 Banking of Roads

For a banked curve of angle θ (no friction): tanθ = v²/(rg)

Optimal speed: v = √(rg tanθ)

With friction (coefficient μ):

  • Maximum safe speed: vmax = √[rg(tanθ+μ)/(1−μtanθ)]
  • Minimum safe speed: vmin = √[rg(tanθ−μ)/(1+μtanθ)]

4.5 Vertical Circular Motion

For a ball on a string of length r moving in a vertical circle:

PositionMinimum SpeedTension
Top of circlevtop = √(gr)Ttop = mv²/r − mg (= 0 at minimum)
Bottom of circlevbottom = √(5gr)Tbottom = mv²/r + mg

Derivation of vbottom: By energy conservation from top to bottom:
½mvb² = ½mvt² + mg(2r)
vb² = vt² + 4gr = gr + 4gr = 5gr → vb = √(5gr)

Tension difference: Tbottom − Ttop = 6mg (always, at same radius)

4.6 Solved Examples

Example 1 – Car on Banked Road

Problem: A road is banked at 30°. Find the optimal speed for a vehicle on a curve of radius 100 m. (g = 10)

v = √(rg tanθ) = √(100×10×tan30°) = √(1000/√3) ≈ √577 ≈ 24 m/s (86.4 km/h)

Example 2 – Ball on String in Vertical Circle

Problem: A stone of mass 0.5 kg is tied to a string of length 1 m. What is the minimum speed at the bottom to complete a vertical circle? What is tension at bottom?

Min speed at bottom: v = √(5gr) = √(5×10×1) = √50 = 5√2 ≈ 7.07 m/s

Tension at bottom: T = mvb²/r + mg = 0.5×50/1 + 0.5×10 = 25 + 5 = 30 N

Alternatively: Tbottom = Ttop + 6mg = 0 + 6×0.5×10 = 30 N ✓

Example 3 – Conical Pendulum

Problem: A conical pendulum has string length 1 m and makes angle 60° with vertical. Find period and tension.

Horizontal: T sin60° = mrω² where r = L sin60° = √3/2 m

Vertical: T cos60° = mg → T = mg/cos60° = 2mg

ω² = T sin60°/(mr) = 2g sin60°/(L sin60°) = 2g/L = 20 → ω = 2√5 rad/s

T = 2π/ω = 2π/(2√5) = π/√5 ≈ 1.41 s

5. Relative Motion

5.1 Relative Velocity Formula

Velocity of A relative to B: v⃗AB = v⃗A − v⃗B

In component form: vAB,x = vAx − vBx, and similarly for y-component.

To solve problems: make one object (usually B) appear stationary by subtracting its velocity from both objects.

5.2 Rain-Man Problem

Rain falls vertically at speed vr. Man walks at speed vm. Relative velocity of rain w.r.t. man:

v⃗rain,man = v⃗rain − v⃗man

Since vrain is downward and vman is horizontal, the relative velocity has both components.

Angle of umbrella with vertical = arctan(vm/vr).

The umbrella must be tilted toward the direction of motion.

5.3 River Crossing Problems

Let river width = d, river velocity = vr (along river), swimmer speed = vs (in still water).

Case 1 – Minimum Time: Swim perpendicular to bank (no upstream angle).

  • Time = d/vs
  • Drift downstream = vr × d/vs

Case 2 – Minimum Drift (Shortest Path): Aim upstream at angle α = sin−¹(vr/vs) from perpendicular.

  • Only possible if vs > vr.
  • If vs < vr: minimum drift angle = 90° − cos−¹(vs/vr).

5.4 Solved Examples

Example 1 – Two Cars

Problem: Car A moves east at 60 km/h, car B moves north at 80 km/h. Find velocity of B relative to A.

v⃗BA = v⃗B − v⃗A = (0,80) − (60,0) = (−60, 80)

|vBA| = √(3600+6400) = √10000 = 100 km/h

Direction: arctan(80/60) = arctan(4/3) ≈ 53.1° north of west.

Example 2 – River Minimum Time

Problem: River 100 m wide flows at 4 m/s. Swimmer can swim at 3 m/s in still water. Find minimum time to cross and drift.

Min time: swim perpendicular. t = 100/3 = 33.3 s

Drift: 4 × 100/3 = 133.3 m

Note: Since vr > vs, zero-drift crossing is not possible.

5.5 Practice Problems – Relative Motion

  1. A train moves north at 30 m/s. A man in it throws a ball east at 10 m/s. Find velocity of ball for ground observer. [Ans: √1000 ≈ 31.6 m/s, tanθ=1/3]
  2. Rain falls at 45° to vertical in still air. Man walks at 2 m/s into rain. If rain appears vertical, find rain speed. [Ans: 2 m/s]
  3. Swimmer swims at 5 m/s. River is 200 m wide, flowing at 3 m/s. Find (a) min time, (b) min drift. [Ans: 40 s; 150 m, time 50 s]
  4. Two cars A and B start from the same point. A goes north at 20 km/h and B goes east at 15 km/h. After 2 h, what is relative displacement of B from A? [Ans: 50 km]
  5. A plane flies at 300 km/h in still air. Wind blows from north at 50 km/h. What heading gives east-directed flight? [Ans: sin−¹(50/300) ≈ 9.6° north of east]

6. Newton's Laws – Applications

6.1 Newton's Three Laws

First Law (Inertia): A body remains at rest or in uniform motion unless acted on by a net external force.

Second Law: F⃗net = ma⃗. The net force equals mass times acceleration.

Third Law: For every action force, there is an equal and opposite reaction force (on a different body).

6.2 Free Body Diagram (FBD) Method

Step 1: Isolate each body.

Step 2: Draw all forces (gravity mg downward, normal N perpendicular to surface, tension T along string, friction f opposing motion).

Step 3: Choose axes conveniently (usually along and perpendicular to direction of motion).

Step 4: Apply F = ma along each axis.

Step 5: Solve simultaneous equations for unknowns (a, T, N).

6.3 Atwood Machine

Two masses m1 > m2 connected by a string over a frictionless pulley.

Acceleration: a = (m1−m2)g / (m1+m2)

Tension: T = 2m1m2g / (m1+m2)

Derivation: For m1: m1g − T = m1a. For m2: T − m2g = m2a. Add: (m1−m2)g = (m1+m2)a.

6.4 Inclined Plane

Smooth incline (no friction): a = g sinθ; Normal = mg cosθ

Rough incline (friction μ):

  • Sliding down: a = g(sinθ − μcosθ)
  • Sliding up: a = g(sinθ + μcosθ) (deceleration)
  • For no sliding: μ ≥ tanθ

6.5 Elevator Problems (Apparent Weight)

Elevator stateApparent weight
At rest or uniform velocityW' = mg (normal weight)
Accelerating upward (a)W' = m(g + a) [heavier]
Accelerating downward (a)W' = m(g − a) [lighter]
Free fall (a = g)W' = 0 (weightlessness)

6.6 Solved Examples

Example 1 – Block on Incline with Friction

Problem: A 5 kg block on a 30° incline (μ=0.3). Does it slide? If yes, find acceleration and tension if connected to a 3 kg hanging mass via frictionless pulley.

tan30° ≈ 0.577 > μ=0.3 so it SLIDES.

System: (m1=5 kg on incline, m2=3 kg hanging)

Net force = m2g − m1g(sin30° − μcos30°) [assuming m2 pulls m1 up the incline]

= 3×10 − 5×10×(0.5 − 0.3×0.866) = 30 − 5×10×0.24 = 30 − 12 = 18 N

a = 18/(5+3) = 2.25 m/s²

T = m2(g−a) = 3×(10−2.25) = 23.25 N

Example 2 – Atwood Machine

Problem: Masses 8 kg and 4 kg in an Atwood machine. Find acceleration and tension.

a = (8−4)×10/(8+4) = 40/12 = 10/3 ≈ 3.33 m/s²

T = 2×8×4×10/(8+4) = 640/12 = 160/3 ≈ 53.3 N

Example 3 – Elevator

Problem: A 60 kg person stands in an elevator. The elevator accelerates upward at 2 m/s². Find the reading on the weighing scale.

W' = m(g+a) = 60×(10+2) = 60×12 = 720 N (about 73.5 kg on scale)

Example 4 – Connected Blocks on a Table

Problem: A 10 kg block A is on a smooth table. It is connected by a string over the edge to a 4 kg hanging block B, and also connected to a 2 kg block C on the other side of the table (also hanging). Find the acceleration and tensions T1 (between C and A) and T2 (between A and B).

Net force = mBg − mCg = (4−2)×10 = 20 N (B heavier, pulls A toward B)

Total mass = 10+4+2 = 16 kg; a = 20/16 = 1.25 m/s²

T1 = mC(g+a) = 2×11.25 = 22.5 N

T2 = mB(g−a) = 4×8.75 = 35 N

8. Work, Energy & Power (Quick Reference)

These topics are closely linked to mechanics and often tested alongside Newton's laws in JEE.

QuantityFormulaUnit

Kinetic energyKE = ½mv²J
Work-energy theoremWnet = ΔKE–
Potential energy (gravity)PE = mghJ
Elastic PE (spring)PE = ½kx²J (k = spring constant)
PowerP = W/t = Fv cosθWatt (W)
Efficiencyη = Poutput/Pinput × 100%%

Conservation of Mechanical Energy: KE + PE = constant (when only conservative forces act)

Spring-mass system: vmax = Aω = A√(k/m) (at equilibrium); v=0 at extremes.

Impulse and Momentum

Linear momentum: p⃗ = mv⃗

Impulse: J⃗ = F⃗Δt = Δp⃗

Conservation of momentum: If Fnet = 0, then p⃗ = constant.

Coefficient of restitution (e): e = (relative speed after) / (relative speed before)

Perfectly elastic: e=1 (KE conserved); Perfectly inelastic: e=0 (bodies stick together)

Solved Problems – Work & Energy

Example 1 – Block Sliding Down Incline

Problem: A 5 kg block slides down a smooth 4 m long, 30° incline from rest. Find speed at bottom.

Using energy conservation: mgh = ½mv²

h = 4 sin30° = 2 m; v = √(2gh) = √(2×10×2) = √40 ≈ 6.32 m/s

Example 2 – Car Power

Problem: A car of mass 1500 kg moves at constant 72 km/h against friction force 3000 N. Find power of engine.

v = 20 m/s; At constant speed, Fengine = Ffriction = 3000 N

P = Fv = 3000×20 = 60,000 W = 60 kW
