1. Wave Motion – Basics

1.1 Types of Waves

Transverse waves: Displacement perpendicular to direction of propagation. Examples: light, string waves. Can be polarized.

Longitudinal waves: Displacement parallel to propagation. Examples: sound, compression waves in air. Cannot be polarized.

Mechanical waves need a medium; Electromagnetic waves do not.

1.2 Wave Equation

General equation for a sinusoidal transverse wave traveling in +x direction:

y(x, t) = A sin(kx − ωt + φ)

SymbolNameFormula / Value
AAmplitudeMaximum displacement
k = 2π/λWave numberrad/m
ω = 2πf = 2π/TAngular frequencyrad/s
φInitial phaseradians
v = fλ = ω/kWave speedm/s
T = 1/f = 2π/ωTime periods

For wave traveling in −x direction: y = A sin(kx + ωt + φ)

1.3 Wave Speed in Different Media

MediumFormulaSymbols

Liquid (bulk modulus B)v = √(B/ρ)ρ = density
Solid (Young's modulus Y)v = √(Y/ρ)longitudinal wave

At 0°C: vsound = 331 m/s; at 25°C: v ≈ 346 m/s

1.4 Intensity and Power

Intensity: I = P/A = ½ρω²A²v (energy per unit time per unit area)

I ∝ A² (intensity proportional to square of amplitude)

I ∝ f² (intensity proportional to square of frequency)

For a point source: I ∝ 1/r² (inverse square law)

2. Superposition & Standing Waves

2.1 Principle of Superposition

When two or more waves pass through the same medium simultaneously, the net displacement at any point is the algebraic sum of displacements due to individual waves.

This is valid for linear media (most mechanical and EM waves in normal conditions).

2.2 Standing (Stationary) Waves

Two waves with same amplitude and frequency, traveling in opposite directions:

y1 = A sin(kx−ωt),   y2 = A sin(kx+ωt)

Superposition: y = y1 + y2 = 2A sin(kx) cos(ωt)

This is NOT a traveling wave – it is a standing wave.

2.3 Modes of Vibration

String fixed at both ends (both ends are nodes):

Condition: L = nλ/2 → λn = 2L/n

fn = nv/(2L) = n/(2L) × √(T/μ)

n=1: fundamental (1st harmonic); n=2: 1st overtone (2nd harmonic); etc.

Open pipe (both ends open, antinodes at both ends):

fn = nv/(2L),   n = 1, 2, 3, … (all harmonics)

Closed pipe (one end closed = node, one end open = antinode):

Condition: L = (2n−1)λ/4

fn = (2n−1)v/(4L),   n = 1, 2, 3, … (odd harmonics only)

Fundamental of closed pipe = ½ fundamental of open pipe (same L).

Closed pipe: only odd harmonics (1st, 3rd, 5th, …)

2.4 End Correction for Pipes

In practice, the antinode at an open end is slightly beyond the pipe end. The effective length:

Leff = L + e   for each open end, where e ≈ 0.6r (r = radius of pipe)

For open pipe: Leff = L + 2e; for closed pipe: Leff = L + e

2.5 Solved Problems – Standing Waves

Example 1 – String Frequency

Problem: A string of length 0.5 m, mass 5 g, is under tension 80 N. Find the fundamental frequency and first three harmonics.

μ = 0.005/0.5 = 0.01 kg/m

v = √(T/μ) = √(80/0.01) = √8000 = 20√20 ≈ 89.4 m/s

f1 = v/(2L) = 89.4/1 = 89.4 Hz

f2 = 178.8 Hz; f3 = 268.3 Hz

Example 2 – Closed vs Open Pipe

Problem: An open pipe of length 0.5 m and a closed pipe of length 0.5 m both use v=340 m/s. Find their fundamentals and first overtones.

Open pipe: f1 = 340/(2×0.5) = 340 Hz; f2 = 680 Hz

Closed pipe: f1 = 340/(4×0.5) = 170 Hz; first overtone = 3rd harmonic = 3×170 = 510 Hz

3. Sound, Intensity & Doppler Effect

3.1 Sound Intensity Level

β = 10 log10(I/I0)   decibels (dB)

Reference intensity: I0 = 10−12 W/m² (threshold of hearing)

SourceApprox. dB
Threshold of hearing0 dB
Whisper20–30 dB
Normal conversation60 dB
City traffic80 dB
Concert/rock music110–120 dB
Threshold of pain120–130 dB

3.2 Doppler Effect

Apparent frequency when source and observer are in relative motion:

f' = f × (v ± vo) / (v ∓ vs)

CaseSign choice
Observer moves toward sourceUse +vo in numerator
Observer moves away from sourceUse −vo in numerator
Source moves toward observerUse −vs in denominator
Source moves away from observerUse +vs in denominator

Mnemonic: Approaching = frequency increases (+ in numerator, − in denominator). Receding = decreases.

3.3 Beats

When two sound waves of slightly different frequencies f1 and f2 interfere:

fbeat = |f1 − f2|

Maximum beats audible: ≈10 beats/sec (human ear perception limit).

Amplitude varies sinusoidally with frequency fbeat/2.

Tuning a musical instrument: Beat frequency decreases as you approach correct pitch.

3.4 Solved Problems – Sound & Doppler

Example 1 – Train Whistles

Problem: A train moves at 20 m/s toward a station blowing a 500 Hz whistle. vsound = 340 m/s. Find apparent frequency at station.

Observer stationary, source approaching: f' = f × v/(v−vs) = 500×340/(340−20) = 500×340/320 = 531.25 Hz

Example 2 – Both Moving

Problem: A source (f=400 Hz) moves at 30 m/s toward an observer who moves at 20 m/s away from the source. v=340 m/s.

f' = 400 × (340−20)/(340−30) = 400×320/310 = 412.9 Hz

Example 3 – Intensity Level

Problem: The intensity at 10 m from a point source is 10−6 W/m². Find (a) sound level in dB, (b) intensity at 20 m.

(a) β = 10 log(10−6/10−12) = 10 log(10&sup6;) = 10×6 = 60 dB

(b) I ∝ 1/r²: I20 = 10−6×(10/20)² = 2.5×10−7 W/m²

Example 4 – Beats

Problem: A tuning fork of 256 Hz produces 4 beats/s with another fork. When wax is applied to the second fork, beats become 2/s. Find frequency of second fork.

Initial: second fork is 256±4 = 252 Hz or 260 Hz.

Wax reduces frequency. If it was 260: after wax, gets closer to 256 → fewer beats. → 260 Hz.

If it was 252: after wax, goes further from 256 → more beats. Not consistent.

Answer: Second fork = 260 Hz

Example 5 – Resonance in Closed Pipe

Problem: A closed pipe resonates at 340 Hz (fundamental). Find (a) length (b) next resonant frequency. v=340 m/s.

(a) f1 = v/(4L) → L = v/(4f) = 340/(4×340) = 0.25 m

(b) Next resonance for closed pipe = 3rd harmonic: 3×340 = 1020 Hz

4. Ray Optics – Reflection & Mirrors

4.1 Laws of Reflection

  1. Incident ray, normal, and reflected ray are coplanar.
  2. Angle of incidence = Angle of reflection (both measured from normal).

These hold for both plane and curved mirrors.

4.2 Sign Convention (New Cartesian)

  • All distances measured from the pole (center) of the mirror/lens.
  • Distances in the direction of incident light (usually left to right): positive.
  • Distances opposite to incident light: negative.
  • Heights above principal axis: positive; below: negative.
  • For mirrors: incident light comes from the left; object is in front of mirror (left side = negative u).

4.3 Mirror Formula and Magnification

1/v + 1/u = 1/f = 2/R

f = focal length = R/2 (R = radius of curvature)

Concave mirror: f is negative (using sign convention: f = −|f|)

Convex mirror: f is positive (f = +|f|)

Linear magnification: m = −v/u = h'/h

Mirror typeImage naturem sign
Concave, u > 2fReal, inverted, diminishedm: negative, |m|<1
Concave, u = 2fReal, inverted, same sizem = −1
Concave, f < u < 2fReal, inverted, magnifiedm: negative, |m|>1
Concave, u < fVirtual, erect, magnifiedm: positive, |m|>1
Convex (all positions)Virtual, erect, diminishedm: positive, |m|<1

4.4 Solved Problems – Mirrors

Example 1 – Concave Mirror

Problem: An object is placed 20 cm in front of a concave mirror of focal length 15 cm. Find image position, nature and magnification.

u = −20 cm, f = −15 cm (concave, sign convention)

1/v = 1/f − 1/u = 1/(−15) − 1/(−20) = −1/15 + 1/20 = (−4+3)/60 = −1/60

v = −60 cm (real, in front of mirror)

m = −v/u = −(−60)/(−20) = −3 → real, inverted, 3x magnified

Example 2 – Convex Mirror

Problem: A convex mirror has f = +20 cm. Object is at 30 cm. Find image.

u = −30 cm, f = +20 cm

1/v = 1/f − 1/u = 1/20 − 1/(−30) = 1/20 + 1/30 = (3+2)/60 = 5/60 = 1/12

v = +12 cm (virtual, behind mirror)

m = −v/u = −12/(−30) = +0.4 (erect, diminished)

5. Refraction, Lenses & Prisms

5.1 Snell's Law of Refraction

n1 sinθ1 = n2 sinθ2

Refractive index: n = c/v = speed of light in vacuum / speed in medium

When going from medium 1 to 2: n1 sinθ1 = n2 sinθ2

Optical density and speed: Higher n → slower light → bends toward normal when entering.

Cauchy's equation: n = A + B/λ² (dispersion – shorter λ has higher n)

5.2 Total Internal Reflection (TIR)

TIR occurs when light travels from denser to rarer medium and angle of incidence exceeds the critical angle C:

sin C = n2/n1   (where n1 > n2)

For glass (n=1.5) to air: sin C = 1/1.5 = 2/3 → C = 41.8°

For diamond (n=2.42): C = 24.4° (very small – explains brilliance)

Applications: optical fibers, endoscopes, diamond brilliance, mirage, prism periscopes.

5.3 Refraction at Spherical Surfaces

For refraction from medium n1 to n2 at spherical surface of radius R:

n2/v − n1/u = (n2 − n1)/R

Sign convention: same as mirrors. R positive if center of curvature is on transmission side.

5.4 Thin Lens Formula

1/v − 1/u = 1/f

Lens Maker's Equation:

1/f = (n − 1)(1/R1 − 1/R2)

n = refractive index of lens material; R1, R2 = radii of curvature of the two surfaces.

Power: P = 1/f(m)   Unit: Dioptre (D)

Magnification: m = v/u = f/(f+u) = (f−v)/f

Lenses in contact: 1/f = 1/f1 + 1/f2 + … (P = P1 + P2)

5.5 Types of Lenses – Image Summary

Convex LensObject positionImage
—u > 2f (beyond C)Real, inverted, diminished, between F and C
—u = 2f (at C)Real, inverted, same size, at C
—f < u < 2fReal, inverted, magnified, beyond C
—u = fImage at infinity
—u < fVirtual, erect, magnified, same side as object

Concave lens: Always produces virtual, erect, diminished image on same side as object.

5.6 Prism

Deviation: δ = i1 + i2 − A

where A = apex angle, i1 = angle of incidence, i2 = angle of emergence.

Also: r1 + r2 = A (angles of refraction at two surfaces sum to apex angle)

Minimum deviation (Dm): At Dm, i1 = i2 = i and r1 = r2 = A/2 (symmetrical path).

n = sin[(A + Dm)/2] / sin(A/2)

Thin prism (δ = (n−1)A): Valid for small apex angles.

Dispersion: White light splits into spectrum because n depends on λ.

Angular dispersion: θ = (nv − nr)A

Dispersive power: ω = (nv − nr)/(ny − 1)

5.7 Solved Problems – Refraction & Lenses

Example 1 – Lens Image

Problem: A convex lens (f=20 cm). Object at 30 cm. Find image position and magnification.

u = −30 cm, f = +20 cm (convex lens, sign convention)

1/v = 1/f + 1/u = 1/20 + 1/(−30) = 1/20 − 1/30 = (3−2)/60 = 1/60

v = +60 cm (real, on other side)

m = v/u = 60/(−30) = −2 (inverted, 2x magnified)

Example 2 – Combination of Lenses

Problem: A convex lens of f = +40 cm and a concave lens of f = −20 cm are in contact. Find combined focal length and power.

1/f = 1/40 + 1/(−20) = 1/40 − 2/40 = −1/40

f = −40 cm (concave combination)

P = 100/f(cm) = 100/(−40) = −2.5 D

Example 3 – Critical Angle and TIR

Problem: A glass-water interface. nglass=1.5, nwater=1.33. Find critical angle for TIR.

sin C = nwater/nglass = 1.33/1.5 = 0.887

C = sin−1(0.887) ≈ 62.4°

Light in glass hitting the interface at more than 62.4° undergoes TIR.

Example 4 – Prism Minimum Deviation

Problem: A prism of apex angle 60° has minimum deviation 30°. Find refractive index.

n = sin[(A+Dm)/2] / sin(A/2) = sin[(60+30)/2] / sin(30)

= sin 45° / sin 30° = (√2/2)/(1/2) = √2 ≈ 1.414

Example 5 – Lens Maker's Equation

Problem: A biconvex lens has both radii = 20 cm and n=1.5. Find focal length.

1/f = (n−1)(1/R1 − 1/R2) = (1.5−1)(1/20 − 1/(−20)) = 0.5×(1/20+1/20) = 0.5×2/20 = 1/20

f = 20 cm

6. Wave Optics

6.1 Young's Double Slit Experiment (YDSE)

This is the most important topic in wave optics for JEE and NEET.

Setup: Two slits S1 and S2, separation d. Screen at distance D.

Path difference for a point P at height y from center:

Δ = y × d / D   (for D >> d)

ConditionPath difference ΔPosition y
Bright fringe (constructive)Δ = nλ (n=0,±1,±2,...)yn = nλD/d
Dark fringe (destructive)Δ = (2n−1)λ/2yn = (2n−1)λD/(2d)

Fringe width (spacing between adjacent bright fringes):

β = λD / d

Note: fringe width is the same for bright and dark fringes, and equals β.

6.2 Intensity in YDSE

Phase difference δ corresponds to path difference Δ by: δ = 2πΔ/λ

General intensity at phase difference δ:

I = I1 + I2 + 2√(I1I2) cosδ

For equal slits I1 = I2 = I0:

I = 4I0 cos²(δ/2)

Imax = 4I0 (when δ = 0, 2π, 4π...)

Imin = 0 (when δ = π, 3π...)

Ratio: Imax/Imin = (√I1 + √I2)² / (√I1 − √I2)²

6.3 Coherence

Coherent sources: Sources with a constant phase difference. Required for sustained interference.

Temporal coherence: Related to monochromaticity of source.

Spatial coherence: Related to size of source.

Laser light is highly coherent. Sunlight is incoherent for interference.

In YDSE, coherence is achieved by deriving both beams from the same source through two slits.

6.4 Single Slit Diffraction

Diffraction of light around a single slit of width a:

Minima (dark fringes):

a sinθ = nλ   (n = ±1, ±2, ±3, …)

Central maximum: Width = 2λ/a (angular); linear width = 2λD/a

Secondary maxima: at a sinθ = (2n+1)λ/2

Each secondary maximum is about 1/22 of the intensity of the central maximum.

Width of secondary maxima = λ/a (half the central maximum).

6.5 Resolving Power

Rayleigh criterion: Two sources are just resolved when the principal maximum of one falls on the first minimum of the other.

Minimum angular resolution: θmin = 1.22λ/D (D = aperture)

Telescope: Resolving power = D/(1.22λ)

Microscope: Limit of resolution = 0.61λ/n sinα (n sinα = numerical aperture)

6.6 Polarization

Malus's Law: When polarized light of intensity I0 passes through a polarizer at angle θ to polarization axis:

I = I0 cos²θ

Brewster's Angle (iB):

tan iB = n2/n1

At Brewster's angle, the reflected ray is completely plane-polarized (perpendicular to plane of incidence). The reflected and refracted rays are perpendicular: iB + r = 90°.

For glass (n=1.5): iB = arctan(1.5) ≈ 56.3°

Unpolarized light: When passed through a polarizer, intensity becomes I0/2.

Polarization methods: Reflection (Brewster's angle), scattering, double refraction (birefringence), using polaroids.

6.7 Solved Problems – Wave Optics

Example 1 – YDSE Fringe Width

Problem: In YDSE, d=0.5 mm, D=100 cm, λ=500 nm. Find fringe width and position of 3rd bright fringe.

β = λD/d = 500×10−9×1/(0.5×10−3) = 500×10−9/5×10−4 = 10−3 m = 1 mm

3rd bright fringe: y3 = 3β = 3 mm

Example 2 – Intensity at a Point

Problem: In YDSE with equal slits, what is the intensity at a point where path difference = λ/3? (Imax = I)

δ = 2π×(λ/3)/λ = 2π/3

I = Imax×cos²(δ/2) = I×cos²(π/3) = I×(0.5)² = I/4

Example 3 – YDSE with Changed Medium

Problem: YDSE setup is submerged in water (n=1.33). How does fringe width change?

λwater = λair/n = λ/1.33

βwater = λwaterD/d = βair/1.33

Fringe width decreases by factor 1.33

Example 4 – Diffraction Central Maximum Width

Problem: Light of 600 nm falls on a slit 0.2 mm wide. Screen at 1.5 m. Find width of central maximum.

Width = 2λD/a = 2×600×10−9×1.5/(0.2×10−3)

= 2×600×10−9×1.5/2×10−4 = 1800×10−9/10−4 = 9×10−3 m = 9 mm

Example 5 – Malus's Law

Problem: Polarized light of intensity 100 W/m² passes through a polaroid at 30°. What emerges?

I = I0cos²θ = 100×cos²30° = 100×(3/4) = 75 W/m²

Example 6 – Brewster's Angle

Problem: Light from water (n1=1.33) hits glass (n2=1.5). Find Brewster's angle.

tan iB = n2/n1 = 1.5/1.33 = 1.128

iB = arctan(1.128) ≈ 48.4°

7. Practice Problems

Wave Motion & Sound:

  1. A string 2 m long has mass 20 g under 50 N tension. Find wave speed and fundamental frequency. [Ans: v=70.7 m/s, f=17.7 Hz]
  2. A closed organ pipe 0.3 m long. v=330 m/s. Find fundamental and first two overtones. [Ans: 275 Hz, 825 Hz, 1375 Hz]
  3. Two tuning forks produce 5 beats/s. One has 300 Hz. Find possible frequencies of other. [Ans: 295 or 305 Hz]
  4. An ambulance horn (f=500 Hz) approaches at 30 m/s. v=340 m/s. Find apparent frequency. [Ans: 548.4 Hz]
  5. Sound intensity at 5 m from source is 10−5 W/m². Find level in dB and intensity at 10 m. [Ans: 70 dB; 2.5×10−6 W/m²]

Ray Optics:

  1. Concave mirror f=10 cm. Object at 15 cm. Find image. [Ans: v=−30 cm, real, inverted, m=−2]
  2. A convex lens of f=25 cm forms an image at 75 cm from lens. Find object distance. [Ans: u=−37.5 cm]
  3. A glass slab (n=1.5, thickness=3 cm). Find lateral shift for 45° incidence. [Ans: use shift formula ≈1.13 cm]
  4. Two lenses +20 D and −8 D in contact. Find f and nature. [Ans: P=+12 D, f=8.33 cm, converging]
  5. Critical angle for diamond-air is 24.4°. Find n of diamond. [Ans: n=1/sin24.4° ≈2.42]

Wave Optics:

  1. YDSE: λ=600 nm, d=0.3 mm, D=0.8 m. Find fringe width and 5th dark fringe position. [Ans: β=1.6 mm; y5=7.2 mm]
  2. In YDSE, path diff = 2.5λ. Is it bright or dark? Find intensity ratio to maximum. [Ans: dark; I=0]
  3. Two coherent sources have intensities in ratio 4:1. Find Imax/Imin. [Ans: (2+1)²/(2−1)² = 9]
  4. Slit of width 0.1 mm, screen 2 m, λ=500 nm. Find 2nd dark fringe position. [Ans: y=2λD/a=20 mm]
