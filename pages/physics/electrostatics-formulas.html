1. Coulomb's Law

1.1 Statement and Formula

The force between two point charges q1 and q2 separated by distance r in vacuum is:

F = kq1q2 / r² = q1q2 / (4πε0r²)

ConstantValueUnits
k (Coulomb's constant)9 × 10&sup9;N·m²/C²
ε0 (permittivity of free space)8.854 × 10−¹²C²/(N·m²)
k = 1/(4πε0)––

In a medium with dielectric constant K (relative permittivity): Fmedium = Fvacuum/K

1.2 Vector Form

Force on q1 due to q2: F⃗12 = kq1q2 r̂21 / r²

where r̂21 is the unit vector from q2 to q1.

Sign convention:

  • Same signs (++ or −−): F is repulsive (positive in the direction away from q2)
  • Opposite signs (+−): F is attractive (negative, toward q2)

1.3 Superposition Principle

The total force on a charge due to multiple charges equals the vector sum of forces due to each charge individually. Each pairwise interaction is independent.

F⃗total = F⃗1 + F⃗2 + F⃗3 + …

1.4 Comparison: Coulomb vs Gravity

Depends on mediumYes (ε changes)No

1.5 Solved Examples

Example 1 – Force between Two Charges

Problem: Two charges +4μC and −2μC are placed 0.3 m apart. Find the force between them. Is it attractive or repulsive?

F = kq1q2/r² = 9×10&sup9; × 4×10−&sup6; × 2×10−&sup6; / 0.09

= 9×10&sup9; × 8×10−¹² / 0.09 = 72×10−³ / 0.09 = 0.8 N, attractive

Example 2 – Three Charges in a Line

Problem: Charges +q, −q, +q are placed at x=0, 0.1 m, 0.2 m. Find net force on the middle charge.

Force from left (+q on −q): FL = kq²/(0.1)² = 100kq² toward left ←

Force from right (+q on −q): FR = kq²/(0.1)² = 100kq² toward right →

Net force = 0 (equal and opposite). Wait – both are attractive toward the respective +q charges.

FL is toward left (+q at x=0), FR is toward right (+q at x=0.2 m).

Net force = 0 by symmetry.

Example 3 – Charge in a Medium

Problem: Two charges in water (εr = 80) are 0.5 m apart. The force is 0.01 N. What would the force be in vacuum?

Fvacuum = εr × Fmedium = 80 × 0.01 = 0.8 N

Example 4 – Equilibrium of Charges

Problem: Charges +9μC and +4μC are 1 m apart. Where should a third charge be placed for equilibrium? What is the sign?

Let charge be at distance x from +9μC (and 1−x from +4μC).

For zero force: 9/x² = 4/(1−x)² → 3/x = 2/(1−x) → 3(1−x) = 2x → x = 3/5 = 0.6 m from +9μC

The charge must be negative to be attracted by both. Its magnitude is arbitrary.

2. Electric Field (E⃗)

2.1 Definition and Basic Formula

Electric field at a point = force per unit positive test charge:

E⃗ = F⃗ / q0

Due to a point charge Q at distance r:

E = kQ / r² = Q / (4πε0r²)

Direction: away from Q if Q is positive; toward Q if Q is negative.

Superposition: E⃗total = ΣE⃗i (vector sum of fields due to all charges)

2.2 Electric Field Due to Various Charge Distributions

DistributionFormulaDirection
Point charge QE = kQ/r²Radially outward (if Q>0)
Infinite line charge (λ = charge/length)E = λ/(2πε0r)Perpendicular to wire, outward
Infinite plane sheet (σ = charge/area)E = σ/(2ε0)Normal to sheet, outward
Uniformly charged sphere (outside, r>R)E = kQ/r²Radially outward
Uniformly charged sphere (inside, r<R)E = kQr/R³ = ρr/(3ε0)Radially outward
Conducting sphere (inside)E = 0–

2.3 Electric Dipole

An electric dipole consists of charges +q and −q separated by distance 2a.

Dipole moment: p = q × 2a (vector from −q to +q)

PositionElectric FieldDirection
Axial point (on axis, at distance r)E = 2kp/r³  (r >> a)Along p⃗ (same direction as dipole)
Equatorial point (perpendicular bisector, at r)E = kp/r³  (r >> a)Opposite to p⃗

Torque on dipole in uniform field: τ = p×E = pE sinθ

Potential energy of dipole: U = −p⃗·E⃗ = −pE cosθ

Stable equilibrium: θ=0 (p parallel to E); Unstable: θ=180°

2.4 Electric Field Lines – Rules

  • Start on positive charges; end on negative charges.
  • Number of lines is proportional to magnitude of charge.
  • Field lines never intersect (if they did, two directions at one point – impossible).
  • Tangent to a field line at any point gives direction of E⃗ at that point.
  • Closer lines = stronger field; farther apart = weaker field.
  • Field lines are perpendicular to equipotential surfaces.
  • Inside a conductor: E = 0, so no field lines inside.

2.5 Solved Examples

Example 1 – E at a Point due to Two Charges

Problem: Charges +3μC and −3μC are 0.4 m apart. Find E at the midpoint.

Distance from each charge to midpoint = 0.2 m

E1 = 9×10&sup9;×3×10−&sup6;/0.04 = 675,000 N/C (away from +, toward mid)

E2 = 675,000 N/C (away from mid, toward −, i.e. same direction as E1)

Both fields point in the same direction (from + to −): Etotal = 1.35 × 10&sup6; N/C

Example 2 – Dipole Axial and Equatorial Fields

Problem: A dipole has p = 4×10−&sup8; C·m. Find E at an axial point 0.3 m away and at an equatorial point 0.3 m away.

Eaxial = 2kp/r³ = 2×9×10&sup9;×4×10−&sup8;/0.027 = 2×9×10&sup9;×4×10−&sup8;/0.027

= 720/0.027 ≈ 26,667 N/C (along dipole direction)

Eequatorial = kp/r³ = half of above = 13,333 N/C (opposite to dipole direction)

Example 3 – E due to Infinite Wire

Problem: An infinitely long wire has linear charge density 2×10−&sup6; C/m. Find E at 0.05 m from the wire.

E = λ/(2πε0r) = 2×10−&sup6;/(2π×8.854×10−¹²×0.05)

= 2×10−&sup6; / (2.79×10−¹²) ≈ 7.19×10&sup5; N/C

3. Gauss's Law

3.1 Statement

ΦE = ∮E⃗·dA⃗ = Qenc / ε0

The total electric flux through any closed surface (Gaussian surface) equals the total enclosed charge divided by ε0.

Key: The shape of the Gaussian surface does not matter – only the enclosed charge matters.

3.2 Application 1 – Uniformly Charged Sphere (radius R, total charge Q)

Outside (r > R): Choose spherical Gaussian surface of radius r.

E × 4πr² = Q/ε0 → E = kQ/r² (same as point charge)

Inside (r < R, non-conductor): Enclosed charge = Q(r³/R³)

E × 4πr² = Qr³/(R³ε0) → E = kQr/R³ (linear increase)

Inside conductor: E = 0 always. Excess charge resides on surface.

3.3 Application 2 – Infinite Line Charge

Choose a cylindrical Gaussian surface of length L and radius r around the wire.

Flux through ends = 0 (E perpendicular to area). Flux through curved surface = E×2πrL.

By Gauss: E×2πrL = λL/ε0 → E = λ/(2πε0r)

3.4 Application 3 – Infinite Plane Sheet

Choose a cylindrical Gaussian surface (pill-box) with cross-section A, half on each side of the sheet.

Flux = 2EA (both faces). Enclosed charge = σA.

2EA = σA/ε0 → E = σ/(2ε0) (each side; field points away from +sheet)

For two parallel plates (++ and −−, a capacitor):

  • Between plates: E = σ/ε0 (fields add up)
  • Outside plates: E = 0 (fields cancel)

3.5 Solved Problems – Gauss's Law

Example 1 – Hollow Charged Sphere

Problem: A hollow conducting sphere of radius 10 cm has charge +5μC. Find E at (a) r=5 cm, (b) r=15 cm.

(a) r=5 cm (inside conductor): E = 0

(b) r=15 cm (outside): E = kQ/r² = 9×10&sup9;×5×10−&sup6;/(0.15)² = 45000/0.0225 = 2×10&sup6; N/C

Example 2 – Flux through a Cube

Problem: A charge of 8×10−&sup9; C is placed at the centre of a cube. Find total electric flux through the cube.

Φ = Q/ε0 = 8×10−&sup9; / 8.854×10−¹² ≈ 904 N·m²/C

Flux through each face = 904/6 ≈ 150.6 N·m²/C

Example 3 – E Inside a Non-conducting Sphere

Problem: A non-conducting sphere of radius 0.1 m has uniform charge density ρ = 10−&sup6; C/m³. Find E at r = 0.05 m.

E = ρr/(3ε0) = 10−&sup6; × 0.05 / (3 × 8.854×10−¹²)

= 5×10−&sup8; / (2.66×10−¹¹) ≈ 1882 N/C

4. Electric Potential & Potential Energy

4.1 Electric Potential (V)

Definition: Work done per unit positive charge to bring it from infinity to that point.

V = W/q0 = kQ/r

V is a scalar quantity. Unit: Volt (V) = Joule/Coulomb.

For multiple charges: Vtotal = V1 + V2 + V3 + … (algebraic sum, not vector)

Relation to E: E = −dV/dr (E points from high V to low V)

In 3D: E⃗ = −∇V = −(∂V/∂x, ∂V/∂y, ∂V/∂z)

4.2 Equipotential Surfaces

  • All points on an equipotential surface have the same potential.
  • E⃗ is always perpendicular to equipotential surfaces.
  • No work is done moving a charge along an equipotential surface.
  • For point charge: equipotentials are concentric spheres.
  • For uniform field: equipotentials are planes perpendicular to field.
  • Two equipotential surfaces never cross each other.

4.3 Electric Potential Energy

Potential energy of two charges q1 and q2 at distance r:

U = kq1q2 / r

For a system of multiple charges: U = ½ ΣiΣj≠i kqiqj/rij

(The factor ½ avoids counting each pair twice)

Work done by field when charge q moves from A to B: W = q(VA − VB)

Work done against field (by external agent): Wext = q(VB − VA)

4.4 Potential due to a Dipole

At a point making angle θ with the dipole axis, at distance r (r >> a):

V = kp cosθ / r²

On axial line (θ=0): V = kp/r²

On equatorial line (θ=90°): V = 0

4.5 Solved Examples

Example 1 – Potential at a Point

Problem: Find potential at a point equidistant (0.2 m) from charges +4μC, +2μC, and −6μC.

V = k(q1+q2+q3)/r = 9×10&sup9;×(4+2−6)×10−&sup6;/0.2 = 9×10&sup9;×0/0.2 = 0 V

Example 2 – Work Done

Problem: How much work is done in bringing a charge of +3μC from infinity to a point where V = 200 V?

W = qΔV = q(Vfinal − Vinitial) = 3×10−&sup6; × (200 − 0) = 6×10−&sup4; J

Example 3 – Potential Energy of a System

Problem: Three charges +1μC, +2μC, +3μC are at the vertices of an equilateral triangle of side 0.2 m. Find total PE.

Three pairs: U12 = k×1×2/0.2 ×10−¹² = 9×10&sup9;×2×10−¹²/0.2 = 0.09 J

U13 = 9×10&sup9;×3×10−¹²/0.2 = 0.135 J

U23 = 9×10&sup9;×6×10−¹²/0.2 = 0.27 J

Utotal = 0.09 + 0.135 + 0.27 = 0.495 J

Example 4 – E from V Formula

Problem: The electric potential in a region is V = 3x² − 4y. Find E⃗.

Ex = −∂V/∂x = −6x

Ey = −∂V/∂y = −(−4) = +4

Ez = 0

E⃗ = (−6x) x̂ + 4 ŷ

5. Capacitors

5.1 Definition and Basic Formulas

Capacitance: C = Q/V (Farads = C/V)

Parallel plate capacitor: C = ε0A/d (area A, separation d, vacuum)

With dielectric (dielectric constant κ): C = κε0A/d

Spherical capacitor (inner r1, outer r2): C = 4πε0r1r2/(r2−r1)

Cylindrical capacitor (length L): C = 2πε0L / ln(r2/r1)

Isolated sphere (in vacuum): C = 4πε0R

5.2 Combinations of Capacitors

ConnectionEquivalent CapacitanceWhat is same?
Series1/Ceq = 1/C1 + 1/C2 + …Charge Q on each
ParallelCeq = C1 + C2 + …Voltage V across each

For two capacitors in series: Ceq = C1C2/(C1+C2)

5.3 Energy Stored in a Capacitor

U = Q²/(2C) = CV²/2 = QV/2

Energy density (energy per unit volume) between the plates:

u = ½ε0E²

This formula is valid for any electric field in any region.

5.4 Effect of Dielectric on a Capacitor

This table is a JEE favourite. The key difference is whether the battery is connected or not.

5.5 Charging Through a Resistor (RC Circuit)

When capacitor C charges through resistance R from voltage V0:

Q(t) = Q0(1 − e−t/RC) where Q0 = CV0

VC(t) = V0(1 − e−t/RC)

I(t) = (V0/R) e−t/RC

Time constant: τ = RC (at t=τ, charge reaches 63.2% of max)

Discharging: Q(t) = Q0e−t/RC

5.6 Solved Problems

Example 1 – Equivalent Capacitance Network

Problem: Three capacitors 2μF, 3μF, 6μF are connected in series. Find equivalent capacitance. Then these are connected in parallel with a 4μF capacitor. Find final C.

Series: 1/Cs = 1/2 + 1/3 + 1/6 = 3/6 + 2/6 + 1/6 = 6/6 = 1 → Cs = 1μF

Parallel with 4μF: Ctotal = 1 + 4 = 5μF

Example 2 – Energy Stored

Problem: A 10μF capacitor is charged to 100 V. Find energy stored.

U = CV²/2 = 10×10−&sup6; × 10000/2 = 0.05 J = 50 mJ

Example 3 – Dielectric Inserted (Battery Disconnected)

Problem: A parallel plate capacitor (C=5μF) is charged to 200 V. Battery is disconnected. A dielectric κ=4 is inserted. Find new V, Q, C, U.

Q = CV = 5×10−&sup6;×200 = 10−³ C = 1000μC (unchanged)

C' = κC = 4×5 = 20μF

V' = Q/C' = 10−³/20×10−&sup6; = 50 V

U = Q²/(2C') = (10−³)²/(2×20×10−&sup6;) = 10−&sup6;/40×10−&sup6; = 0.025 J

Original U = CV²/2 = 5×10−&sup6;×40000/2 = 0.1 J. Ratio = 1/4 = 1/κ ✓

Example 4 – Charge Redistribution

Problem: A 4μF capacitor charged to 100 V is connected in parallel with an uncharged 6μF capacitor. Find final voltage and charges.

Initial charge Q = 4×100 = 400μC. After connection, charge distributes: Vf = Q/Ctotal = 400/10 = 40 V

Q4 = 4×40 = 160μC; Q6 = 6×40 = 240μC

Energy lost = ½×4×104 − ½×10×1600 = 20000 − 8000 = 12000μJ (lost to spark/heat)

Example 5 – Force Between Capacitor Plates

Problem: A parallel plate capacitor has plate area 100 cm² and separation 1 mm. Voltage = 500 V. Find force between plates.

E = V/d = 500/10−³ = 5×10&sup5; V/m; σ = ε0E = 8.854×10−¹²×5×10&sup5; = 4.43×10−&sup6; C/m²

F = σ²A/(2ε0) = (ε0E)²×A/(2ε0) = ½ε0E²A

= ½×8.854×10−¹²×(5×10&sup5;)²×10−² = ½×8.854×10−¹²×2.5×1011×10−² = 0.011 N

Example 6 – RC Charging

Problem: In an RC circuit, R=1MΩ, C=2μF, V0=10 V. Find (a) time constant, (b) charge at t=2τ, (c) current at t=0.

(a) τ = RC = 10&sup6;×2×10−&sup6; = 2 s

(b) Q0 = CV0 = 20μC; Q(2τ) = 20(1−e−2) = 20(1−0.135) = 17.3μC

(c) I0 = V0/R = 10/10&sup6; = 10μA

5b. Conductors, Earthing & Van de Graaff

Properties of a Conductor in Electrostatic Equilibrium

  • E = 0 inside a conductor. Any free charges redistribute on the surface so the net internal field vanishes.
  • E = σ/ε0 just outside the surface (twice the sheet formula because field exists only on one side).
  • The surface is always an equipotential in equilibrium.
  • Charge resides entirely on the outer surface of a hollow conductor.
  • On an irregularly shaped conductor, charge density is highest where curvature is greatest (sharp points).

Electrostatic Shielding

A hollow conductor shields its interior from external electric fields. No field penetrates inside. This is the principle behind the Faraday cage.

Conversely, if a charge is placed inside a hollow conductor, it induces equal and opposite charges on the inner surface. The outer surface carries the same charge as the object inside.

Earthing (Grounding) a Conductor

When a charged conductor is connected to the earth (ground), charge flows between the conductor and the earth until the conductor's potential becomes zero.

If a positive charge is brought near an isolated conductor and the conductor is then grounded (while the inducing charge is still present), negative charges flow from ground; when the ground wire is removed first and then the inducing charge is removed, the conductor retains a net negative charge.

Van de Graaff Generator

A machine that builds up very high potential (up to several million volts) on a large hollow metallic sphere.

Principle: A moving belt carries charge from a lower brush to the inside of the sphere. Because the inside of a conductor has no field, any charge placed inside immediately moves to the outer surface, accumulating indefinitely.

Maximum potential: Limited by air breakdown (dielectric strength of air ≈ 3×10&sup6; V/m). For a sphere of radius R: Vmax = 3×10&sup6;×R volts.

Application: Accelerating charged particles for nuclear physics experiments.

Electric Flux Detailed Treatment

Electric flux through a surface element dA:

dΦ = E⃗ · dA⃗ = E dA cosθ

where θ is the angle between E⃗ and the area vector (outward normal).

For a uniform field and flat surface of area A: Φ = EA cosθ

Total flux out of a closed surface = Qenc/ε0 (Gauss's Law)

Sign convention for flux: Outward flux is positive; inward is negative.

Solved Problems – Conductors & Flux

Example 1 – Charge Distribution on Concentric Spheres

Problem: A solid conducting sphere of radius 5 cm has charge +4μC. It is surrounded by a concentric conducting shell of inner radius 10 cm and outer radius 12 cm with charge −2μC. Find the charge on each surface.

Inner surface of shell: −4μC (induced by the +4μC sphere)

Outer surface of shell: total shell charge + inner surface = −2 + 4 = +2μC

E field between the two conductors (5 cm < r < 10 cm): E = kQ/r² = k(4×10−6)/r²

E field outside shell (r > 12 cm): E = k(4−2)×10−6/r² = k(2×10−6)/r²

Example 2 – Maximum Potential of a Sphere

Problem: A Van de Graaff sphere has radius 0.5 m. What is the maximum charge it can hold before air breaks down?

Emax = 3×10&sup6; V/m at the surface; E = kQ/R² → Q = EmaxR²/k

Q = 3×10&sup6;×0.25/(9×10&sup9;) = 0.75×10&sup6;/(9×10&sup9;) = 8.33×10−5 C = 83.3μC

Vmax = kQ/R = 9×10&sup9;×8.33×10−5/0.5 = 1.5×10&sup6; V = 1.5 MV

Electric Potential – Additional Results

Uniformly charged sphere (solid, non-conductor):

Outside (r > R): V = kQ/r

Inside (r < R): V = kQ(3R²−r²)/(2R³)   [maximum at centre: Vcentre = 3kQ/(2R) = (3/2)Vsurface]

Uniformly charged ring (charge Q, radius a) at axial point distance x from centre:

V = kQ/√(x²+a²);   Eaxial = kQx/(x²+a²)3/2

Maximum E on axis at x = a/√2

Uniformly charged disc (surface charge σ, radius R) at axial point x:

V = σ(√(R²+x²) − x)/(2ε0)

Eaxial = σ/(2ε0)[1 − x/√(R²+x²)]

Capacitor with Partially Inserted Dielectric

If a dielectric of constant κ is inserted to cover fraction (x/L) of the plate area A:

The remaining area (1−x/L)A is in air. The two regions act as capacitors in parallel:

C = ε0A/(d) × [(1−x/L) + κ(x/L)] = (ε0A/d)[1 + (κ−1)x/L]

Force on dielectric being pulled out (at constant voltage V):

F = (κ−1)ε0AV²/(2dL) (attractive, pulls dielectric in)

Solved Problems – Advanced Capacitors

Example – Bridge Circuit of Capacitors

Problem: Four capacitors 2μF, 4μF, 2μF, 4μF form a Wheatstone-bridge arrangement. A 5th capacitor of 1μF connects the bridge points. Find equivalent capacitance across the battery.

Check balance: C1/C2 = C3/C4 → 2/4 = 2/4 = 0.5 ✓ → Balanced bridge

For balanced bridge: no charge on bridge capacitor; it can be ignored.

Two arms in series: (2+2)=4μF and (4+4)=8μF – wait, recalculate.

Top arm: 2+4=6μF? No. Series: 1/(1/2+1/2)=1; each arm: 2 and 2 in series = 1μF; 4 and 4 in series = 2μF.

Simplified (balanced, Cbridge ignored): Ceq = (2×2/(2+2)) + (4×4/(4+4)) ... use actual circuit topology.

For the balanced case with C1=C3=2μF and C2=C4=4μF in bridge: Ceq = (C1C2/(C1+C2)) + (C3C4/(C3+C4)) = 8/6 + 8/6 = 8/3 ≈ 2.67μF

Example – Capacitor Energy in Series vs Parallel

Problem: Two capacitors 3μF and 6μF are connected (a) in series, (b) in parallel to 12 V. Find total energy in each case.

(a) Series: Ceq = 3×6/(3+6) = 2μF; U = ½CV² = ½×2×144 = 144μJ

(b) Parallel: Ceq = 3+6 = 9μF; U = ½×9×144 = 648μJ

Parallel stores 4.5 times more energy (Cparallel/Cseries = 9/2 = 4.5)

6. Practice Problems

Conceptual quick checks before solving:

  • For Coulomb's law problems: always verify that k = 9×10&sup9; and convert charges to Coulombs (1μC = 10−6 C).
  • For Gauss's law: identify the symmetry (spherical/cylindrical/planar) before choosing a Gaussian surface.
  • For potential: it is a scalar; just add algebraically. For field: vector sum required.
  • For capacitors: determine if battery remains connected or is disconnected before inserting dielectric.
  1. Two equal charges repel each other with 0.36 N at 0.1 m separation. Find the charge. [Ans: 2×10−&sup5; C]
  2. Charges +5μC and +20μC are 30 cm apart. Find E at the midpoint. [Ans: 2×10&sup6; N/C toward +5μC]
  3. An electron is accelerated through 1000 V. Find its final kinetic energy in eV and joules. [Ans: 1000 eV, 1.6×10−¹&sup6; J]
  4. A dipole (p=5×10−&sup9; C·m) is placed in uniform field E=10&sup4; N/C. Find torque at θ=30°. [Ans: 2.5×10−&sup5; N·m]
  5. Find flux through one face of a cube enclosing a charge Q at its centre. [Ans: Q/(6ε0)]
  6. A capacitor of 2μF is charged by 12 V battery. Find charge and energy stored. [Ans: 24μC, 144μJ]
  7. Two capacitors 4μF and 6μF in series across 100 V. Find charge and voltage across each. [Ans: 240μC; V4=60 V, V6=40 V]
  8. A 10μF capacitor has initial charge 500μC. It is connected to an uncharged 40μF capacitor. Find final energy. [Ans: initial U=12500μJ; final U=2500μJ]
  9. In Millikan's oil drop, a drop of mass 10−¹&sup5; kg is suspended in field E=5×10&sup4; N/C. Find charge on the drop. [Ans: 2×10−¹&sup9; C = 2e]
  10. A parallel plate capacitor (C=4μF, V=200 V). Find (a) E if d=2mm (b) σ on plates. [Ans: 10&sup5; V/m; σ=8.854×10−&sup7; C/m²]

7. Common Mistakes & JEE Tips

Top 10 Mistakes to Avoid

  1. Forgetting εr in a medium: Coulomb force and capacitance both change. Fmedium = Fvac/κ, Cmedium = κCvac.
  2. Treating potential as a vector: V is scalar. Add algebraically, not as vectors.
  3. Confusing E = 0 inside conductor with V = 0: Inside a conductor E = 0 but V = constant (the surface potential).
  4. Wrong sign in work-energy: Wfield = q(Vi−Vf); Wexternal = q(Vf−Vi).
  5. Gauss's law with non-uniform fields: Gauss's law gives the net flux; you need symmetry to find E from it.
  6. Series vs. Parallel capacitors: In series, same Q; in parallel, same V.
  7. Forgetting the factor 2 in parallel plates: E between plates = σ/ε0, not σ/(2ε0).
  8. Energy stored vs. energy supplied: Only half the energy supplied by battery is stored in a capacitor; the other half is dissipated as heat in the circuit.
  9. RC time constant units: τ = RC in seconds. Verify: (Ω)(F) = (V/A)(C/V) = C/A = s ✓
  10. Dipole direction: Dipole moment p points from −q to +q (opposite to Eequatorial direction).
