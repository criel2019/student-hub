1. Dual Nature of Radiation & Matter

1.1 Photon – Particle Nature of Light

Photon energy: E = hf = hc/λ

Photon momentum: p = E/c = h/λ = hf/c

Planck's constant: h = 6.626 × 10−34 J·s = 4.136 × 10−15 eV·s

hc = 1240 eV·nm (very useful for quick calculations)

1 eV = 1.6 × 10−19 J; c = 3×108 m/s

1.2 Photoelectric Effect

When light of frequency above a threshold falls on a metal surface, electrons are emitted. Einstein (1905) explained this using photon concept (Nobel Prize 1921).

KEmax = hf − φ = hf − hf0

where φ = work function = minimum energy to remove an electron from the surface.

QuantityFormulaPhysical Meaning
Work function φφ = hf0Energy to remove electron from surface
Threshold frequency f0f0 = φ/hMin frequency for photoelectric effect
Threshold wavelength λ0λ0 = hc/φMax wavelength for photoelectric effect
Stopping potential V0eV0 = KEmaxV needed to stop fastest electrons

1.3 Key Observations (JEE/NEET Important)

  • KEmax depends on frequency, NOT intensity. Higher intensity = more electrons, same KE.
  • Photocurrent is proportional to intensity (more photons = more electrons).
  • No time delay between illumination and electron emission (quantum effect).
  • Below threshold frequency: no emission regardless of how intense the light is.
  • Stopping potential depends on frequency, not intensity.

1.5 Solved Problems – Photoelectric Effect

Example 1 – Stopping Potential

Problem: Light of wavelength 300 nm falls on sodium (φ=2.3 eV). Find KEmax and stopping potential.

hf = hc/λ = 1240/300 = 4.13 eV

KEmax = 4.13 − 2.3 = 1.83 eV

Stopping potential V0 = KEmax/e = 1.83 V

Example 2 – Threshold Wavelength

Problem: Work function of tungsten is 4.52 eV. Find (a) threshold wavelength, (b) max KE for light of 200 nm.

(a) λ0 = hc/φ = 1240/4.52 = 274 nm (ultraviolet)

(b) hf = hc/λ = 1240/200 = 6.2 eV; KEmax = 6.2 − 4.52 = 1.68 eV

Example 3 – Comparing Two Metals

Problem: Light of 350 nm falls on metals A (φ=1.9 eV) and B (φ=4.0 eV). Which shows photoelectric effect? Compare stopping potentials.

Photon energy = 1240/350 = 3.54 eV

Metal A: KE = 3.54−1.9 = 1.64 eV > 0 → shows PE effect; V0 = 1.64 V

Metal B: 3.54 < 4.0 → no photoelectric effect

Example 4 – Effect of Intensity

Problem: In a photoelectric experiment, the intensity of light is doubled. What happens to (a) photocurrent, (b) KEmax, (c) stopping potential?

(a) Photocurrent doubles (more photons → more electrons)

(b) KEmax remains unchanged (depends only on frequency)

(c) Stopping potential remains unchanged (V0 = KEmax/e, unchanged)

1.6 de Broglie Wavelength (Wave Nature of Matter)

Louis de Broglie (1924): Matter also has wave-particle duality.

λ = h/p = h/(mv)

For a particle of mass m, velocity v, or kinetic energy KE:

λ = h/√(2mKE)

Electron accelerated through potential V:

λ = h/√(2meV) = 12.27/√V Å   (λ in Ångstrom, V in volts)

Or: λ = 1.227/√V nm

At room temperature T (thermal de Broglie): λ = h/√(3mkT)

1.7 Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle

It is impossible to simultaneously know the exact position and momentum of a particle:

Δx · Δp ≥ ℏ/2 = h/(4π)

Energy–time uncertainty: ΔE · Δt ≥ ℏ/2

Angular momentum: ΔLz · Δθ ≥ ℏ/2

This is a fundamental quantum limit, NOT due to measurement imperfection.

1.8 Solved Problems – de Broglie

Example 1 – Electron de Broglie Wavelength

Problem: An electron is accelerated through 100 V. Find its de Broglie wavelength.

λ = 12.27/√100 = 12.27/10 = 1.227 Å = 0.1227 nm

This is similar to X-ray wavelengths → electrons can be diffracted by crystal lattices (Davisson-Germer experiment).

Example 2 – Macroscopic Body

Problem: A 0.1 kg ball moves at 30 m/s. Find de Broglie wavelength.

λ = h/mv = 6.626×10−34/(0.1×30) = 6.626×10−34/3 = 2.2×10−34 m

This is undetectably small – wave nature of macroscopic objects is negligible.

Example 3 – Uncertainty Principle

Problem: An electron's position is measured to ±0.1 nm. What is minimum uncertainty in momentum?

Δp ≥ h/(4πΔx) = 6.626×10−34/(4π×10−10) = 5.27×10−25 kg·m/s

2. Bohr's Atomic Model

2.1 Bohr's Postulates

  1. Stable orbits: Electrons revolve in certain stable circular orbits without emitting radiation (contrary to classical EM theory).
  2. Quantization of angular momentum: L = mvr = nℏ = nh/(2π), where n = 1, 2, 3, … (principal quantum number)
  3. Energy quantization: Each orbit has a definite energy. Electrons in these orbits don't radiate.
  4. Radiation condition: When an electron jumps from higher orbit n2 to lower orbit n1, it emits a photon: hf = En2 − En1.

2.2 Key Formulas for Hydrogen-like Atoms

The wavelength of emitted photons follows the Rydberg formula:

2.4 Energy Level Diagram for Hydrogen

Energy levels: En = −13.6/n² eV

  • n=1: E = −13.6 eV (ground state)
  • n=2: E = −3.4 eV (first excited)
  • n=3: E = −1.51 eV (second excited)
  • n=4: E = −0.85 eV
  • n=∞: E = 0 (ionization level)

Ionization energy = 13.6 eV (energy to remove electron from n=1 to n=∞)

Excitation energy = difference between energy levels

2.5 Limitations of Bohr's Model

  • Explains only hydrogen and hydrogen-like (one-electron) species.
  • Cannot explain fine structure (splitting of spectral lines in magnetic fields – Zeeman effect).
  • Cannot explain multi-electron atoms.
  • Violates Heisenberg uncertainty principle (assumes definite orbit).
  • Does not explain relative intensities of spectral lines.
  • Replaced by quantum mechanical model (Schrödinger equation).

2.6 Solved Problems – Bohr Model

Example 1 – Wavelength of Hα Line (Balmer Series)

Problem: Find the wavelength of the first line of the Balmer series (Hα, n=3→2) in hydrogen.

1/λ = RH(1/2² − 1/3²) = 1.097×10&sup7; × (1/4 − 1/9)

= 1.097×10&sup7; × (9−4)/36 = 1.097×10&sup7; × 5/36 = 1.524×10&sup6; m−1

λ = 1/(1.524×10&sup6;) = 656.3 nm (red light)

Example 2 – Energy of Emitted Photon

Problem: A hydrogen atom transitions from n=4 to n=2. Find energy and wavelength of emitted photon.

E4 = −13.6/16 = −0.85 eV; E2 = −13.6/4 = −3.4 eV

ΔE = E4 − E2 = −0.85 − (−3.4) = 2.55 eV

λ = hc/E = 1240/2.55 = 486.3 nm (blue-green) (Balmer series, Hβ line)

Example 3 – Find n Given Radius

Problem: For He+ (Z=2), the radius of an orbit is 0.529 Å. Find n.

rn = n²a0/Z → 0.529 = n²×0.529/2 → n² = 2 → n = √2 …

Wait: rn = 0.529 n²/Z Å → 0.529 = 0.529n²/2 → n² = 2. This doesn't give integer n.

If radius = 2.116 Å: 2.116 = 0.529n²/2 → n² = 4×2.116/0.529/4 = 4 → n = 2

Example 4 – Ionization Energy of He+

Problem: Find ionization energy of He+ in ground state.

E1(He+) = −13.6×Z²/1² = −13.6×4 = −54.4 eV

Ionization energy = 54.4 eV

Example 5 – Spectral Lines from n=4

Problem: How many spectral lines can a hydrogen atom emit when excited to n=4?

Number of lines = n(n−1)/2 = 4×3/2 = 6 lines

Transitions: 4→3, 4→2, 4→1, 3→2, 3→1, 2→1

3. Nuclear Physics – Structure & Binding Energy

3.1 Nuclear Nomenclature

Nucleus AZX has:

  • Z = proton number (atomic number)
  • N = neutron number
  • A = Z + N (mass number)

Example: 23592U has 92 protons, 143 neutrons, mass number 235.

Isotopes: Same Z, different N (same element, different mass)

Isobars: Same A, different Z

Isotones: Same N, different Z

3.2 Nuclear Size and Density

Nuclear radius: R = R0A1/3

R0 = 1.2 × 10−15 m = 1.2 fm (femtometers)

Nuclear volume ∝ A (proportional to mass number)

Nuclear density: ρ ≈ 2.3 × 1017 kg/m³

This density is the same for all nuclei – remarkable result from R ∝ A1/3.

Compare: density of water = 1000 kg/m³; nuclear density is 1014 times greater.

3.3 Mass Defect and Binding Energy

Actual nuclear mass is always less than the sum of constituent nucleon masses. This difference is the mass defect Δm:

Δm = Z·mp + N·mn − Mnucleus

where mp = 1.007276 amu, mn = 1.008665 amu, 1 amu = 931.5 MeV/c²

Binding energy: BE = Δm × 931.5 MeV  (energy that holds the nucleus together)

Binding energy per nucleon: BE/A (measures nuclear stability)

3.4 Binding Energy per Nucleon Curve

The BE/nucleon curve (plot of BE/A vs mass number A) has these key features:

  • Rises steeply for light nuclei (H, He, Li …)
  • Reaches maximum near Fe-56: ≈8.8 MeV/nucleon (most stable nucleus)
  • Gradually decreases for heavy nuclei (Uranium ≈7.6 MeV/nucleon)
  • Fusion (light nuclei): Combining light nuclei increases BE/A → releases energy
  • Fission (heavy nuclei): Splitting heavy nucleus increases BE/A of products → releases energy
  • Notable peaks: 4He (7.07 MeV), 12C (7.68 MeV), 16O (7.98 MeV)

3.6 Solved Problems – Nuclear Structure

Example 1 – Binding Energy of Helium-4

Problem: Find the binding energy of 42He. m(4He) = 4.002602 amu.

Δm = 2mp + 2mn − m(He-4)

= 2(1.007276) + 2(1.008665) − 4.002602

= 2.014552 + 2.017330 − 4.002602 = 0.029280 amu

BE = 0.029280 × 931.5 = 27.28 MeV

BE/A = 27.28/4 = 6.82 MeV/nucleon

Example 2 – Nuclear Radius Ratio

Problem: Compare the radii of nuclei 27Al and 64Cu.

R ∝ A1/3: RAl/RCu = (27/64)1/3 = (3/4)1 = 3:4

(Since 27 = 3³ and 64 = 4³, the cube roots are exactly 3 and 4)

4. Radioactive Decay

4.1 Types of Radioactive Decay

Decay TypeNuclear ReactionChange in Z, A
α decayAZX → A−4Z−2Y + 42HeZ−2, A−4
β− decayAZX → AZ+1Y + e− + ν̅eZ+1, A same; n→p
β+ decayAZX → AZ−1Y + e+ + νeZ−1, A same; p→n
γ decayAZX* → AZX + γNo change; excited nucleus
Electron capturee− + AZX → AZ−1Y + νeZ−1, A same; p→n

Conservation laws: Mass number (A), charge number (Z), energy, momentum, lepton number – all conserved.

4.2 Radioactive Decay Law

The number of nuclei decreases exponentially:

N(t) = N0 e−λt

Activity: A(t) = |dN/dt| = λN(t) = A0e−λt

Unit of activity: Becquerel (Bq) = 1 decay/s; Curie (Ci) = 3.7×1010 Bq

QuantityFormula
Decay constant λλ = 0.693/T1/2
Half-life T1/2T1/2 = 0.693/λ = ln2/λ
Mean (average) life ττ = 1/λ = T1/2/0.693 = 1.44 T1/2
After n half-livesN = N0/2n
Fraction remainingN/N0 = (1/2)n = e−λt

4.3 Radioactive Series

Heavy nuclei decay in a series of steps until they reach a stable nucleus. Three major series:

  • Uranium series (4n+2): 238U → … → 206Pb (14 steps)
  • Thorium series (4n): 232Th → … → 208Pb
  • Actinium series (4n+3): 235U → … → 207Pb

The stable end products are all lead isotopes.

4.4 Solved Problems – Radioactivity

Example 1 – Finding Half-life from Activity Data

Problem: The activity of a radioactive sample drops from 1200 dpm to 150 dpm in 6 hours. Find the half-life.

1200 → 600 → 300 → 150: three half-lives in 6 hours.

T1/2 = 6/3 = 2 hours

Example 2 – Carbon Dating

Problem: A piece of wood has 25% of the 14C activity of living wood. Estimate its age. (T1/2 of 14C = 5730 years)

25% = (1/2)² → 2 half-lives have passed.

Age = 2 × 5730 = 11,460 years

Example 3 – Alpha Decay

Problem: 23892U undergoes alpha decay. Write the equation and identify the daughter nucleus.

23892U → 23490Th + 42He

Daughter: Thorium-234 (Z=90, A=234)

Check: Z: 92 = 90 + 2 ✓; A: 238 = 234 + 4 ✓

Example 4 – Activity Calculation

Problem: A sample of 60Co (T1/2 = 5.27 years) has mass 1 mg. Find its activity.

N = (m/M) × NA = (10−6/60) × 6.022×1023 = 1.004×1016 nuclei

λ = 0.693/T1/2 = 0.693/(5.27×365×24×3600) = 4.17×10−9 s−1

A = λN = 4.17×10−9×1.004×1016 = 4.19×107 Bq ≈ 1.13 mCi

Example 5 – Fraction Remaining

Problem: T1/2 of radioactive substance is 10 days. What fraction remains after 40 days?

n = 40/10 = 4 half-lives

Fraction = (1/2)4 = 1/16 = 6.25%

5. X-rays

5.1 Production of X-rays

X-rays are produced when high-energy electrons hit a metal target. Two mechanisms:

  1. Bremsstrahlung (braking radiation): Continuous X-ray spectrum. Electrons are decelerated and lose energy as X-ray photons. Produces a continuous range of wavelengths.
  2. Characteristic X-rays: Line spectrum. Incoming electron knocks out inner-shell electron; outer electrons fill the vacancy, emitting photons of specific energies.

5.2 Key Formulas

Minimum wavelength (Duane-Hunt law): All KE converted to photon energy:

λmin = hc/(eV) = 1240/V nm   (V in volts)

This is the shortest wavelength in the continuous spectrum.

Moseley's Law (for characteristic X-ray frequency f):

√f = a(Z − b)

where a and b are constants for a given series (K, L, …). Z = atomic number.

This provided strong experimental evidence for the concept of atomic number.

5.3 Solved Problems – X-rays

Example 1 – Minimum Wavelength

Problem: An X-ray tube operates at 50 kV. Find the minimum wavelength of X-rays produced.

λmin = hc/(eV) = 1240 eV·nm / (50,000 eV) = 0.0248 nm = 0.248 Å

6. Semiconductor Basics (JEE/NEET)

6.1 Intrinsic and Extrinsic Semiconductors

Intrinsic semiconductor: Pure Si or Ge. Both holes and electrons carry current. ne = nh.

n-type: Doped with pentavalent atoms (P, As, Sb). Extra electrons. ne >> nh.

p-type: Doped with trivalent atoms (B, Al, In). Extra holes. nh >> ne.

Mass action law: ne × nh = ni² (constant at given temperature)

6.2 p-n Junction Diode

  • Depletion region: Near junction, carriers recombine leaving fixed ions. Creates built-in electric field (barrier potential V0 ≈ 0.3 V for Ge, 0.7 V for Si).
  • Forward bias: p-side to +ve terminal. Barrier reduced. Large current flows (exponential: I = I0(eeV/kT −1)).
  • Reverse bias: p-side to −ve terminal. Barrier increased. Very small reverse saturation current I0.
  • At breakdown voltage (Zener breakdown or avalanche): large reverse current.

6.3 Special Diodes

6.4 Solved Problems – Semiconductors

Example 1 – Carrier Concentration

Problem: In n-type Si, ne = 5×1016/cm³. If ni = 1.5×1010/cm³, find nh.

ne×nh = ni² → nh = ni²/ne = (1.5×1010)²/(5×1016)

8. Nuclear Reactions & Energy

8.1 Nuclear Fission

A heavy nucleus splits into two lighter nuclei with release of enormous energy and 2–3 neutrons.

Example: 23592U + n → 14156Ba + 9236Kr + 3n + 200 MeV

Chain reaction: The neutrons produced cause further fissions. Uncontrolled → atomic bomb; controlled (in reactor) → nuclear power.

Critical mass: Minimum mass for self-sustained chain reaction (for 235U ≈ 1–50 kg depending on geometry and reflector).

8.2 Nuclear Fusion

Two light nuclei combine to form a heavier nucleus with release of energy.

Example (in the Sun): 41H → 4He + 2e+ + 2νe + 26.7 MeV

D-T reaction (most promising for fusion reactors):

2H + 3H → 4He + n + 17.6 MeV

Why fusion needs high temperature: Both nuclei are positively charged; thermal energy must overcome the Coulomb barrier (≈108 K needed).

Energy per unit mass is higher in fusion than fission. Stars are powered by fusion.

8.3 Mass-Energy Equivalence

Einstein's famous relation:

E = mc²

Energy released when mass Δm is converted: ΔE = Δmc²

For nuclear reactions: Δm is typically in amu; use 1 amu = 931.5 MeV/c²

The Q-value of a reaction: Q = (Σmreactants − Σmproducts) × 931.5 MeV

Q > 0: exothermic (energy released); Q < 0: endothermic (energy absorbed)

8.4 Particle Physics Basics

Fundamental particles:

  • Quarks: up (u), down (d), charm (c), strange (s), top (t), bottom (b)
  • Proton = uud; Neutron = udd (quark composition)
  • Leptons: electron, muon, tau, and their neutrinos
  • Force carriers: photon (γ, EM), W±/Z (weak), gluons (strong), graviton (gravity, unconfirmed)

Antiparticles: Every particle has an antiparticle with opposite charge. Positron e+ is antielectron.

Pair production: γ → e− + e+ (requires Eγ ≥ 2mec² = 1.022 MeV)

Pair annihilation: e− + e+ → 2γ (each photon has E = 0.511 MeV)

8.5 Solved Problems – Nuclear Reactions

Example 1 – Q-value of a Reaction

Problem: Find Q-value: 73Li + 11H → 2 42He

Masses (amu): m(7Li)=7.01601, m(1H)=1.00783, m(4He)=4.00260

Δm = (7.01601+1.00783) − 2(4.00260) = 8.02384 − 8.00520 = 0.01864 amu

Q = 0.01864 × 931.5 = 17.36 MeV (exothermic – energy released)

Example 2 – Fission Energy

Problem: How much energy (in MWh) is released by fission of 1 g of 235U? (200 MeV per fission)

N = NA×m/M = 6.022×1023×0.001/235 = 2.563×1018 atoms (fissions)

Total energy = 2.563×1018×200×10&sup6;×1.6×10−19 J = 2.563×1018×3.2×10−11 J

= 8.2×10&sup7; J = 82 MJ ≈ 22.8 kWh ≈ 0.023 MWh

This is enough to power a 100W bulb for ≈228 hours from just 1 gram!
