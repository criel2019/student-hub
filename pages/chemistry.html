1. Physical Chemistry Formulas

Atomic Structure

Bohr's model gives the radius and energy of the n-th orbit for a hydrogen-like atom of atomic number Z:

rₙ = n² × 0.529 Å / Z    (orbit radius)
Eₙ = −13.6 × Z² / n² eV    (orbit energy, negative = bound state)
vₙ = 2.18 × 10⁶ × Z/n m/s    (orbital velocity)
Frequency of photon emitted: ν = (E₂ − E₁)/h   where h = 6.626 × 10⁻³⁴ J·s

Electronic Configuration Rules

  • Aufbau Principle: Fill orbitals in order of increasing energy: 1s → 2s → 2p → 3s → 3p → 4s → 3d → 4p → 5s → 4d → 5p → 6s → 4f → 5d → 6p …
  • Hund's Rule of Maximum Multiplicity: Electrons occupy all orbitals of equal energy singly before pairing. All singly occupied orbitals have parallel spins.
  • Pauli's Exclusion Principle: No two electrons in an atom can have identical sets of four quantum numbers. Maximum 2 electrons per orbital, with opposite spins.

Bond Polarity & Electronegativity

Electronegativity (Pauling scale) increases across a period and decreases down a group. F has the highest electronegativity (4.0). A bond is polar if electronegativity difference > 0.4; ionic character dominates when > 1.7.

Dipole moment: μ = q × d   (unit: Debye, D; 1 D = 3.336 × 10⁻³⁰ C·m)
Bond energy order (for carbon): C≡C > C=C > C−C (bond length: triple < double < single)

Key Element Group Summaries

  • Alkali metals (Group 1 — Li, Na, K, Rb, Cs, Fr): Soft metals, highly reactive with water producing H₂ and MOH. +1 oxidation state only. Reactivity increases down group. Li shows anomalous properties (diagonal relationship with Mg).
  • Alkaline earth metals (Group 2 — Be, Mg, Ca, Sr, Ba, Ra): +2 ions. Be shows diagonal relationship with Al. Ca is biologically vital (bones, teeth, nerve signals).
  • Halogens (Group 17 — F, Cl, Br, I, At): Form −1 ions. Reactivity: F₂ > Cl₂ > Br₂ > I₂. F can only form −1; others can have positive oxidation states in compounds with more electronegative atoms.
  • Noble gases (Group 18 — He, Ne, Ar, Kr, Xe, Rn): Complete valence shell, very low reactivity. Xe forms stable compounds (XeF₂, XeF₄, XeO₃).
  • Transition metals (Groups 3–12): Variable oxidation states, form colored compounds, paramagnetism, catalytic activity, ability to form complexes.

Magnetic Properties of Transition Metal Ions

Spin-only magnetic moment: μ = √(n(n+2)) BM   (Bohr Magnetons)
where n = number of unpaired electrons

Examples:
Fe³⁺ (d⁵, 5 unpaired): μ = √(5×7) = √35 ≈ 5.92 BM
Cu²⁺ (d⁹, 1 unpaired): μ = √(1×3) = √3 ≈ 1.73 BM
Zn²⁺ (d¹⁰, 0 unpaired): μ = 0 BM (diamagnetic)

4. Organic Chemistry

IUPAC Nomenclature Rules

  • Select the longest carbon chain containing the principal functional group as the parent chain (longest chain = highest number of carbons).
  • Priority order for principal characteristic group (suffix): Carboxylic acid > Anhydride > Ester > Acid halide > Amide > Aldehyde > Ketone > Alcohol > Amine
  • Number the chain from the end nearest the principal functional group to give it the lowest locant.
  • E/Z nomenclature: Assign CIP priorities to groups on each sp² carbon; E = higher-priority groups on opposite sides; Z = same side.
  • R/S configuration: Assign CIP priorities (1–4). With lowest priority (4) pointing away, clockwise sequence of 1→2→3 = R; counterclockwise = S.

Directing Effects in EAS

  • Ortho/Para directors (activating): −OH, −NH₂, −NHR, −NR₂, −OR, −alkyl groups — donate electron density into ring through resonance and/or induction.
  • Ortho/Para directors (weakly deactivating): −F, −Cl, −Br, −I — halogens withdraw by induction but donate lone pairs by resonance; net o/p direction.
  • Meta directors (deactivating): −NO₂, −CN, −CHO, −COOR, −COOH, −SO₃H, −NR₃⁺, −CF₃ — withdraw electrons by resonance; make ring electron-poor.

AldehydesTollens' test: Ag mirror (RCHO only, not ketones). Fehling's/Benedict's: brick-red Cu₂O precipitate. Nucleophilic addition: HCN, NaHSO₃, Grignard (RMgX), RNH₂ → imine/Schiff base. Iodoform test: only for CH₃CHO.
KetonesNo Tollens', no Fehling's. Iodoform test: methyl ketones (RCOCH₃) give CHI₃ (yellow ppt) with I₂/NaOH. Nucleophilic addition: Grignard, HCN. Reduction: NaBH₄ or LiAlH₄ → secondary alcohol.
Carboxylic AcidsEsterification (H⁺), reduction (LiAlH₄ → 1° alcohol), decarboxylation (−CO₂), HVZ alpha-halogenation, hell conversion to acyl halides.
AminesBasicity order in gas phase: 3° > 2° > 1° > NH₃. In water: 2° > 1° > 3° ≈ NH₃ (solvation effect). 1° ArNH₂ + HNO₂ (0–5°C) → ArN₂⁺ (diazonium salt). Coupling reaction with phenols/ArH (azo dye synthesis).

Key Rules and Regioselectivity

Markovnikov's Rule: In electrophilic addition of HX to unsymmetrical alkene, H adds to C with more H's (more H-substituted), forming more stable carbocation.

Anti-Markovnikov (Radical / Peroxide effect): With ROOR (peroxides), HBr adds via radical mechanism — Br adds to less substituted carbon (H to more substituted).

Zaitsev's Rule (elimination): With strong, small base (NaOH/EtOH), more substituted alkene is the major product (thermodynamic control).

Hofmann's Rule (elimination): With bulky base (e.g., t-BuOK), less substituted alkene is the major product (steric control).

Amino Acids and Proteins

  • Essential amino acids (9): Histidine, Isoleucine, Leucine, Lysine, Methionine, Phenylalanine, Threonine, Tryptophan, Valine (mnemonic: "His Ile Leu Lys Met Phe Thr Trp Val").
  • Primary structure: Sequence of amino acids linked by peptide bonds (−CO−NH−). Determined by DNA sequence.
  • Secondary structure: α-helix (right-handed, 3.6 residues/turn, H-bonds between NH and C=O four residues apart) or β-pleated sheet (H-bonds between adjacent strands). Both stabilized by H-bonds.
  • Tertiary structure: Complete 3D shape of a polypeptide; stabilized by disulfide bridges (Cys-Cys), hydrophobic interactions, hydrogen bonds, electrostatic interactions.
  • Quaternary structure: Two or more polypeptide chains. Example: hemoglobin (4 subunits: 2α + 2β).

Lipids

  • Triglycerides: Glycerol + 3 fatty acids (ester linkages). Saturated fats (no C=C) = solid at RT; unsaturated fats (cis C=C) = liquid oils.
  • Phospholipids: Glycerol + 2 fatty acids + phosphate group + polar head. Form lipid bilayer (cell membrane). Amphipathic.
  • Steroids: Fused 4-ring system. Cholesterol (precursor to hormones), testosterone, estrogen, progesterone, cortisol, bile acids, vitamin D.

9. Chemistry Mnemonics

LEO says GER (Oxidation/Reduction):
LEO = Loss of Electrons is Oxidation
GER = Gain of Electrons is Reduction
Alternative: OIL RIG (Oxidation Is Loss, Reduction Is Gain)
Activity Series (most to least reactive metals):
"Please Stop Calling Me A Zebra, I Like Snobbish Horses, Can My Art Please Glow"
K, Na, Ca, Mg, Al, Zn, Fe (Iron), Ni (Nickel), Sn, H (Hydrogen), Cu, Hg, Ag, Pt, Au
Diatomic elements — BrINClHOF (pronounced "Brinklehoff"):
Br₂, I₂, N₂, Cl₂, H₂, O₂, F₂ — these 7 elements exist as diatomic molecules at room temperature.
Strong acids (7 strong acids to memorize):
HCl, HBr, HI (hydrohalic acids), H₂SO₄ (sulfuric), HNO₃ (nitric), HClO₄ (perchloric), HClO₃ (chloric)
Mnemonic: "Horses Briskly Ignite Sulfuric Nitric Perchloric Chloric"
Aufbau filling order:
1s → 2s → 2p → 3s → 3p → 4s → 3d → 4p → 5s → 4d → 5p → 6s → 4f → 5d → 6p
Tip: Draw the (n+l) diagonal rule grid; follow diagonal arrows top-right to bottom-left.
Hybridization shortcut:
Count sigma bonds + lone pairs on central atom:
2 = sp (linear, 180°), 3 = sp² (trigonal planar, 120°), 4 = sp³ (tetrahedral, 109.5°)
5 = sp³d (trigonal bipyramidal), 6 = sp³d² (octahedral)
Colors of transition metal ions (Ti to Cu):
"Tim's Very Colorful Crystals Make Few Colorful Nice Pieces"
Ti³⁺=Purple, V³⁺=Green, Cr³⁺=Dark green, Cr²⁺=Blue (Crystal), Mn²⁺=Pink, Fe²⁺=pale green, Co²⁺=Pink (Colorful), Ni²⁺=Green, Cu²⁺=Blue (Pieces)
Oxoacid strength of chlorine:
Increasing acid strength: HOCl < HClO₂ < HClO₃ < HClO₄
More oxygen atoms = more electronegative surroundings = more polar O−H bond = stronger acid.
Colligative properties (all depend on NUMBER of particles, not their nature):
RBOF = Relative lowering of VP, Boiling point elevation, Osmotic pressure, Freezing point depression
All use van't Hoff factor i: i=1 (non-electrolyte), i=2 (1:1 electrolyte like NaCl), i=3 (1:2 salt like Na₂SO₄)
EAS directing groups — Ortho/Para vs Meta:
Ortho/Para directors: Electron-donating groups (OH, NH₂, OR, alkyl, halogens)
Meta directors: Electron-withdrawing groups (NO₂, CN, CHO, COOH, SO₃H)
Tip: If the group next to benzene has a lone pair (donating), it goes o/p. If electron-withdrawing (withdraws via resonance), it goes meta.

10. Practice Problems (Solved)

Q1. Calculate the pH of 0.01 M CH₃COOH solution. (Ka = 1.8 × 10⁻⁵)
Solution:
For weak acid: pH = ½(pKa − log[HA])
pKa = −log(1.8 × 10⁻⁵) = 4.74
log[HA] = log(0.01) = −2
pH = ½(4.74 − (−2)) = ½(6.74) = 3.37
Verification: [H⁺] = √(Ka × C) = √(1.8×10⁻⁵ × 0.01) = √(1.8×10⁻⁷) = 4.24×10⁻⁴ M → pH = 3.37 ✓
Q2. A Daniell cell (Zn-Cu galvanic cell) operates under standard conditions. Calculate E°cell and ΔG°.
Solution:
Anode (oxidation): Zn → Zn²⁺ + 2e⁻   E°(Zn²⁺/Zn) = −0.76 V
Cathode (reduction): Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu   E°(Cu²⁺/Cu) = +0.34 V
E°cell = E°cathode − E°anode = 0.34 − (−0.76) = +1.10 V
n = 2 electrons transferred
ΔG° = −nFE°cell = −2 × 96500 × 1.10 = −212,300 J = −212.3 kJ/mol
Since ΔG° < 0, the cell reaction is spontaneous under standard conditions. ✓
Q3. The half-life of a first-order reaction is 693 seconds. What percentage of the reactant remains after 2079 seconds?
Solution:
t½ = 693 s → k = 0.693/693 = 1.0 × 10⁻³ s⁻¹
Number of half-lives = 2079/693 = 3 half-lives
Fraction remaining = (½)³ = 1/8 = 0.125
Percentage remaining = 12.5%
Q4. What are the major and minor products when 2-butanol undergoes acid-catalyzed dehydration? Name the rule used.
Solution:
2-Butanol (CH₃-CHOH-CH₂-CH₃) heated with conc. H₂SO₄ at 170°C undergoes E1 elimination.
Applying Zaitsev's Rule: the major product is the more substituted (more stable) alkene.
Major product: but-2-ene (CH₃-CH=CH-CH₃) — disubstituted double bond
Minor product: but-1-ene (CH₃-CH₂-CH=CH₂) — monosubstituted double bond
But-2-ene also exists as cis (Z) and trans (E) isomers; trans predominates.
Q5. 4.5 g of glucose (M = 180 g/mol) is dissolved in 250 g of water. Calculate (a) molality, (b) boiling point elevation, (c) freezing point depression. (Kb = 0.512 °C·kg/mol, Kf = 1.86 °C·kg/mol)
Solution:
Moles of glucose = 4.5 / 180 = 0.025 mol
(a) Molality m = 0.025 mol / 0.250 kg = 0.10 mol/kg
Glucose is a non-electrolyte → i = 1
(b) ΔTb = Kb × m × i = 0.512 × 0.10 × 1 = 0.0512 °C
New boiling point = 100 + 0.0512 = 100.0512 °C
(c) ΔTf = Kf × m × i = 1.86 × 0.10 × 1 = 0.186 °C
New freezing point = 0 − 0.186 = −0.186 °C

FAQ

What is the best way to study organic chemistry for JEE and NEET?

Start with IUPAC naming and isomerism to build a solid foundation. Study reaction mechanisms — understand electron-pushing (arrow-pushing) for addition, substitution, and elimination reactions rather than memorizing products blindly. Create a functional group interconversion map (connecting alcohols, aldehydes, ketones, acids, esters, amines). For JEE Advanced, pay special attention to stereochemistry (R/S, E/Z, Walden inversion in SN2, racemization in SN1). Revise named reactions with mechanism weekly.

How are elements organized in the periodic table?

The 118 elements are arranged by increasing atomic number in 7 periods (rows) and 18 groups (columns). Elements in the same group have identical valence electron configurations and similar chemical properties. The table divides into: s-block (Groups 1–2, valence electrons in s orbitals), p-block (Groups 13–18, valence electrons in p orbitals), d-block (Groups 3–12, transition metals), and f-block (lanthanides and actinides, valence electrons in f orbitals).

What is the Nernst equation and when is it used?

The Nernst equation calculates cell potential under non-standard conditions: E = E° − (0.0592/n) × log Q at 25°C, where n = electrons transferred and Q = reaction quotient. Use it when concentrations differ from 1 M (or pressures from 1 atm for gases). At equilibrium Q = K and E = 0, so ΔG° = −RT ln K = −nFE°. This connects electrochemistry with thermodynamics and equilibrium.

What are the most important physical chemistry formulas for JEE and NEET?

Critical physical chemistry formulas: Nernst equation (electrochemistry), integrated rate laws and Arrhenius equation (kinetics), Gibbs free energy ΔG = ΔH − TΔS and ΔG° = −RT ln K (thermodynamics), Henderson-Hasselbalch for buffer pH, colligative property equations (ΔTb = Kbmi, ΔTf = Kfmi, π = iMRT), van't Hoff factor i for electrolytes, and Kp = Kc(RT)^Δn for equilibrium. Learn when each formula applies and its limitations.

Which inorganic chemistry topics are most important for NEET?

For NEET: p-block elements (especially Groups 15, 16, 17, 18) — read NCERT Chapter 7 (Class 11) and Chapters 7–8 (Class 12) thoroughly. s-block elements and their important compounds (NaOH, Na₂CO₃, CaO, Ca(OH)₂). Coordination compounds (IUPAC naming, VBT, isomerism). d-block transition metals (electronic configurations, oxidation states, colors, magnetic properties). Metallurgy principles. Environmental chemistry. Hydrogen and its compounds (H₂O₂). NEET frequently tests direct NCERT facts, tables, and reactions in these chapters.
