1. Mendelian Genetics

1.1 Mendel's Experiments – Why Garden Pea?

Gregor Mendel (1822–1884) used Pisum sativum (garden pea) for his experiments. Reasons:

  • Short life cycle (one generation per year; fast results)
  • Self-pollinating (can maintain pure lines easily)
  • Can be cross-pollinated artificially
  • Many contrasting, clearly distinguishable characters
  • Large number of offspring (good statistics)
  • Easily available and cheap to grow

1.3 Monohybrid Cross (Tt × Tt)

Crossing two plants heterozygous for one character (e.g., plant height):

T (from parent 1)t (from parent 1)
T (from parent 2)TT (Tall)Tt (Tall)
t (from parent 2)Tt (Tall)tt (Dwarf)

Phenotype Ratio: 3 Tall : 1 Dwarf (3:1)

Genotype Ratio: 1 TT : 2 Tt : 1 tt

Test Cross (Tt × tt): 1 Tall (Tt) : 1 Dwarf (tt) → used to determine unknown genotype

Back Cross (Tt × TT): All Tall (TT and Tt equally); phenotype ratio 4 Tall : 0 Dwarf

1.4 Dihybrid Cross (TtRr × TtRr)

T=tall/t=dwarf; R=round/r=wrinkled. Each parent produces 4 types of gametes: TR, Tr, tR, tr (in equal frequency – 1:1:1:1)

Phenotype Ratio: 9 Tall Round : 3 Tall Wrinkled : 3 Dwarf Round : 1 Dwarf Wrinkled (9:3:3:1)

Genotype Ratio: 1:2:1:2:4:2:1:2:1 (9 phenotypic classes collapse to 4 phenotypes)

Parental types: Tall Round + Dwarf Wrinkled (same as parents)

Recombinants: Tall Wrinkled + Dwarf Round (new combinations; evidence for independent assortment)

1.5 Mendel's Three Laws

LawStatementChromosomal Basis
Law of Dominance In a heterozygote, only one allele (dominant) is expressed; the other (recessive) is masked Dominant allele produces functional protein; recessive may produce non-functional or no protein
Law of Segregation (Purity of Gametes) The two alleles of a gene separate during gamete formation; each gamete carries only one allele Homologous chromosomes separate during Meiosis I → each gamete gets one homolog with one allele
Law of Independent Assortment Alleles of different genes assort independently during gamete formation (only if genes are on different chromosomes) Non-homologous chromosomes align randomly at metaphase I and segregate independently → 2ⁿ gamete types

1.6 Extensions of Mendelian Genetics

Incomplete Dominance (Blending Inheritance)

Neither allele is completely dominant → intermediate (blended) phenotype in heterozygote.

Example: 4 o'clock plant (Mirabilis jalapa)

  • RR = Red flowers
  • Rr = Pink flowers (intermediate!)
  • rr = White flowers

Cross Rr × Rr: Phenotype ratio = 1 Red : 2 Pink : 1 White = 1:2:1 (phenotype = genotype ratio)

Mechanism: One R allele produces only enough pigment for pink color; two R alleles needed for full red. One gene dose is insufficient for full phenotype.

Codominance

Both alleles are expressed simultaneously and independently in the heterozygote (no blending; both phenotypes visible).

Example: ABO blood group (IA and IB alleles are codominant to each other)

AIAIA or IAiAntigen AAnti-B

ABIAIBAntigen A + BNeither anti-A nor anti-B
OiiNeither A nor BBoth anti-A and anti-B

Blood Group Inheritance Rules:

  • IA and IB are codominant to each other
  • Both IA and IB are dominant over i (recessive)
  • ABO system = multiple alleles (3 alleles in population, but individual has only 2)
  • Universal donor: O (no antigens; AB has no antibodies → universal recipient)

Polygenic Inheritance

  • Multiple genes (each with multiple alleles) contribute to a single quantitative trait
  • Produces a continuous range of phenotypes → normal (bell-curve) distribution in population
  • Also influenced by environment
  • Examples: Human skin color (at least 3–5 gene loci), height, body weight, intelligence (IQ)
  • More dominant alleles → more extreme phenotype (e.g., darker skin)

Pleiotropy (One Gene → Multiple Traits)

  • A single gene affects multiple (apparently unrelated) phenotypic traits
  • Examples:
  • PKU (phenylketonuria): PAH gene mutation → mental retardation + light skin color + musty urine odor
  • Sickle cell disease (HBB gene): abnormal hemoglobin → anemia + organ damage + jaundice + bone pain + swollen hands
  • Marfan syndrome: FBN1 gene → tall stature + lens dislocation + aortic aneurysm

2.3 Sex-Linked Inheritance

X-linked Recessive (e.g., Haemophilia A, Colour Blindness)

  • Gene is on X chromosome; Y has no corresponding allele (hemizygous in males)
  • Males: only need one copy of recessive allele to be affected (XʰY)
  • Females: need two copies to be affected; one copy = carrier (XᴴXʰ)
  • Carrier female: unaffected phenotype but passes disease allele to 50% of children

Cross 1: Carrier mother (XᴴXʰ) × Normal father (XᴴY)

Xᴴ (from father)Y (from father)
Xᴴ (from mother)XᴴXᴴ (Normal female)XᴴY (Normal male)
Xʰ (from mother)XᴴXʰ (Carrier female)XʰY (Affected male)

Result: 50% of sons affected; 50% of daughters carriers

Cross 2: Carrier mother (XᴴXʰ) × Affected father (XʰY)

Daughters: 50% carrier (XᴴXʰ) + 50% AFFECTED (XʰXʰ) — affected females ARE possible! Sons: 50% normal + 50% affected.

Y-linked (Holandric) Inheritance

  • Gene only on Y chromosome → only males affected; transmitted from father to ALL sons
  • Example: hairy pinnae (hairy ears) in some human families
  • Males: 100% of sons affected; 0% of daughters affected

3. Molecular Genetics

3.1 DNA Structure

  • Double helix proposed by Watson and Crick in 1953; based on Franklin's X-ray diffraction data (Photo 51) and Chargaff's rules
  • B-DNA (most common form): Right-handed helix; 10 base pairs per turn; rise of 3.4 Å per bp; pitch = 34 Å; major and minor grooves alternate
  • Base pairs (Chargaff's rules):
    • A = T (2 hydrogen bonds) — Adenine pairs with Thymine
    • G ≡ C (3 hydrogen bonds) — Guanine pairs with Cytosine
    • A + G = T + C (purines = pyrimidines)
    • A + T/G + C ratio varies between species (GC content = species characteristic)
  • Antiparallel strands: One strand runs 5'→3', its complement runs 3'→5'
  • Sugar-phosphate backbone: Deoxyribose + phosphate alternating; bases project inward toward helix axis
  • Purines (double ring): Adenine (A), Guanine (G) — remember: PuRine has R; AG are purines
  • Pyrimidines (single ring): Thymine (T), Cytosine (C), Uracil (U in RNA)

3.2 DNA Replication

Semi-conservative replication proved by Meselson-Stahl experiment (1958):

  • Grew E. coli in ¹⁵N medium (heavy) → switched to ¹⁴N medium (light)
  • After 1 generation: all DNA hybrid density (¹⁵N-¹⁴N) — confirms semi-conservative
  • After 2 generations: 50% hybrid + 50% light — rules out conservative model

Alternative (rejected) models: Conservative (one all-heavy + one all-light) and Dispersive (both strands mixture throughout)

Leading vs Lagging Strand

  • Leading strand: Synthesized continuously in the 5'→3' direction (toward the replication fork); one primer needed
  • Lagging strand: Synthesized discontinuously (away from fork); multiple Okazaki fragments (100–200 nt in eukaryotes, 1000–2000 nt in prokaryotes); each fragment requires its own primer
  • Okazaki fragments are joined by DNA Ligase after RNA primers removed by Pol I

3.3 Transcription

  • Template strand (antisense strand, –strand) is read 3'→5' by RNA polymerase
  • mRNA is synthesized 5'→3' and has the same sequence as the non-template (coding, sense, +strand), except U replaces T
  • RNA Polymerase: No primer needed (unlike DNA Pol III); does not proofread

Prokaryotic transcription:

  • Sigma (σ) factor: helps RNA polymerase recognize promoter (–10 Pribnow box: TATAAT; –35 region: TTGACA)
  • Transcription and translation can occur simultaneously (no nuclear membrane)
  • mRNA is polycistronic (one mRNA → multiple proteins)

Eukaryotic transcription:

  • Three RNA polymerases: Pol I (rRNA, in nucleolus), Pol II (pre-mRNA, snRNA), Pol III (tRNA, 5S rRNA, small RNAs)
  • Promoter: TATA box (~–30), general transcription factors (TFs) required
  • mRNA is monocistronic (one mRNA → one protein)
  • Transcription occurs in nucleus; mRNA must be processed and exported

mRNA Processing (eukaryotes only):

  • 5' Cap: 7-methylguanosine (m7G) added to 5' end; protects mRNA from degradation; needed for ribosome binding
  • 3' Poly-A tail: 100–250 adenine residues added to 3' end; increases mRNA stability; aids nuclear export
  • Splicing: Introns (intervening sequences) removed; exons (expressed sequences) joined together. Performed by spliceosome (snRNPs + snRNA)
  • Alternative splicing: Same pre-mRNA can be spliced differently → multiple protein variants from one gene (e.g., 20,000 human genes → 100,000+ proteins)

3.4 Translation

The Genetic Code

  • 64 codons total: 61 sense (code for amino acids) + 3 stop codons (UAA, UAG, UGA)
  • Degenerate: Most amino acids coded by more than one codon (synonymous codons)
  • Exceptions (non-degenerate): Methionine (Met) = AUG only; Tryptophan (Trp) = UGG only
  • AUG: Start codon AND codes for internal Met; first codon translated in ALL organisms
  • Non-overlapping: Each nucleotide is part of only one codon
  • Commaless: No punctuation between codons
  • Universal: Same code in virtually all organisms (few exceptions: mitochondria, some ciliates)
  • Wobble hypothesis (Crick): 3rd position of codon (wobble position) can pair with multiple bases of anticodon → fewer tRNAs than codons needed

tRNA Structure and Function

  • Cloverleaf secondary structure; L-shaped 3D structure
  • Anticodon loop: 3 nucleotides complementary to mRNA codon (antiparallel, anticomplementary)
  • Acceptor stem: 3' end always –CCA-OH where amino acid is attached
  • Aminoacyl-tRNA synthetase: Enzyme that attaches correct amino acid to its tRNA ("second genetic code"); one enzyme per amino acid (20 enzymes); uses ATP

5. Pedigree Analysis

Symbols Used in Pedigrees

  • Circle (○) = Female; Square (□) = Male
  • Filled (●/■) = Affected; Unfilled (○/□) = Unaffected
  • Half-filled (◑) = Carrier (for X-linked recessive)
  • Horizontal line between male and female = Mating
  • Vertical line from mating line = Offspring
  • Roman numerals = Generation (I, II, III...); Arabic numerals = Individual (1, 2, 3...)

Pedigree Practice Example 1 – Autosomal Recessive

Given: Two normal parents (II-1 and II-2) have one affected son (III-1) and three normal children. No affected individuals in generation I.

Analysis: Affected child with two normal parents → recessive. Both parents are carriers (Aa). Equal sex ratio in affected → autosomal (not X-linked). Pattern: Autosomal Recessive.

Probability that unaffected sibling is a carrier: Of the 3 unaffected (from Aa×Aa cross: 1 AA + 2 Aa + 1 aa), unaffected are 1 AA + 2 Aa → probability = 2/3.

Pedigree Practice Example 2 – X-linked Recessive

Given: All affected individuals are male. Carrier females (II-2) are daughters of normal father and normal mother from generation I. Affected males in generation III are sons of carrier females.

Analysis: Only males affected; carrier females; no male-to-male transmission → X-linked recessive.

Pedigree Practice Example 3 – Autosomal Dominant

Given: Affected individual in every generation; both males and females affected; one affected parent always present when child is affected.

Analysis: Trait appears in every generation; both sexes affected equally → Autosomal Dominant. Affected individual is usually heterozygous (Aa); aa = unaffected.

6. DNA Fingerprinting

Principle: Certain regions of human DNA contain short sequences repeated in tandem (VNTR – Variable Number Tandem Repeats, or STR – Short Tandem Repeats). The number of repeats is highly variable between individuals → unique pattern.

Steps in DNA Fingerprinting (Southern Blotting method):

  1. DNA extraction: From blood, saliva, hair roots, semen, skin cells
  2. Restriction digestion: Cut DNA with restriction enzyme (e.g., EcoRI) → fragments of different sizes
  3. Gel electrophoresis: Separate fragments by size (smaller → run farther in gel)
  4. Denaturation: Gel treated with NaOH → DNA fragments become single-stranded
  5. Southern blotting: Transfer DNA from gel to nitrocellulose or nylon membrane
  6. Hybridization: Radioactive/fluorescent probe (complementary to VNTR sequence) added → probe binds to complementary sequences
  7. Autoradiography: X-ray film placed over membrane → bands appear where probe hybridized → unique banding pattern = DNA fingerprint

Modern methods: PCR-based STR analysis is faster, more sensitive, requires very little DNA

Applications of DNA Fingerprinting

ApplicationDescription
Forensic scienceIdentify criminals from biological evidence (blood, semen, hair) at crime scenes
Paternity testingChild's bands must be present in one parent; compare 50% from each parent
Disaster victim identificationIdentify victims when visual identification not possible (Bhopal, 9/11, tsunamis)

India: DNA fingerprinting technology was first used in India in 1989, pioneered by Dr. Lalji Singh at the Centre for Cellular and Molecular Biology (CCMB), Hyderabad. India's first case of DNA fingerprinting was a paternity dispute case.

Practice MCQs (10 Questions)

Q1. Mendel's law of independent assortment holds true when the two genes are:

(A) On the same chromosome   (B) On non-homologous chromosomes   (C) Closely linked   (D) On sex chromosomes

Show Answer

Answer: (B) On non-homologous chromosomes. Independent assortment requires that genes segregate independently, which occurs when they are on different (non-homologous) chromosomes. Linked genes (same chromosome) do not assort independently unless they are very far apart.

Q2. In a cross between a normal vision female (carrier) and a normal vision male, what is the probability of having a colour-blind daughter?

Show Answer

Answer: 0 (zero; impossible). Carrier female = XᴴXʰ. Normal male = XᴴY. Cross: daughters = XᴴXᴴ (normal) and XᴴXʰ (carrier). No daughter is XʰXʰ → no colour-blind daughters. Sons: 50% XᴴY (normal), 50% XʰY (colour-blind).

Q3. Chargaff's rules state that in DNA, A+G/T+C = ?

Show Answer

Answer: 1 (A+G = T+C always; ratio = 1). This is because A pairs with T and G pairs with C (purines = pyrimidines in double-stranded DNA).

Q4. The number of Barr bodies in a person with Klinefelter syndrome (47, XXY) is:

Show Answer

Answer: 1. Barr bodies = number of X chromosomes − 1. XXY has 2 X chromosomes → 1 Barr body.

Q5. Which of the following is NOT a feature of the genetic code?

(A) Degenerate   (B) Non-overlapping   (C) Ambiguous   (D) Universal

Show Answer

Answer: (C) Ambiguous. The genetic code is NON-ambiguous (each codon codes for only one specific amino acid). It IS degenerate (multiple codons per AA), non-overlapping, and universal (with minor exceptions in mitochondria).

Q6. Meselson and Stahl's experiment proved which model of DNA replication?

Show Answer

Answer: Semi-conservative replication. They used ¹⁵N and ¹⁴N isotopes. After one generation, all DNA was hybrid density. After two generations: 50% hybrid + 50% light. This pattern is only consistent with semi-conservative replication (each new DNA molecule has one old + one new strand).

Q7. Sickle cell anaemia is caused by a point mutation where GAG→GTG in the β-globin gene. This type of mutation is:

Show Answer

Answer: Missense mutation (transition). GAG (Glu) → GTG (Val) — one amino acid changed to another → missense. The change A→T at 2nd position of codon 6 is a transversion (actually). But it results in a missense mutation because the amino acid changes from glutamic acid to valine. The HbS (sickle hemoglobin) polymerizes under low O2.

Q8. A man with blood group A (IAi) marries a woman with blood group B (IBi). What blood groups can their children have?

Show Answer

Answer: A, B, AB, and O. Cross IAi × IBi → IAIᴮ (AB), IAi (A), IᴮI (B), ii (O) in 1:1:1:1 ratio → all four blood groups are possible.

Q9. Which enzyme seals the nicks and joins Okazaki fragments during DNA replication?

Show Answer

Answer: DNA Ligase. After DNA Pol I removes RNA primers and fills gaps, DNA Ligase seals the remaining nick by forming a phosphodiester bond between adjacent fragments.

Q10. The 9:3:4 ratio in F2 generation of a dihybrid cross indicates which type of epistasis?

Show Answer

Answer: Recessive epistasis. When the aa genotype (homozygous recessive at one locus) suppresses the expression of the other locus, the ratio is modified from 9:3:3:1 to 9:3:4 (the two recessive phenotype classes merge). Example: mouse coat color where cc = albino regardless of A or a locus.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

Q: What is the difference between incomplete dominance and codominance?

In incomplete dominance, neither allele is fully dominant and the heterozygote shows an intermediate phenotype (blending). Example: Rr in 4 o'clock plant → pink (between red RR and white rr). In codominance, both alleles are equally and simultaneously expressed in the heterozygote — there is NO blending. Example: IAIB blood group shows both A and B antigens on the same RBC. The key distinction: incomplete dominance = blend/intermediate; codominance = both phenotypes expressed fully.

Q: Why do cells need RNA primers for DNA replication but not for RNA synthesis?

DNA polymerases can only extend an existing nucleotide chain; they cannot start a new strand de novo (from nothing). They need a free 3'-OH group to add nucleotides to. RNA primase, however, can start a new RNA chain from scratch. Once the RNA primer provides a 3'-OH end, DNA polymerase can take over and extend. After replication, DNA Pol I removes the RNA primer and fills the gap with DNA, and ligase seals the nick. RNA polymerase (for transcription) works differently — it can initiate new RNA chains without a primer.

Q: What is the difference between a gene mutation and a chromosomal mutation?

Gene mutation (point mutation): change at the level of a single gene — one or a few nucleotide bases are changed, inserted, or deleted. Usually not visible on a karyotype. Examples: sickle cell disease, cystic fibrosis. Chromosomal mutation (structural aberration): a larger change involving a visible segment of a chromosome — deletion, duplication, inversion, or translocation. Visible on a karyotype. Examples: Cri du chat (del 5p), Philadelphia chromosome (t(9;22)). Chromosomal mutations affect multiple genes at once.

Q: Why are males more frequently affected by X-linked recessive conditions?

Males have only one X chromosome (XY). If that single X carries the recessive allele, the male is affected — there is no second X chromosome to provide a functional allele. Females (XX) would need the recessive allele on both X chromosomes to be affected, which is much less likely. Females with one recessive allele are carriers but phenotypically normal (the dominant allele on the other X provides enough functional protein). This is called the hemizygous condition in males.

Q: What is the difference between transcription template strand and coding strand?

DNA has two strands. The template strand (also called antisense strand, non-coding strand, or (−) strand) is the strand that RNA polymerase reads in the 3'→5' direction to synthesize mRNA. The coding strand (sense strand, non-template strand, or (+) strand) has the same sequence as the mRNA (with T instead of U). The mRNA sequence matches the coding strand. This is confusing because the "coding strand" is NOT the one being read — the template strand IS being read, and the mRNA is made complementary to the template = identical to the coding strand.
