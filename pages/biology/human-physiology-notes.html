1.3 Absorption Mechanisms

  • Glucose & amino acids: Active transport via Na⁺-dependent co-transporters (Na⁺-glucose symporter, SGLT1) → enter blood capillaries → portal vein → liver
  • Fatty acids + 2-monoglycerides: Absorbed into enterocyte → re-esterified to triglycerides → packaged into chylomicrons (with phospholipids, cholesterol, apolipoprotein B48) → enter lacteals (lymph capillaries) → thoracic duct → subclavian vein (bypass liver initially)
  • Water: Osmosis (follows absorbed solutes)
  • Na⁺: Active transport; drives glucose co-transport
  • Ca²⁺: Active transport enhanced by Vitamin D (calcitriol)
  • Iron: Absorbed as Fe²⁺ (ferrous; enhanced by Vitamin C); Fe³⁺ reduced to Fe²⁺ by stomach acid
  • Vitamin B12: Requires Intrinsic Factor (glycoprotein from stomach parietal cells); B12–IF complex absorbed in ileum. Absence of IF → pernicious anaemia
  • Fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K): Absorbed with dietary fat (require bile); enter lacteals with chylomicrons
  • Water-soluble vitamins (B complex, C): Absorbed by simple diffusion or facilitated transport; enter blood capillaries

Rh System: Rh antigen (D antigen; RhD) present → Rh positive (Rh+); absent → Rh negative (Rh−).

Erythroblastosis Fetalis (Hemolytic Disease of Newborn):

  • Rh− mother + Rh+ father → Rh+ fetus
  • During delivery, fetal RBCs enter mother's blood → mother produces anti-Rh antibodies (sensitization)
  • Second Rh+ pregnancy: Maternal anti-Rh IgG crosses placenta → attacks fetal RBCs → hemolysis → anemia, jaundice, hydrops fetalis
  • Prevention: Anti-D (anti-Rh) immunoglobulin (RhoGAM) given to Rh− mother within 72h of first delivery → destroys fetal RBCs before sensitization occurs

2.3 Cardiac Cycle

One complete heartbeat = 0.8 seconds (72 beats/min at rest)

PhaseDurationEvents
Atrial systole (atrial contraction)0.1 sBoth atria contract → blood pushed into ventricles; AV valves open; semilunar valves closed
Ventricular systole0.3 sBoth ventricles contract; AV valves close (1st heart sound "Lub"); semilunar valves open; blood ejected into aorta and pulmonary artery
Joint diastole (complete relaxation)0.4 sAll chambers relax; semilunar valves close (2nd heart sound "Dub"); AV valves open; chambers fill passively; 70% filling passive, 30% from atrial systole

Cardiac Output (CO) = Heart Rate (HR) × Stroke Volume (SV)

CO = 72 beats/min × 70 mL/beat = 5040 mL/min ≈ 5 L/min (at rest)

End-Diastolic Volume (EDV): ~120–130 mL; End-Systolic Volume (ESV): ~50–60 mL; Ejection Fraction: SV/EDV = 70/120 ≈ 58%

2.4 Heart Sounds

  • S1 ("Lub"): Closure of AV (mitral and tricuspid) valves at beginning of ventricular systole; low-pitched, longer
  • S2 ("Dub"): Closure of semilunar (aortic and pulmonary) valves at end of ventricular systole; high-pitched, shorter
  • Murmurs: abnormal sounds due to turbulent blood flow through leaky or stenotic valves

2.6 Double Circulation

Pulmonary Circulation: Right ventricle → pulmonary artery → lungs (O2 absorbed, CO2 released) → pulmonary vein → left atrium. Carries deoxygenated blood to lungs and returns oxygenated blood.

Systemic Circulation: Left ventricle → aorta → body tissues → venae cavae → right atrium. Carries oxygenated blood to tissues; returns deoxygenated blood.

Portal Circulation: Intestines → hepatic portal vein → liver (processes absorbed nutrients, detoxifies) → hepatic veins → inferior vena cava. A portal system has two capillary beds in series.

Coronary Circulation: Aorta → coronary arteries → heart muscle (myocardium) → coronary sinus → right atrium. Blockage → MI (heart attack).

3. Respiratory System

3.1 Pulmonary Ventilation (Breathing)

Inspiration (active process):

  1. Diaphragm contracts and flattens (moves down) + external intercostal muscles contract (ribs move up and out)
  2. Thoracic volume increases → lung volume increases → intrapulmonary pressure drops below atmospheric (−2 to −3 mmHg)
  3. Air flows in (high pressure outside → low pressure inside)

Expiration (passive at rest):

  1. Diaphragm and external intercostals relax → elastic recoil of lungs
  2. Thoracic volume decreases → lung volume decreases → pressure rises above atmospheric (+2 to +3 mmHg)
  3. Air flows out

Forced expiration uses internal intercostals and abdominal muscles (active).

Dead Space: ~150 mL of air per breath is in conducting airways (trachea, bronchi, bronchioles) and does NOT participate in gas exchange. Effective alveolar ventilation = (TV − Dead space) × RR = (500 − 150) × 12 = 4200 mL/min.

3.3 Gas Exchange

Driving force: Partial pressure gradients (diffusion from high pO2 to low pO2)

At the Alveoli (External Respiration):

  • pO2 of alveolar air = 104 mmHg; pO2 of deoxygenated blood arriving = 40 mmHg → O2 diffuses from alveoli to blood
  • pCO2 of blood arriving = 45 mmHg; pCO2 of alveolar air = 40 mmHg → CO2 diffuses from blood to alveoli

At the Tissues (Internal Respiration):

  • pO2 of arterial blood = 95 mmHg; pO2 of tissues = 40 mmHg → O2 diffuses from blood to tissues
  • pCO2 of tissues = 45–50 mmHg; pCO2 of arterial blood = 40 mmHg → CO2 diffuses from tissues to blood

3.4 Gas Transport in Blood

Oxygen Transport:

  • 97% bound to hemoglobin as oxyhemoglobin (HbO2) — one Hb carries 4 O2 molecules (1 per heme group)
  • 3% dissolved in plasma
  • Oxygen-hemoglobin dissociation curve is sigmoidal (cooperative binding: binding of one O2 increases affinity for next)
  • Bohr effect: Low pH (high CO2, high H⁺) → curve shifts RIGHT → lower O2 affinity → more O2 released to tissues
  • At lungs: High pO2, low pCO2, higher pH → O2 binds Hb readily
  • At tissues: Low pO2, high pCO2, lower pH → O2 released from Hb to tissues
  • 2,3-BPG (2,3-bisphosphoglycerate) also causes right shift (more O2 unloaded at tissues)

CO2 Transport:

  • 70% as bicarbonate ion (HCO3⁻) — CO2 + H2O → H2CO3 → H⁺ + HCO3⁻ (reaction in RBC, catalyzed by carbonic anhydrase); HCO3⁻ exits RBC (Cl⁻ enters: chloride shift)
  • 23% as carbaminohaemoglobin (HbCO2) — CO2 binds directly to NH2 groups of Hb (not to heme iron)
  • 7% dissolved in plasma

4. Nervous System

4.1 Neuron Structure

  • Cell body (soma): Contains nucleus, Nissl granules (rough ER for protein synthesis), mitochondria; no centrioles in mature neurons (cannot divide)
  • Dendrites: Short, highly branched processes; receive signals from other neurons; increase surface area for synaptic input
  • Axon: Single long process; conducts impulses away from cell body; axon hillock = site of AP generation
  • Myelin sheath: Formed by Schwann cells (PNS) or oligodendrocytes (CNS); white, lipid-rich insulation; increases conduction velocity dramatically
  • Nodes of Ranvier: Gaps in myelin where membrane is exposed; site of action potential regeneration in myelinated fibers (saltatory conduction)
  • Conduction velocity: Myelinated fiber (A-fibers) up to 120 m/s; unmyelinated C-fibers 0.5–2 m/s

4.2 Resting Membrane Potential (−70 mV)

  • Inside of neuron is negative relative to outside
  • Ion distribution: More K⁺ inside; more Na⁺ and Cl⁻ outside; more negatively charged proteins inside
  • Na⁺/K⁺ ATPase pump: Actively transports 3 Na⁺ out and 2 K⁺ in per cycle (electrogenic: net positive charge out → maintains negativity inside)
  • At rest: membrane far more permeable to K⁺ than Na⁺ (K⁺ leaks out along concentration gradient → leaves extra negative charge inside)

4.3 Action Potential Steps

Resting state−70 mVK⁺ leaks out; Na⁺/K⁺ pump maintains balance

Absolute Refractory Period: During Na⁺ channel inactivation; no AP possible regardless of stimulus strength.

Relative Refractory Period: During hyperpolarization; AP possible only with stronger-than-normal stimulus.

All-or-Nothing Law: Once threshold is reached, AP fires fully. Magnitude of AP is constant (does not vary with stimulus strength). Intensity coded by frequency of APs.

4.4 Synaptic Transmission

  1. AP arrives at presynaptic terminal (bouton)
  2. Depolarization → voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels open → Ca²⁺ enters presynaptic terminal
  3. Ca²⁺ triggers fusion of synaptic vesicles with presynaptic membrane (exocytosis)
  4. Neurotransmitter (NT) released into synaptic cleft (~20–40 nm wide)
  5. NT diffuses across cleft → binds to postsynaptic receptors → opens ion channels
  6. EPSP: Excitatory postsynaptic potential (Na⁺ in → depolarization). NT: glutamate, ACh (NMJ)
  7. IPSP: Inhibitory postsynaptic potential (Cl⁻ in or K⁺ out → hyperpolarization). NT: GABA, glycine
  8. NT removal: reuptake into presynaptic terminal (serotonin, dopamine, norepinephrine); enzymatic degradation (ACh → choline + acetate by acetylcholinesterase); diffusion

4.6 Spinal Cord and Reflex Arc

  • Grey matter (H-shaped): Neuron cell bodies; dorsal horn (sensory processing); ventral horn (motor neurons)
  • White matter: Myelinated axon tracts; ascending (sensory, toward brain) and descending (motor, toward muscles)

Reflex Arc (5 components):

Stimulus → Receptor → Sensory (afferent) neuron → Integration center (spinal cord synapse) → Motor (efferent) neuron → Effector (muscle/gland)

Simple (Monosynaptic) Reflex: Knee-jerk (patellar tendon stretch reflex) — only 2 neurons, one synapse; no interneuron; same segment

Polysynaptic Reflex: Withdrawal reflex — interneurons involved; can involve multiple spinal segments; allows crossed-extensor reflex

6. Excretory System

6.1 Nephron Types

Cortical nephron (85%): Short loop of Henle; mostly in cortex; primarily responsible for reabsorption; less important for urine concentration.

Juxtamedullary nephron (15%): Very long loop of Henle extending deep into medulla; essential for producing concentrated urine via countercurrent multiplier mechanism; peritubular capillaries = vasa recta (long, loop-shaped).

6.3 Hormonal Regulation of Kidney Function

  • ADH (Antidiuretic Hormone / Vasopressin): Released from posterior pituitary when blood is too concentrated (high osmolality) or blood volume low. Inserts aquaporin-2 channels into collecting duct → water reabsorbed → concentrated urine produced. Deficiency → Diabetes Insipidus (produces up to 20 L/day of dilute urine).
  • Aldosterone: Released from adrenal cortex (zona glomerulosa) in response to high K⁺, low blood volume, or angiotensin II. Stimulates Na⁺/K⁺ ATPase in DCT/CD principal cells → more Na⁺ reabsorbed, more K⁺ excreted → water follows Na⁺ → ↑blood volume, ↑BP.
  • RAAS (Renin-Angiotensin-Aldosterone System): Low BP → kidney releases Renin → converts angiotensinogen → Angiotensin I → ACE (in lungs) → Angiotensin II → stimulates aldosterone + ADH + vasoconstriction → ↑BP.
  • ANP (Atrial Natriuretic Peptide): Released when atria are stretched (high blood volume/pressure). Inhibits Na⁺ reabsorption → more Na⁺ and water excreted → ↓blood volume → ↓BP. Opposes aldosterone and ADH.
  • PTH: Increases Ca²⁺ reabsorption in DCT; decreases phosphate reabsorption in PCT → lowers plasma phosphate; activates Vitamin D in kidney.

Ovulatory phase Day 13–14 LH surge (positive feedback from high estrogen) → Graafian follicle ruptures → oocyte released (ovulation) Endometrium thickens; cervical mucus thins (sperm-friendly) LH surge (peak); Estrogen ↑ (2nd peak); FSH brief surge
Luteal (Secretory) phase 15–28 Ruptured follicle → corpus luteum (produces progesterone + estrogen) Endometrium prepares for implantation: glands develop, blood supply increases; secretory phase Progesterone ↑↑ (dominant); Estrogen moderate ↑

If fertilization occurs: Embryo implants; hCG (human chorionic gonadotropin) produced by trophoblast → maintains corpus luteum → corpus luteum continues producing progesterone → no menstruation → period missed (basis of pregnancy tests).

If no fertilization: Corpus luteum degenerates after ~14 days → progesterone and estrogen fall → endometrium sheds → menstruation begins → cycle restarts.

NEET Important Points Summary

  • Enterokinase activates trypsinogen → trypsin; trypsin then activates other pro-enzymes (chymotrypsinogen, procarboxypeptidase)
  • Bile has NO digestive enzymes; it emulsifies fats (increases surface area for lipase)
  • Digestion of fat starts in mouth (lingual lipase) but mainly occurs in duodenum (bile + pancreatic lipase)
  • Digestion of starch starts in mouth (salivary amylase) but mainly in small intestine (pancreatic amylase)
  • Protein digestion starts in stomach (pepsin) and is completed in small intestine
  • Cardiac output = 5 L/min at rest; can increase to 25 L/min during exercise
  • SA node (sinoatrial node) = pacemaker of heart (60–100 impulses/min); in right atrium
  • AV node delays impulse 0.1s → allows complete atrial contraction before ventricles contract
  • Bundle of His + Purkinje fibers: Rapid conduction to ventricles → synchronized ventricular contraction
  • Tidal volume = 500 mL; vital capacity = 4600 mL; functional residual capacity = 2200 mL
  • Oxygenated blood in pulmonary VEIN (not artery); deoxygenated blood in pulmonary ARTERY (opposite to naming convention for systemic)
  • Resting Membrane Potential = −70 mV; threshold = −55 mV; peak of action potential = +40 mV
  • 70S ribosome in prokaryotes (and mitochondria); 80S in eukaryotic cytoplasm
  • GFR = 125 mL/min = 180 L/day; urine output = 1–2 L/day; 99% of filtrate reabsorbed
  • Insulin lowers blood glucose; Glucagon raises blood glucose; Cortisol also raises blood glucose (gluconeogenesis)

Practice MCQs (15 Questions)

Q1. Which enzyme converts trypsinogen to trypsin?

Show Answer

Enterokinase (Enteropeptidase), secreted by the mucosa of the duodenum. Trypsin then autocatalytically activates more trypsinogen and also activates other proenzymes (chymotrypsinogen, proelastase, procarboxypeptidase).

Q2. Bile is secreted by _____ and stored in _____ and emulsifies _____ in the _____.

Show Answer

Liver; gallbladder; fats/lipids; duodenum. Bile contains bile salts (detergents) that break large fat droplets into smaller ones (emulsification) → increases surface area for pancreatic lipase action. Note: Bile has NO digestive enzymes.

Q3. The normal duration of ventricular systole in the cardiac cycle is:

(A) 0.1 s   (B) 0.4 s   (C) 0.3 s   (D) 0.8 s

Show Answer

Answer: (C) 0.3 s. Cardiac cycle at 72 bpm = 0.8 s total. Atrial systole = 0.1 s. Ventricular systole = 0.3 s. Joint diastole = 0.4 s.

Q4. Which wave on ECG represents ventricular depolarization?

Show Answer

QRS complex. P wave = atrial depolarization. QRS = ventricular depolarization. T wave = ventricular repolarization. Atrial repolarization is hidden within the QRS complex.

Q5. The threshold potential for a neuron is approximately:

(A) −90 mV   (B) −70 mV   (C) −55 mV   (D) +40 mV

Show Answer

Answer: (C) −55 mV. Resting potential = −70 mV. Threshold = −55 mV. Peak of action potential = +40 mV. Hyperpolarization = −80 mV.

Q6. The Bohr effect states that hemoglobin releases more O2 when:

Show Answer

When pH decreases (blood becomes more acidic) and CO2 concentration increases. This occurs at actively metabolizing tissues which produce CO2 and lactic acid. The O2-dissociation curve shifts to the right → Hb has lower affinity for O2 → more O2 delivered to tissues. This is physiologically ideal: tissues that need O2 most get it most.

Q7. The part of the brain responsible for balance and coordination is:

Show Answer

Cerebellum. It receives input from proprioceptors, vestibular system, and cerebral cortex. It coordinates fine motor movements, maintains balance and posture, and is involved in motor learning. Damage to cerebellum → ataxia (uncoordinated movements), intention tremor, gait abnormalities.

Q8. Which hormone is responsible for the reabsorption of Na⁺ in the distal convoluted tubule?

Show Answer

Aldosterone (from adrenal cortex, zona glomerulosa). It increases synthesis of Na⁺/K⁺ ATPase pumps and Na⁺ channels in principal cells of DCT and collecting duct → more Na⁺ reabsorbed, more K⁺ excreted → water follows Na⁺ → increased blood volume and blood pressure.

Q9. Glomerular Filtration Rate (GFR) in a healthy human adult is approximately:

(A) 12.5 mL/min   (B) 125 mL/min   (C) 1.25 L/min   (D) 1800 mL/min

Show Answer

Answer: (B) 125 mL/min = 180 L/day. Of this 180 L of filtrate, 178–179 L is reabsorbed → 1–2 L excreted as urine. GFR is measured by creatinine clearance or inulin clearance (inulin is filtered but neither secreted nor reabsorbed → ideal GFR marker).

Q10. Diabetes insipidus is caused by deficiency of which hormone?

Show Answer

ADH (Antidiuretic Hormone / Vasopressin), produced in hypothalamus and released from posterior pituitary. Deficiency → collecting duct cannot reabsorb water → large volumes (10–20 L/day) of very dilute urine. Not related to insulin or blood sugar (unlike Diabetes Mellitus).

Q11. The segment of nephron impermeable to water but actively transporting NaCl is:

Show Answer

Thick ascending limb of Loop of Henle. This is water-impermeable but actively transports NaCl (NKCC2 cotransporter) → generates the osmotic gradient in the medulla (countercurrent multiplier) needed for concentrated urine production. This is also the site of action of loop diuretics (furosemide) which block NKCC2.

Q12. LH surge occurs on approximately which day of the menstrual cycle and causes what event?

Show Answer

Day 13–14; causes ovulation. Rising estrogen from the Graafian follicle eventually triggers a positive feedback loop on the anterior pituitary → massive LH surge → Graafian follicle ruptures → secondary oocyte released → ovulation. After ovulation, the ruptured follicle becomes corpus luteum (LH maintains it).

Q13. Which part of the nervous system controls the fight-or-flight response?

Show Answer

Sympathetic nervous system (part of ANS). Activated by adrenal medulla (epinephrine/norepinephrine release). Effects: ↑HR, ↑BP, bronchodilation, pupil dilation, glycogenolysis (↑blood glucose), ↓digestive activity, piloerection, sweating. Parasympathetic system opposes it ("rest and digest").

Q14. The enzyme carbonic anhydrase catalyzes which reaction in RBCs?

Show Answer

CO2 + H2O → H2CO3 → H⁺ + HCO3⁻ (and the reverse in lungs). This reaction allows CO2 from tissues to be transported as bicarbonate (70% of CO2). In lungs, the reaction reverses: HCO3⁻ → CO2 (regenerated) → exhaled. Inhibitors: acetazolamide (used as altitude sickness medication, diuretic).

Q15. Cortisol is secreted by which part of the adrenal gland and what triggers its release?

Show Answer

Zona fasciculata of the adrenal cortex. Release triggered by ACTH from anterior pituitary (which is stimulated by CRH from hypothalamus). Feedback: high cortisol → inhibits both CRH and ACTH (negative feedback). Major functions: raises blood glucose (gluconeogenesis, protein catabolism), anti-inflammatory, immunosuppressive; essential for stress response. Excess → Cushing's syndrome; deficiency → Addison's disease.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

Q: What is the difference between digestion of fats in mouth, stomach, and small intestine?

Mouth: Lingual lipase (serous glands of tongue) begins fat digestion; active in slightly acidic conditions; mainly short-chain triglycerides. Stomach: Gastric lipase (from chief cells) continues with short-chain fats; acid pH. Small intestine (main site): Bile salts emulsify fats (increase surface area) → pancreatic lipase converts triglycerides to 2-monoglycerides + free fatty acids → absorbed into enterocytes → re-esterified to TG → packaged as chylomicrons → enter lacteals. The majority (90%+) of fat digestion and absorption occurs in the small intestine.

Q: Why does blood pressure need to be maintained, and what are the main mechanisms?

BP must be maintained to ensure adequate perfusion of all organs (brain, kidneys, heart). Main mechanisms: (1) Baroreceptor reflex (short-term, seconds): Baroreceptors in carotid sinus and aortic arch → detect ↓BP → sympathetic activation → ↑HR, ↑vasoconstriction → ↑BP. (2) RAAS (medium-term, minutes-hours): Low BP → renin → angiotensin II → aldosterone → Na⁺/water retention → ↑blood volume → ↑BP. (3) ADH: High osmolality or low BP → water reabsorption → ↑blood volume. (4) ANP (opposes): High BP → Na⁺ excretion → ↓BP.

Q: How is the action potential propagated along a myelinated nerve fiber?

In myelinated fibers, the myelin sheath prevents ion flow across the membrane. Current can only flow across the membrane at the Nodes of Ranvier (gaps in myelin). An action potential at one node creates local currents that depolarize the next node to threshold → AP "jumps" from node to node. This is called saltatory conduction (saltare = to jump in Latin). Advantages: (1) Much faster conduction (up to 120 m/s vs 0.5 m/s unmyelinated); (2) Energy efficient (ions exchanged only at nodes, not entire membrane → less Na⁺/K⁺ pump work needed).

Q: What is the difference between Type 1 and Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus?

Type 1 (Insulin-dependent DM): Autoimmune destruction of β cells of pancreatic islets → absolute insulin deficiency → hyperglycemia → polyuria (osmotic diuresis), polydipsia, polyphagia, weight loss, ketoacidosis (fatty acid metabolism due to lack of insulin). Treatment: insulin injections. Onset: usually childhood/adolescence. Type 2 (Insulin-resistant DM): Peripheral tissues (muscle, liver, adipose) become resistant to insulin; initially β cells compensate with more insulin → eventually β cell exhaustion. Associated with obesity, sedentary lifestyle. Onset: usually middle age or older. Treatment: lifestyle changes, oral medications (metformin), eventually insulin. Diabetes Insipidus is unrelated – it is ADH deficiency, not insulin-related.

Q: Why is the ascending limb of the Loop of Henle impermeable to water, and why does this matter?

The thick ascending limb actively transports NaCl out (via NKCC2 cotransporter) but is water-impermeable (lacks aquaporins). As NaCl leaves without water, the tubular fluid becomes hypoosmotic (dilute), while the surrounding medullary interstitium becomes hyperosmotic. This hyperosmotic medulla is the driving force for water reabsorption: when collecting duct permeability to water is increased by ADH, water moves out by osmosis into the hyperosmotic medullary interstitium → concentrated urine. Without the water-impermeable ascending limb and its active NaCl transport, no osmotic gradient would exist and concentrated urine could not be produced. This is the essence of the countercurrent multiplier system.
